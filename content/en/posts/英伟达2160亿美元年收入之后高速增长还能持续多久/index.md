NVIDIA's current share price is around $178, with a DCF valuation of approximately $190, implying about 6% undervaluation. The gap isn't large, but what's interesting is that the assumptions in the model are already quite conservative — the terminal EBIT margin is set at 50%, and the WACC at 12.5%. In other words, even after applying significant discounts to the future, the calculated price still comes in above the market price.

With that anchor in mind, let's examine three key questions.

## How Long Can This Growth Rate Hold?

In FY2026, NVIDIA generated $216 billion in revenue, up 65% year-over-year. What's even more surprising is the FY2027 Q1 guidance — $78 billion, representing 77% YoY growth, with the growth rate actually accelerating. Analyst consensus for full-year FY2027 is approximately $342 billion, implying about 58% growth. The DCF model uses this exact figure for Year 1 growth — 58%, essentially anchored to the sell-side consensus, which is hardly aggressive.

The real divergence lies in the years that follow. Management's vision is for global AI infrastructure investment to reach $3–4 trillion annually by 2030, with NVIDIA capturing 20–25% share, translating to $600 billion to $1 trillion in revenue. It's a grand vision, but the law of large numbers is at play — growing from $340 billion to $700 billion implies a five-year CAGR of just over 15%. The DCF assumes a 20% CAGR for Years 2–5, and considering the Vera Rubin platform ramping to mass production in the second half and the Chinese market reopening through case-by-case H200 approvals, 20% isn't outlandish — but it already embeds a "everything goes smoothly" premise.

Analyst estimates for FY2028 revenue are around $423 billion, roughly 23% growth over FY2027, and this year is most likely when the deceleration inflection point appears. Whether the deceleration curve from 58% to the low-20s to the mid-teens is smooth will determine a large chunk of the valuation.

## Can the 60% Margin Hold?

In FY2025 and FY2026, NVIDIA's adjusted EBIT margin held steady at 60–62%. That figure is staggering by semiconductor industry standards — driven by the lock-in effect of the CUDA ecosystem and near-monopolistic pricing power in AI chips.

However, the DCF model sets the terminal margin at just 50%, a full ten percentage points below current levels. This isn't an arbitrary haircut — it's pricing in several trends: AMD's MI series climbing from 9% to 15% market share; Google's TPUs, Amazon's Trainium, and other custom chips growing shipments by over 40% annually; and large customers gaining greater bargaining power as their procurement scales up.

That said, margin compression won't happen overnight. The CUDA ecosystem has locked in millions of developers worldwide, and switching costs are extremely high — this moat should hold for at least another three to five years. The model allows roughly eight years for convergence, letting margins gradually slide from 60% toward 50%, a fairly gentle pace. Even if the final landing zone is 48–52% rather than precisely 50%, the valuation impact would only be plus or minus a dozen dollars or so.

## What Assumptions Is the Current Share Price Paying For?

The sensitivity analysis yields an interesting set of indifference curves — the following assumption combinations can all justify a share price around $178:

- Long-term growth of 16–17% paired with margins around 55%
- Long-term growth of 20% paired with 46% margins
- Long-term growth of 18% paired with 51% margins

The market's current pricing roughly corresponds to "mid-to-high single-digit to low-double-digit long-term growth, paired with a moderately compressed margin." This set of implied assumptions is neither demanding nor optimistic — it essentially says: the AI investment boom will continue, but NVIDIA's excess profits will be gradually eroded by competition.

If you believe CUDA's moat is more durable than the market expects and that margin compression will be slower, then the current price offers a margin of safety. Conversely, if the pace of custom chip substitution exceeds expectations, or if the AI capex cycle peaks earlier than anticipated, even the 50% margin assumption could prove too generous.

## Where Are the Risks?

Two variables worth watching closely: first, the sustainability of AI capital expenditure — hyperscalers are currently spending tens of billions of dollars annually building GPU clusters, and if ROI falls short of expectations, spending could hit the brakes at any time; second, export control uncertainty — while the Chinese market has reopened a crack, policy winds could reverse at any moment.

The DCF's ~$190 valuation is built on a base case of "growth decelerating but not stalling, margins narrowing but not collapsing." With a relatively high 12.5% discount rate serving as a buffer, the current price offers a reasonable entry window for a company whose moat remains deep — provided your conviction in long-term AI demand hasn't wavered.
