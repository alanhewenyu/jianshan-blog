*Stories of Accounting Standards (IX)*

## The Lagging Impairment

In January 2022, A-share earnings pre-announcements were dominated by a single name—Evergrande.

On January 14, Wenke Landscape (now Wenke Co., Ltd.) pre-announced a loss of 1.3 to 1.8 billion yuan for 2021. The primary cause was impairment charges on notes receivable and accounts receivable totaling 1.879 billion yuan related to Evergrande—yet this company's cumulative net profit over the previous five years was only 1.04 billion yuan. On January 26, Jiayu Holdings pre-announced a loss of 1 to 1.4 billion yuan for 2021, eventually finalized at 1.347 billion yuan; its Evergrande-related commercial acceptance bill exposure alone was 1.316 billion yuan—a figure that by itself exceeded the company's cumulative net profit over the preceding fifteen years. On the evening of January 28, Grandland Group pre-announced a loss of 4 to 5 billion yuan for 2021, primarily due to full or near-full impairment charges on 3.25 billion yuan in Evergrande commercial bills.

The list goes on: Gold Mantis, Quanzhu Holdings, Jianghee Group, Piano, Holike, Oupai Home, and dozens more. According to Sina Finance, Evergrande's debt directly affected more than forty listed companies, with a combined market capitalization exceeding 370 billion yuan, and over 40% of them posted their first-ever annual loss since listing.

The numbers are striking enough, but what deserves deeper scrutiny is the timing of these concentrated impairment charges.

The first time Evergrande commercial bills became overdue was in November 2020. By July 2021, the discount rate on Evergrande commercial bills in the open market had soared above 30%—while similar bills from Sunshine City and R&F Properties were quoted at over 20% during the same period. This meant that anyone with even a passing familiarity with the bills market understood: the market no longer believed these bills would be honored in full and on time. On June 7, 2021, Evergrande publicly admitted for the first time that "a very small number of commercial bills at individual project companies had not been honored on time." In September, Evergrande Wealth imploded. On December 3, Evergrande formally declared it could no longer meet its debt obligations.

In other words, from Evergrande's first overdue bills in late 2020 to its official default, roughly a year elapsed. Yet the vast majority of A-share supply chain companies' large-scale impairment charges on Evergrande-related receivables occurred in Q4 2021 or even on the eve of annual report disclosure—not in the 2020 annual report, the 2021 interim report, or even the Q3 2021 report.

The most compelling evidence comes from Grandland Group itself. Its Q3 2021 report (disclosed in late October) showed a cumulative loss of just over 21 million yuan, while the full-year pre-announced loss was 4 to 5 billion yuan—meaning virtually all of those billions in losses were booked in Q4. Yet by the end of Q3 2021, Evergrande commercial bills had been overdue for nearly a year, Evergrande itself had publicly acknowledged the overdue bills in June, and Evergrande Wealth had already collapsed in September. Jiayu Holdings showed a similar pattern: its 2021 interim report showed a loss of 55.19 million yuan, while the full-year loss was 1.348 billion yuan, with the vast majority of losses concentrated in a single Q4 release.

In theory, the new financial instrument impairment rules that took effect starting in 2018 had long required companies to provision for losses based on forward-looking projections even before "bad debt indicators" appeared. One of the core changes was the shift from looking only at history and trigger events to predicting the future—accountants were no longer just recording what happened yesterday, but were now required to answer a far more uncertain question: over the next 12 months or the entire remaining life, what is the probability that this receivable won't be collected?

This shift in role is precisely the story this article tells. How a standard reform that the G20 placed great hopes on in 2009 pushed accountants from "recording history" to "predicting the future"—and how, in doing so, it exposed the inherent limitations of predicting the future itself.

## From the Incurred Loss Model to Expected Credit Loss

To understand what this revolution actually changed, we first need to understand what it overturned.

Under the old standards (IAS 39 in international accounting, and China's 2006 version of CAS 22), impairment of financial assets followed the "incurred loss model." Its logic was entirely consistent with the spirit of traditional accounting—a company could only recognize an impairment provision when objective evidence existed that a financial asset had already suffered impairment. "Objective evidence" included but was not limited to: the debtor experiencing significant financial difficulty, default or delinquency, or the likelihood of entering bankruptcy or liquidation.

The advantages of this method were obvious: it was conservative, verifiable, and hard to manipulate. A bank could not write down the carrying value of a loan simply because "we think this loan might not be repaid in the future"; it had to wait until signs of default appeared. Auditing was also straightforward—all that was needed was to verify whether triggering events had actually occurred.

The disadvantages were equally obvious. It locked accounting standards into a "rearview mirror only" position. Throughout the entire window from loan origination to default, no matter how much the market environment deteriorated or the debtor's creditworthiness declined, as long as no clear "objective evidence" had appeared, the carrying value remained untouched. Once trouble hit, losses were released all at once.

The "Expected Credit Loss model" (ECL) established by the new standards (IFRS 9 and China's 2017 version of CAS 22) fundamentally reversed this approach. It required companies to begin provisioning for potential future defaults from the very first day a financial asset was recognized, and to remeasure at each balance sheet date based on updated information.

At the operational level, for assets like bank loans that require ongoing credit risk monitoring, IFRS 9 designed the now-famous "three-stage model":

Stage 1 covers assets whose credit risk has not increased significantly since initial recognition, with provisions based on expected credit losses that may occur within the next 12 months. Stage 2 covers assets whose credit risk has increased significantly but where credit impairment has not yet occurred, with provisions based on expected credit losses over the entire remaining life. Stage 3 covers assets that have already experienced credit impairment, still provisioned over the entire remaining life, but with interest income now calculated on the "net carrying amount" rather than the "gross carrying amount"—an asset that has already been impaired can no longer accrue interest as if it were a healthy asset based on the original principal.

The moment a loan is originated, loss provisioning begins—this is the most fundamental difference from the old standards. It's not "wait for the loss to occur and then record it," but rather "probability of default (PD) times loss given default (LGD) times exposure at default (EAD)"—these three variables multiplied together give the credit asset impairment provision at the balance sheet date. All three variables must be estimated on a forward-looking basis—grounded in historical experience, layered with current conditions, and overlaid with reasonable forecasts of future macroeconomic conditions.

For trade receivables of general enterprises, IFRS 9 provides a "simplified approach"—no need to track three-stage migration; simply provision based on lifetime expected credit losses. China's CAS 22 application guidance further explains that companies may reference historical credit loss experience to prepare a mapping table of aging brackets (the original text uses "days past due") to fixed provision rates, and use this to calculate expected credit losses.

Sounds similar to the old "aging analysis method"? This is precisely a key confusion that recurs throughout. The two methods may look similar in form, but their essence is completely different. The aging analysis method looks only at history; the new method requires companies to adjust historical loss rates based on "forward-looking information."

That adjustment is the very step that pushed accountants from historians to fortune-tellers.

## Forced onto the Operating Table by a Financial Crisis

The ECL model was not born from the accounting profession's own self-improvement—it was a revolution forced into existence by a financial crisis.

During the 2008 financial crisis, Lehman Brothers collapsed, AIG nearly imploded, Royal Bank of Scotland teetered on the brink of nationalization, and the balance sheets of global banking were ripped open overnight. In the post-mortems, analysts and regulators pointed repeatedly to the same phenomenon—many loans that were ultimately proven to have been rotten long before had left virtually no trace on the financial statements on the eve of default: provision coverage ratios looked healthy, carrying values looked intact, until bad debts burst through like a dam breaking.

The finger naturally pointed at the "incurred loss" model under IAS 39. Its critics coined a label that has endured to this day—"too little, too late." It allowed banks to understate losses during boom times and forced them to make massive catch-up provisions when crisis struck, creating a procyclical effect: in good times, low provisions and loose lending; in bad times, surging provisions and credit contraction—precisely the feedback loop a financial system should never have.

In April 2009, the G20 London Summit issued a communiqué that has been cited repeatedly in the history of financial regulation. At that summit, leaders of the twenty nations formally called on accounting standard-setters and regulators to urgently cooperate on improving valuation and provisioning standards—a document that had been considered "accounting technical detail" was pushed to the front of the global financial system reform agenda.

What followed was a grueling five years. The IASB (International Accounting Standards Board) and FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board) initially attempted to jointly develop a unified expected loss model—after all, they had largely achieved convergence on multiple projects including revenue recognition and leases. But this time, convergence failed.

The two bodies could not agree on two fundamental issues. The first was "day 1 loss"—for a new loan originated at fair terms, does it conceptually make sense to require a loss provision on the very first day? The IASB had reservations—for a new contract priced at market rates, immediately writing down the carrying value defied basic economic intuition. FASB's position was more direct—since losses will eventually occur, recognize them on day one.

The second issue was the measurement time horizon. The IASB ultimately designed the "three-stage model" as a compromise: for assets that had not significantly deteriorated, only 12-month expected losses were provisioned; only upon deterioration would it be upgraded to lifetime losses. FASB chose a more direct approach—all financial assets, from day one, would be provisioned for lifetime expected credit losses. This became CECL (Current Expected Credit Loss), codified in ASC 326.

Behind these two trade-offs lie two often-overlooked details. First, FASB's more front-loaded approach was partly because the U.S. domestic banking industry's "originate-to-distribute" business model was more prevalent—before loans are packaged and sold off, the window for loss exposure is shorter, so regulators preferred to "put the full loss on the face from the start," ensuring that even if the asset was transferred, the books had already recognized the risk. Second, although CECL is more aggressive in dollar terms, it dispensed with the three-stage granularity, making it actually simpler to implement than IFRS 9—this was FASB's trade-off between theory and implementation cost.

Ultimately, IFRS 9 was officially published in 2014 and became effective globally on January 1, 2018; CECL was published in 2016 and began phased implementation for U.S. SEC-reporting companies starting in 2020. Both are called "expected credit loss" models, yet they differ significantly in day-1 provisioning, stage classification, interest income treatment, and other areas—making cross-standard comparisons nearly impossible. This stands as a landmark exception in the era of accounting convergence: five years after launching a joint project, the two bodies ultimately produced two incompatible versions.

China's story began in 2017. Between March and May that year, the Ministry of Finance issued a series of revised financial instrument accounting standards, of which the most relevant to this article is CAS 22—Recognition and Measurement of Financial Instruments. Its effective dates came in three tiers: enterprises listed both domestically and overseas, and those overseas enterprises adopting IFRS or CAS, from January 1, 2018; other domestically listed enterprises from January 1, 2019; and non-listed enterprises following Chinese Accounting Standards from January 1, 2021.

This meant that the vast majority of ordinary A-share investors first encountered the term "expected credit loss" in the notes to their companies' financial statements in the 2019 annual report—and by then, there was only one year left before Evergrande's commercial bills first became overdue.

## Who Predicts, Who Questions

Behind every standard reform stands a group of beneficiaries and a group of strong opponents. What makes ECL interesting is that the line between supporters and opponents was not drawn along the usual "regulators vs. companies" divide.

The most steadfast supporters of reform were central banks and financial stability institutions in various countries. From the Basel Committee to the European Central Bank, from the Bank of England to the Federal Reserve, regulators unanimously considered the incurred loss model one of the culprits that amplified the 2008 crisis. Forward-looking loss provisioning would build up sufficient "buffers" during boom times so that the system would not be caught off guard overnight during downturns—this was a victory for financial stability logic.

The investor community, represented by the CFA Institute, also supported the reform, for slightly different reasons: investors cared not about macroeconomic stability but about information content. They believed the incurred loss model made banks' financial statements appear overly smooth, masking the true trajectory of credit risk changes. Although ECL was subjective, it at least made management's judgment about the future explicit, allowing analysts to question and compare based on disclosures.

Opposition was concentrated in two camps, both from groups traditionally considered "conservative."

The first camp came from banks' risk management departments and CFOs. Their concern was that ECL "accounting-ized" a large volume of judgments that originally belonged to risk management, forcibly embedding them into the income statement. If a bank adjusted its default rate assumptions for the Chinese real estate sector in a given quarter, this would immediately manifest as a multi-billion-yuan fluctuation in impairment losses for that period—a fluctuation that didn't necessarily correspond to actual credit quality changes but more likely reflected a reinterpretation of macroeconomic scenarios. In other words, it amplified profit volatility and introduced a new variable: management's forecasting model itself.

The second camp came from auditors. Under the incurred loss model, auditors only needed to verify triggering events. Under ECL, auditors had to audit a complex model based on PD (probability of default), LGD (loss given default), EAD (exposure at default), and forward-looking macroeconomic scenarios. The model itself, the parameters, the weighting of macroeconomic scenarios—each was an exercise in "professional judgment." Auditors' workload surged, but the portion where they could deliver "right/wrong" conclusions shrank, replaced by fuzzy assessments within the gray zone of "whether it's reasonable."

European regulators' positions revealed yet another dimension. In 2017, the European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) published an important report acknowledging that ECL did improve financial stability while simultaneously warning: because the parameters are highly sensitive to macroeconomic conditions, the migration from Stage 1 to Stage 2 creates a "cliff effect"—a modest economic deterioration could cause a massive volume of loans to suddenly jump from 12-month expected loss provisioning to lifetime provisioning, triggering a nonlinear amplification of impairments. A standard designed to "combat procyclicality" could, under certain circumstances, create new procyclicality.

And the real test came far earlier than anyone had anticipated.

## The First Major Test Under COVID-19

In March 2020, the world entered its first wave of COVID lockdowns. Governments rolled out a series of relief measures including loan payment deferrals, interest reductions, and government guarantees. This was common-sense crisis response, but these measures immediately posed an almost unsolvable problem for the ECL model—

Should banks move a loan from Stage 1 to Stage 2 because a borrower accepted a payment deferral? A deferral was, by the letter of the standard, a sign of "significant increase in credit risk." But the deferral was government-organized and virtually all customers were pushed into accepting it—did it truly reflect an individual customer's credit deterioration?

If all deferred customers were mechanically moved to Stage 2 with lifetime expected losses provisioned, the banking industry would have collectively seen provisions explode and profits collapse in Q2 2020, followed by forced credit contraction—precisely the opposite of the government's relief policy intentions. If they weren't moved, that would clearly violate the standard's literal provisions regarding "significant increase in credit risk."

On March 27, 2020, the IASB issued a brief but enormously consequential application guidance, explicitly stating: IFRS 9 is not a set of mechanical rules; entities should exercise judgment; relief measures themselves should not be automatically interpreted as a "significant increase in credit risk"; and expected loss estimates should reflect the mitigating effects of government support and banking system support measures. The Basel Committee, the ECB, and the Bank of England simultaneously issued guidance with consistent messaging.

This was the first time the ECL model was hit head-on by a global crisis since going live, and the results had two layers. On one hand, global banks still saw significant increases in provisions in 2020—impairment losses at major European and American banks in Q2 were typically several times the same period the prior year. On the other hand, because the IASB and regulators consistently allowed "exercise of judgment," the actual increase in provisions was far smaller than what mechanical application of the standard's literal provisions would have produced. The standard's flexibility saved it—it was neither disproven nor abolished, nor did it trigger a secondary shock to the financial system.

But the cost was equally real. The macroeconomic scenario assumptions and scenario weightings used by different countries—and even different banks within the same country—were almost entirely unique, making cross-bank comparisons extraordinarily difficult. One UK bank's provisions increased 80% in 2020 while a German bank's increased only 40%—it was nearly impossible to determine whether their asset quality truly differed that much, or whether their pessimism settings were simply calibrated differently. ECL's greatest strength (forward-looking) and greatest weakness (incomparability) were both amplified in the COVID year.

China's situation was somewhat different. The major A-share listed banks had already been operating under the ECL framework for one to two years—the large banks with dual A+H listings began implementation in 2018, while other domestically listed banks started in 2019. When COVID broke out in early 2020, ECL was no longer new for A-share listed banks. That year, provision coverage ratios at A-share listed banks generally rose, driven primarily by regulatory requirements and banks' own proactive prudent provisioning. But more noteworthy was how the vast number of non-financial enterprises reacted when first encountering ECL.

## The A-Share Implementation Landscape

The biggest challenge facing general A-share companies (as distinct from licensed financial institutions like banks and securities firms) when first implementing the new financial instrument standards was the impairment of accounts receivable.

On the face of the standards, this is not particularly complicated. CAS 22 does not mandate that general enterprises adopt the three-stage model. For accounts receivable arising from CAS 14 (the revenue standard) that do not contain a significant financing component, companies must adopt the simplified approach and provision based on lifetime expected credit losses. The application guidance helpfully adds that companies may reference historical credit loss experience to prepare a mapping table of aging brackets with fixed provision rates.

This seemingly accommodating language actually buried an enormous practical trap.

Under the old standards, virtually all non-financial companies provisioned for bad debts using the "aging percentage method"—5% for receivables aged under one year, 10% for one to two years, and so on. This method was empirical and retrospective, with provisioning based entirely on historical default and recovery rates.

The "aging bracket to fixed provision rate mapping table" required by the new standards does indeed look very similar to the old aging table—so similar, in fact, that the vast majority of companies simply carried over their old aging tables when first implementing the new standards, without changing a single number. In 2019, the Fujian Province Institute of Certified Public Accountants specifically issued an audit advisory on this point, reminding auditors that a client "using an aging table" and "using an expected credit loss model" might produce identical results, but methodologically they are "alike in form, different in spirit." The former looks only at historical loss rates; the latter requires using historical loss rates as a starting point and then adjusting based on current conditions and reasonable, supportable forecasts of future economic conditions. In theory, loss rates under the new standards should be no lower than under the old standards—unless the company explicitly expects the future environment to improve significantly.

In practice, this became a classic case of "new wine in old bottles." On the surface, the notes used new vocabulary—"expected credit loss," "portfolios," "forward-looking adjustments." In substance, the vast majority of companies continued using the same aging table, the same percentages, and the same "estimated based on experience" approach from the old standards. New vocabulary did not bring new judgment—it merely gave old practices a new name.

Returning to the Evergrande supply chain issue from the opening. In theory, from the time the "three red lines" policy landed in the second half of 2020—let alone after Evergrande commercial bills first became overdue in November 2020—the credit environment in the real estate sector had already undergone changes at the "forward-looking information" level in the sense required by the standards. A construction and decoration company with Evergrande as a major customer and extremely high customer concentration should, under ECL logic, have long since applied significant forward-looking adjustments on top of historical loss rates. But in fact, these forward-looking adjustments appeared neither in the 2020 annual report, nor in the 2021 interim report, nor even in the Q3 2021 report. As mentioned earlier, Grandland Group had a cumulative loss of just over 21 million yuan at the end of Q3 2021 but ended the year with a loss of 4–5 billion yuan—all the losses were released in a single Q4 burst, even though its receivable exposure to Evergrande had existed for well over a year. This is the most typical characteristic of the incurred loss model: the books waited for a definitive "event" rather than acting on signals that had long been visible.

This was not the failure of any single company but a market-wide lack of consensus in the face of "forward-looking" requirements. When the biggest customer was still expanding, still acquiring land, still issuing bonds in the open market, it was difficult for the finance department of any individual company to conclude that "the industry is about to systematically deteriorate." When auditors had no peer benchmarks and regulators had not provided clear guidance, it was even harder to insist that a single company dramatically increase its impairment ratios. Standards can only provide principles and the boundaries of flexibility; they cannot substitute for each market participant making their own judgment. In an environment lacking consensus, this flexibility is most easily used to continue deferring rather than accelerating loss recognition—effectively running ECL as a de facto "incurred loss" model.

## How Investors Should Read Expected Credit Loss

Distilling the above into an actionable analytical framework, there are at least three angles.

**The first angle: treat ECL's subjectivity as information, not noise.** How a company's disclosed "expected credit loss rate on accounts receivable" changes across different years is more meaningful than the level of that rate itself. If a company whose core business is infrastructure construction maintains its receivable impairment rate at precisely the same figure for three consecutive years, you can be almost certain it is still mechanically applying the old aging percentage method without truly performing forward-looking adjustments. This is itself a signal—the company's finance team either does not take forward-looking information seriously or is actively choosing to ignore it.

**The second angle: focus on the "fit between impairment rates and industry cycles and customer structure."** A construction and decoration company whose accounts receivable are heavily concentrated in private real estate developers should theoretically apply a dramatically different expected credit loss rate than a peer whose receivables come mainly from large state-owned enterprises—the former bears much higher default risk exposure, and its loss rate should be significantly higher. In practice, however, the impairment rates of these two types are often set nearly identically, typically because the company's finance department applies a single uniform aging table across different customer types without segmenting by customer structure. This "portfolio mismatch" is a recurring issue flagged in CSRC regulatory reports and the most direct lever analysts can use to identify earnings management—if a company reflects neither customer structure differences in its portfolio classification nor industry risk changes in its loss rate assumptions, its accounts receivable balance is, relatively speaking, an unreliable asset.

**The third angle: shift your gaze from "the current period's impairment amount" to "how impairment assumptions change across periods."** For a bank, the most valuable thing to track is not a given year's provision coverage ratio per se, but how that bank's PD, LGD, and forward-looking scenario weightings have been adjusted across different years. If a bank maintains the same default probability assumptions during a year when the real estate industry has clearly weakened as it did during boom times, its reported stability comes at the price of a concentrated blow-up at some future point. Conversely, if a bank consistently raises default rate assumptions for the relevant industry under stress scenarios and proactively increases the share of Stage 2 loans, its reported results will be more volatile, but its exposure in the next storm will be smaller.

Expected credit loss is never an objectively existing number. It is an estimate jointly generated by management's judgment, model specifications, and macroeconomic scenario weightings—its "accuracy" can only be verified after the fact, but its "honesty" can be scrutinized at every balance sheet date. Whether a report whose notes state "adjusted based on forward-looking information" has truly made adjustments or merely swapped in new phrasing is always a question investors must answer for themselves.

## The Cost of Prediction

Accounting has not been in the business of "predicting the future" for very long. For the previous two or three centuries, it prized the principle of prudence—recording only transactions that have already occurred, compressing the room for judgment to a minimum, treating "subjectivity" as a source of professional risk. The expected credit loss model turned all of this on its head, requiring accountants at every balance sheet date to answer a set of weather-forecast-style questions: What is the probability this loan will default within the next 12 months? Over the entire remaining life? Under the most pessimistic macroeconomic scenario?

The benefits of this transformation are enormous. It did make the global financial system's response to the 2020 COVID shock somewhat more measured than in 2008. It did move general enterprises' receivable impairment away from purely mechanical aging-based provisioning. It did make management's judgments about the future explicit, disclosed, and open to scrutiny. In this sense, the reform goals championed in the G20 leaders' 2009 communiqué have been partially achieved.

But the cost is equally real. It shifted a massive volume of judgment from "facts" to "assumptions," and raised the difficulty of comparison from "understanding the financial statements" to "understanding the model." It gave companies more tools to look forward, but also gave management more room to adjust backward. A standard that simultaneously makes both truth and window-dressing easier is the inevitable trade-off under financial stability logic—regulators would rather have a flexible "provision a little early" than a rigid "wait until the event occurs."
