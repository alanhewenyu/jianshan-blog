*Stories of Accounting Standards (IX)*

## The Lagging Impairment

In January 2022, A-share earnings previews were dominated by one name over and over again — Evergrande.

On January 14, Wenke Landscape (now Wenke Co., Ltd.) forecast a 2021 loss of RMB 1.3 to 1.8 billion, primarily due to impairment provisions on RMB 1.879 billion in notes receivable and accounts receivable related to Evergrande — while the company's cumulative net profit over the previous five years was only RMB 1.04 billion. On January 26, Jiayu Co., Ltd. forecast a 2021 loss of RMB 1.0 to 1.4 billion, which ultimately settled at RMB 1.347 billion in the annual report; Evergrande-related commercial acceptance bills alone accounted for RMB 1.316 billion — an exposure that by itself exceeded the company's cumulative net profit over the prior fifteen years. On the evening of January 28, Grandland Group forecast a 2021 loss of RMB 4.0 to 5.0 billion, mainly due to full or near-full impairment provisions on RMB 3.25 billion in Evergrande commercial bills.

The longer list includes dozens of companies such as Gold Mantis, Quanzhu Co., Ltd., Jangho Group, Piano, Holike, and Oupai Home. According to Sina Finance, Evergrande's debt directly affected more than forty listed companies, with a combined market capitalization exceeding RMB 370 billion, of which over forty percent reported their first-ever annual loss.

The numbers alone are striking enough, but what deserves deeper examination is the timing of these concentrated provisions.

Evergrande's commercial bills first became overdue in November 2020. By July 2021, the discount rate on Evergrande commercial bills in the open market had already soared above 30% — with Sunshine City and R&F Properties also quoting above 20% during the same period. This meant that anyone with even basic knowledge of the bills market already understood: the market no longer believed these instruments would be redeemed in full and on time. On June 7, 2021, Evergrande publicly admitted for the first time that "a very small number of commercial bills from individual project companies had not been redeemed on time." In September, Evergrande Wealth collapsed. On December 3, Evergrande officially declared it could no longer fulfill its debt obligations.

In other words, from Evergrande's first overdue event in late 2020 to its formal default, roughly a year elapsed. Yet the vast majority of A-share supply chain companies booked their large-scale impairments on Evergrande-related receivables in Q4 2021 or even on the eve of annual report disclosure — not in the 2020 annual report, the 2021 interim report, or even the Q3 2021 report.

The most compelling evidence comes from Grandland Group itself. Its Q3 2021 report (disclosed at the end of October) showed cumulative losses of only around RMB 21 million, while the full-year pre-loss estimate was RMB 4.0 to 5.0 billion — meaning virtually all of those tens of billions in losses were booked in Q4 alone. By the end of Q3 2021, Evergrande commercial bills had been overdue for nearly a year, Evergrande itself had publicly acknowledged the overdue issue in June, and Evergrande Wealth had already collapsed in September. Jiayu Co., Ltd. followed a similar pattern: a first-half loss of RMB 55.19 million, a full-year loss of RMB 1.348 billion, with the vast majority released as a one-time charge in Q4.

In theory, the new financial instruments impairment rules, which had been phased in since 2018, had long required companies to provision for losses based on forward-looking estimates *before* any signs of bad debt appeared. One of the core changes was the shift from looking only at history and trigger events to predicting the future — accountants were no longer merely recording what happened yesterday, but were expected to answer a far more uncertain question: over the next twelve months or the entire remaining life of the asset, what is the probability that this receivable will not be collected?

This shift in role is exactly the story this article sets out to tell. How a standard reform that the G20 placed great hope in during 2009 pushed accounting from "recording history" to "predicting the future," and how, in the process, it exposed the inherent limitations of "predicting the future" itself.

## From the Incurred Loss Model to Expected Credit Loss

To understand what this revolution actually changed, we first need to understand what it overturned.

Under the old standards (IAS 39 in international accounting standards, and China's 2006 edition of CAS 22), impairment of financial assets followed the "incurred loss model." Its logic was perfectly consistent with the spirit of traditional accounting — a company could only recognize an impairment provision when there was objective evidence that a financial asset had already suffered impairment. "Objective evidence" included but was not limited to: significant financial difficulty of the debtor, default or delinquency, likely bankruptcy or liquidation, and so on.

The advantages of this approach were obvious: it was prudent, verifiable, and resistant to manipulation. A bank could not write down the carrying value of a loan simply because "I think this loan might not be repaid someday"; it had to wait for signs of default to appear. Auditing was also straightforward — auditors only needed to verify whether trigger events actually existed.

The disadvantages were equally obvious. It locked accounting standards into a "rearview mirror only" position. During the entire window from loan origination to default, no matter how much the market environment deteriorated or the debtor's creditworthiness declined, as long as no clear "objective evidence" had emerged, the carrying value remained untouched. When trouble finally struck, losses were released all at once.

The "Expected Credit Loss" (ECL) model established by the new standards (IFRS 9 and China's 2017 edition of CAS 22) works on a completely opposite principle. It requires companies to begin provisioning for potential future defaults from the very first day a financial asset is recognized, and to remeasure at each balance sheet date based on updated information.

At the operational level, for assets like bank loans that require ongoing credit risk monitoring, IFRS 9 designed the well-known "three-stage model":

Stage 1 covers assets whose credit risk has not significantly increased since initial recognition — provisions are based on expected credit losses that may occur within the next 12 months. Stage 2 covers assets whose credit risk has significantly increased but where credit impairment has not yet occurred — provisions are based on lifetime expected credit losses. Stage 3 covers assets that are already credit-impaired — provisions are still based on lifetime expected losses, but interest income must now be calculated on the "net carrying amount" rather than the "gross carrying amount" — an asset that already has an impairment provision against it can no longer accrue interest as if it were a healthy asset calculated on the original principal.

From the very day a loan is originated, loss provisioning begins — this is the most fundamental difference from the old standards. It is not "wait for a loss to occur, then book it," but rather "probability of default (PD) times loss given default (LGD) times exposure at default (EAD)" — the product of these three variables equals the credit impairment provision at each balance sheet date. All three variables must be estimated on a forward-looking basis — grounded in historical experience, layered with current conditions, and overlaid with reasonable forecasts of future macroeconomic conditions.

For trade receivables of general enterprises, IFRS 9 provides a "simplified approach" — there is no need to track three-stage migration; provisions are simply based on lifetime expected credit losses. China's CAS 22 application guidance further explains that companies may reference historical credit loss experience and compile a look-up table mapping aging buckets (the original text uses "days past due") to fixed provision rates, and calculate expected credit losses accordingly.

Sounds similar to the old "aging analysis method"? This is precisely a key confusion that will recur throughout this article. The form of the new and old methods may be similar, but their essence is entirely different. The aging analysis method looks only at history; the new method requires companies to adjust historical loss rates based on "forward-looking information."

That adjustment is the step that pushes accountants from historians into fortune-tellers.

## Pushed onto the Operating Table by a Financial Crisis

The ECL model was not born out of the accounting profession's self-improvement — it was a revolution forced by a financial crisis.

During the 2008 financial crisis, Lehman Brothers collapsed, AIG nearly imploded, Royal Bank of Scotland approached nationalization, and the balance sheets of the global banking industry were ripped open in an instant. In the post-mortems, analysts and regulators pointed repeatedly to the same phenomenon — many loans that were ultimately proven to have been rotten long ago showed almost no trace on the financial statements the night before default: provision coverage ratios looked healthy, carrying values looked intact, until bad debts burst through like a dam breaking.

The finger naturally pointed at the "incurred loss" model under IAS 39. Its critics coined a label that endures to this day — "too little, too late." It allowed banks to underestimate losses during boom times and forced them to make massive catch-up provisions when crises hit, creating a procyclical effect: low provisions and loose credit during good times; surging provisions and credit contraction during bad times — precisely the kind of feedback loop the financial system should never produce.

In April 2009, the G20 London Summit issued the communiqué that financial regulatory history would cite again and again. At that summit, the leaders of the twenty major economies formally called on accounting standard-setters and regulators to urgently cooperate on improving valuation and provisioning standards — a document that had been considered "technical accounting minutiae" was pushed to the forefront of the global financial reform agenda.

What followed was a long five years. The IASB (International Accounting Standards Board) and FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board) initially attempted to jointly develop a unified expected loss model — after all, they had essentially completed convergence on multiple projects including revenue recognition and leases. But this time, convergence did not succeed.

The two bodies could not agree on two fundamental questions. The first was "day 1 loss" — does it make conceptual sense to require a new loan, originated at fair terms, to carry an expected loss provision on the very first day? The IASB had reservations — writing down the carrying value of a new contract priced at market terms on day one went against basic economic intuition. FASB's position was more direct — since losses will happen eventually, recognize them on day one.

The second disagreement was over the measurement time horizon. The IASB ultimately designed the "three-stage model" as a compromise: for assets that have not noticeably deteriorated, only 12-month expected losses are provisioned; once they deteriorate, provisions escalate to lifetime. FASB chose a more direct approach — all financial assets, from day one, are provisioned based on lifetime expected credit losses. This became CECL (Current Expected Credit Loss), codified in ASC 326.

Behind these two different trade-offs lie two often-overlooked details. First, FASB's more front-loaded approach was partly because the "originate-to-distribute" business model was more prevalent among U.S. domestic banks — since loans were packaged and sold, the window for loss exposure was shorter, and regulators preferred to "put all the losses on the table upfront," so that even when assets were transferred, the books would already reflect the risk. Second, while CECL is more aggressive in dollar terms, it abandoned the granular three-stage layering, making it operationally simpler than IFRS 9 — a deliberate trade-off by FASB between theoretical purity and implementation cost.

Ultimately, IFRS 9 was officially issued in 2014 and became effective globally on January 1, 2018; CECL was issued in 2016 and phased in for SEC-reporting companies starting in 2020. Two models both called "expected credit loss" differ significantly on day 1 provisioning, stage classification, interest calculation, and more — making cross-standard comparison virtually impossible. This stands as a landmark exception in the era of accounting convergence: two major standard-setters launched a joint project, and five years later, landed on two versions that do not interoperate.

China's story began in 2017. Between March and May of that year, the Ministry of Finance issued revised financial instruments accounting standards, of which the most relevant to this article is CAS 22 — Recognition and Measurement of Financial Instruments. Implementation was phased in three tiers: companies listed both domestically and overseas, and overseas entities using IFRS or CAS, from January 1, 2018; other domestically listed companies from January 1, 2019; non-listed entities applying Chinese Accounting Standards from January 1, 2021.

This means that the vast majority of ordinary A-share investors first encountered the term "expected credit loss" in the notes to the annual reports of the companies they follow in the 2019 filings — and by that point, only one year remained before Evergrande's commercial bills first became overdue.

## Who Must Predict, Who Must Doubt

Behind any standard reform stand a group of supporters and a group of fierce opponents. What makes ECL interesting is that the dividing line between supporters and opponents does not run along the usually imagined boundary of "regulators vs. companies."

The most steadfast supporters of reform were central banks and financial stability institutions worldwide. From the Basel Committee to the European Central Bank, from the Bank of England to the Federal Reserve, regulators unanimously agreed that the incurred loss model was one of the culprits that amplified the 2008 crisis. Forward-looking loss provisioning would build up a sufficient "buffer" during boom times, preventing balance sheets from being blown apart overnight during downturns — this was a victory for financial stability logic.

The investor community, represented by the CFA Institute, also supported reform, though for slightly different reasons. Investors cared not about macroeconomic stability but about information content. They argued that the incurred loss model made bank financial statements appear excessively smooth, masking the true trajectory of credit risk changes; ECL, though subjective, at least made management's judgments about the future explicit, allowing analysts to challenge and compare based on disclosures.

Opposition concentrated in two camps, both of which came from groups traditionally considered "conservative."

The first camp consisted of banks' risk management departments and chief financial officers. Their concern was that ECL "accountified" a large volume of judgments that originally belonged to risk management, forcibly embedding them into the income statement. When a bank adjusted its default rate assumption for China's real estate sector in a given quarter, the change would immediately show up as hundreds of millions in impairment loss fluctuations for that period — yet such volatility did not necessarily correspond to real changes in credit quality; more likely, it was simply a reinterpretation of macroeconomic scenarios. In other words, it amplified profit volatility and introduced a new variable: management's predictive model itself.

The second camp consisted of auditors. Under the incurred loss model, auditors only needed to verify trigger events; under ECL, they had to audit a complex model built on PD, LGD, EAD, and forward-looking macroeconomic scenarios. The model itself, the parameters, the weighting of macro scenarios — every component involved "professional judgment." Auditor workload skyrocketed, but the portion where they could render a "right/wrong" conclusion shrank, replaced increasingly by fuzzy judgments within the gray zone of "whether it is reasonable."

European regulators' stance revealed yet another dimension. In 2017, the European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) published an important report acknowledging that ECL did improve financial stability, while warning that because parameters are highly sensitive to macroeconomic conditions, migration from Stage 1 to Stage 2 would create a "cliff effect" — a slight economic deterioration could cause large volumes of loans to jump from 12-month provisions to lifetime provisions, triggering nonlinear amplification of impairments. A standard designed to "counter procyclicality" could, in certain scenarios, create new procyclicality.

And the real test arrived earlier than anyone had expected.

## The First Major Test Under COVID-19

In March 2020, the world entered its first wave of COVID lockdowns. Governments rolled out loan payment deferrals, interest relief, government guarantees, and other support measures. These were commonsense crisis responses, but they immediately posed a nearly unsolvable puzzle for the ECL model —

Should banks move a loan from Stage 1 to Stage 2 just because the borrower accepted a payment deferral? Literally, a deferral is a sign of "significant increase in credit risk," but the deferral was organized by the government, and virtually all borrowers were pushed into accepting it — so did it truly reflect individual borrower credit deterioration?

If banks mechanically moved all deferred borrowers to Stage 2, provisioning lifetime expected losses, the banking sector would collectively experience provision explosions and profit collapses in Q2 2020, forcing credit contraction — exactly the opposite of the government's relief intentions. If they did not upgrade, they would clearly be violating the standard's literal requirements regarding "significant increase in credit risk."

On March 27, 2020, the IASB issued a brief but critically important application guidance, explicitly stating: IFRS 9 is not a set of mechanical rules, and entities should exercise judgment; relief measures should not automatically be interpreted as "significant increase in credit risk"; expected loss estimates should reflect the mitigating effects of government support and banking system support measures. The Basel Committee, the ECB, and the Bank of England simultaneously issued guidance in the same vein.

This was the first time the ECL model was hit head-on by a global crisis since going live, and the outcome operated on two levels. On one hand, global banks still saw significant increases in provisions in 2020 — impairment losses at major European and American banks in Q2 were typically several times the prior-year period. On the other hand, because the IASB and regulators uniformly permitted "exercise of judgment," actual provision increases were far smaller than what mechanical application of the standard's literal requirements might have produced. The standard's flexibility saved it — it was neither falsified, nor repealed, nor did it trigger a secondary shock to the financial system.

But the cost was also real. The macroeconomic scenario assumptions and scenario weightings used by different countries — and even different banks within the same country — were almost entirely proprietary, making cross-bank comparison extraordinarily difficult. If a British bank's provisions increased 80% in 2020 and a German bank's only 40%, it was very hard to tell whether the two banks' asset quality truly differed that much, or whether their degrees of pessimism had simply been calibrated differently. The ECL model's greatest strength (forward-looking nature) and greatest weakness (lack of comparability) were both amplified simultaneously during the COVID year.

China's situation was somewhat different. Major A-share listed banks had already been operating under the ECL framework for a year or two by then — A+H dual-listed large banks started in 2018, other domestically listed banks in 2019. When COVID broke out in early 2020, ECL was already old news for A-share listed banks. That year, overall provision coverage ratios at A-share listed banks rose, driven mainly by regulatory requirements and banks' own proactive prudent provisioning. But what deserves more attention is how large numbers of non-financial companies reacted when they faced ECL for the first time.

## The Practical Landscape of A-Share Implementation

The biggest challenge that general A-share enterprises (as distinct from licensed financial institutions like banks and securities firms) faced when first applying the new financial instruments standard was the impairment of accounts receivable.

On the face of the standard, this was not particularly complicated. CAS 22 does not mandate the three-stage model for general enterprises; for receivables arising under CAS 14 (Revenue) that do not contain a significant financing component, companies must use the simplified approach and provision based on lifetime expected credit losses. The application guidance even helpfully explains that companies may reference historical credit loss experience and compile a look-up table mapping aging buckets to fixed provision rates for the calculation.

This seemingly lenient instruction actually laid an enormous practical trap.

Under the old standards, virtually all non-financial companies provisioned bad debts using the "aging percentage method" — 5% for receivables aged under one year, 10% for one to two years, and so on. This method was empirical and backward-looking, with provisioning based entirely on historical default and recovery rates.

The "aging and fixed provision rate look-up table" required by the new standard does indeed look very similar in form to the old aging table — so similar that the vast majority of companies, when first applying the new standard, simply carried over the old aging table as-is, without changing a single number. In 2019, the Fujian Institute of Certified Public Accountants specifically issued an audit alert reminding auditors that while an auditee's "using an aging table" and "using an expected credit loss model" might produce identical results, methodologically they were "similar in form but different in substance." The former looks only at historical loss rates; the latter requires using historical loss rates as a starting point, then adjusting based on current conditions and reasonable, supportable forecasts of future economic conditions. In theory, loss rates under the new standard should be no lower than those under the old — unless the company explicitly expects the future environment to improve significantly.

In practice, this became a classic case of "new label, old wine." On the surface, the notes used new vocabulary — "expected credit loss," "portfolio," "forward-looking adjustment." In substance, the vast majority of companies continued using the same aging table, the same set of percentages, and the same "estimated based on experience" approach from the old standard. New vocabulary did not bring new judgment — it merely gave the old practice a new name.

Returning to the Evergrande supply chain issue from the opening. In theory, from the second half of 2020, when the "three red lines" policy landed — let alone after November 2020, when Evergrande commercial bills first became overdue — the credit environment in the real estate sector had already undergone changes at the level of "forward-looking information" as defined by the standard. A construction and decoration company with Evergrande as a major client and extremely high customer concentration should, under ECL logic, have long ago layered significant forward-looking adjustments on top of historical loss rates. But in reality, the relevant companies' forward-looking adjustments appeared neither in the 2020 annual report, the 2021 interim report, nor the Q3 2021 report. As mentioned earlier, Grandland Group had cumulative losses of only around RMB 21 million through Q3 2021, with a full-year pre-loss estimate of RMB 4.0 to 5.0 billion — all losses were released as a one-time charge in Q4, by which time its receivable exposure to Evergrande had already existed for over a year. This is precisely the hallmark behavior of the incurred loss model: the books wait for a definitive "event" rather than acting on signals that have long been visible.

This was not the failure of any single company, but rather the entire market's lack of consensus when confronting the concept of "forward-looking." When the largest client is still expanding, still acquiring land, still issuing bonds in the public market, it is very difficult for any individual company's finance department to independently conclude that "the industry is about to systematically deteriorate." When auditors have no peer benchmarks and regulators have not issued clear guidance, it is even harder to stand alone in demanding that a company significantly increase its impairment rates. Standards can only provide principles and flexible boundaries; they cannot substitute for market participants making their own judgments. And in an environment lacking consensus, that flexibility is most easily used to continue deferring rather than accelerating loss recognition — effectively running ECL as a de facto "incurred loss" model.

## How Investors Should Read Expected Credit Loss

Distilling the story above into an actionable analytical framework, there are at least three angles.

**The first angle is to treat ECL's subjectivity as information, not noise.** How a company's disclosed "expected credit loss rate on accounts receivable" changes from year to year is more meaningful than the level of the rate itself. If a company whose main business is infrastructure construction maintains its receivables impairment rate at precisely the same figure for three consecutive years without any change, it is almost certain that it is still mechanically applying the old aging percentage method without genuinely performing forward-looking adjustments. This is itself a signal — the company's finance team either does not take forward-looking information seriously, or has actively chosen to ignore it.

**The second angle is to focus on "the alignment between impairment rates and industry cycles and customer structure."** A construction and decoration company with receivables heavily concentrated in private real estate developers, versus a peer whose receivables come mainly from large state-owned enterprises, should theoretically apply markedly different expected credit loss rates — the former bears higher default risk exposure, and its loss rate should be significantly higher. In practice, however, the two often have nearly identical impairment rates, typically because the finance department uses a single, uniform aging table across all customer types without differentiating by customer structure. This kind of "portfolio mismatch" is an issue repeatedly flagged in CSRC supervisory reports, and the most direct tool analysts use to identify earnings management headroom — if a company reflects neither customer structure differences in its portfolio classification nor industry risk changes in its loss rate assumptions, then its accounts receivable balance is, relatively speaking, an unreliable asset.

**The third angle is to shift focus from "the current-period amount of impairment" to "cross-period changes in impairment assumptions."** For a bank, the most valuable thing to track is not the provision coverage ratio in any given year, but how the bank's PD, LGD, and forward-looking scenario weightings have been adjusted across different years. If a bank maintains the same default probability assumptions during a year when the real estate sector is clearly weakening as it did during boom times, its reported stability comes at the cost of a concentrated blowup at some future point. Conversely, if a bank continuously raises default rate assumptions for relevant industries under stress scenarios and proactively increases the share of Stage 2 loans, its reported earnings will be more volatile, but it will also suffer less damage in the next storm.

Expected credit loss is never an objectively existing number. It is an estimate jointly generated by management judgment, model design, and macroeconomic scenario weights — its "accuracy" can only be verified after the fact, but its "honesty" can be scrutinized at every balance sheet date. Whether a report that states "adjustments have been made based on forward-looking information" truly made those adjustments, or merely swapped in a new phrase, is a question investors must always answer for themselves.

## The Cost of Prediction

It has not been long since financial statements were pushed into the position of "predicting the future." For the previous two or three hundred years, accounting upheld the principle of prudence — recording only transactions that had already occurred, compressing the room for judgment to a minimum, and treating "subjectivity" as a source of professional risk. The expected credit loss model turned all of this on its head, requiring accountants at every balance sheet date to answer a set of weather-forecast-style questions: what is the probability of default on this loan within the next 12 months? Over the entire remaining life? Under the most pessimistic macroeconomic scenario?

The benefits of this transformation are substantial. It genuinely made the global financial system's response to the 2020 COVID shock somewhat more composed than in 2008. It genuinely moved trade receivable impairment at general enterprises away from purely mechanical aging-based provisioning. It genuinely made management's judgments about the future explicit, disclosed, and subject to scrutiny. In this sense, the reform objectives driven by the G20 leaders' 2009 communiqué have been partially achieved.

But the cost is equally real. It transferred vast amounts of judgment from "facts" to "assumptions," and elevated the difficulty of comparison from "understanding the statements" to "understanding the model." It gave companies more tools to look forward, and also gave management more room to adjust backward. A standard that simultaneously makes both truth and window-dressing easier is an inevitable trade-off under financial stability logic — regulators would rather have a flexible "provision a little earlier" than a rigid "wait until it happens."
