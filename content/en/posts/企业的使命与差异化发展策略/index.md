The fundamental mission of a business is to provide products or services to society. Whether a product or service is viable depends on whether there is genuine market demand for it.

We often say that businesses exist to make a profit. However, for an entrepreneur, if the primary motivation is simply to make money, it may paradoxically become harder to do so. Such a narrow focus limits one's vision and causes the entrepreneur to overlook what truly matters: how to better serve customer needs. In the long run, sustained success in business is always predicated on continuously meeting the needs of customers.

Therefore, making money should not be the purpose of building a business (especially a startup), but rather the outcome.

So does meeting customer needs guarantee business success? A look at the many startups that failed during their cash-burning phase reveals that, in many cases, the product or service was excellent, yet the company simply could not turn a profit. While we noted that making money should not be the purpose of a business, if it is not the outcome either, the venture is ultimately doomed to fail. After all, entrepreneurship is not philanthropy.

Therefore, the success of a product or service hinges on whether a sustainable and profitable business model can be found.

Products or services that address entirely new human needs represent groundbreaking innovation. Consider the iPhone introduced by Steve Jobs, or ChatGPT today -- the value of such innovations amounts to nothing less than a social revolution with far-reaching impact.

For most entrepreneurs, however, the typical path involves offering products and services built around existing demand. Since the market usually already has similar offerings, new entrants inevitably face intense competition on the supply side. To survive in such a competitive landscape, one must either pursue a differentiated solution or achieve lower production costs.

Pursuing ever-lower costs is an endless race to the bottom, and in the long run, there are rarely any winners. This is exemplified by the current state of Chinese industry, where relentless competition on cost and price has improved industrial efficiency and benefited consumers, but from the perspective of businesses, profit margins have declined and widespread losses have become common.

By contrast, differentiation helps businesses avoid this race to the bottom and build competitive advantages. For differentiated products or services addressing known demand, there are several possible approaches:

### Upgrade: Offering Superior Products or Services

Take Atour Hotels as an example. In its early days, Atour identified a gap in the mid-to-high-end hotel market. In terms of service, it subtracted from the five-star hotel experience while adding to the budget hotel experience, thereby carving out a distinctive product positioning.

How do you let people know your offering is superior? Through branding and design. Consumer brands with high brand equity tend to achieve higher returns on investment.

In the current economic climate, there is a prevailing sense of belt-tightening, and consumption downgrade is a common narrative. Yet one must recognize that opportunities for consumption upgrade still coexist within the consumer market. After all, the pursuit of a better life is a timeless human aspiration. The growing demand for health, self-care, and quality of life is giving rise to a new generation of brands.

### Go Downmarket: Offering More Cost-Effective Products or Services

Take Pinduoduo as an example. When Pinduoduo was founded, JD.com and Taobao had already captured the minds of consumers. Through its distinctive business model, Pinduoduo first established a strong foothold in lower-tier markets. In today's environment of consumer austerity, Pinduoduo has successfully parlayed this regional advantage into overall market prominence.

China's urban-rural divide and regional disparities are vast, and one could say the market is distinctly stratified. As the old saying goes, "One cannot see the true face of Mount Lu while standing upon it" -- each of us only sees the small patch of ground we are familiar with. For a business to succeed in the mass market, it must go deep to understand the needs of lower-tier markets, while also rising above to gain clearer insight into the structural stratification of society.

### Serve the Long Tail: Meeting More Personalized Market Demand

Modern society has brought about a diversification of values and liberated individual expression. At the same time, as overall societal wealth has increased, consumers have both the means and the willingness to pursue personalized needs. China has the world's largest consumer market, which means there are abundant opportunities for experimentation, and serving personalized demand can still achieve economies of scale.

Through continuous innovation and strategic repositioning, entrepreneurs can find the right business model to establish themselves in a fiercely competitive market and achieve long-term success.
