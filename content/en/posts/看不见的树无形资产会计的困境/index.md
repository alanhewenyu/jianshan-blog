*The Story of Accounting Standards (Part VII)*

In January 2026, the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis published Staff Report 682, a working paper that studied seventy years of U.S. macroeconomic data (primarily from the BEA's National Income and Product Accounts and the Fed's Flow of Funds) to answer a question that has long troubled value investors: why have traditional relative valuation metrics for U.S. equities remained persistently above their historical averages without mean-reverting?

Their core conclusion came from a deep decomposition of a simple identity:

**Earnings/V − Free Cash Flow/V = Net Investment/V**

Here, enterprise value V is the valuation of the entire firm (market capitalization plus the market value of interest-bearing debt). The difference between earnings (operating surplus before financing costs) and the free cash flow returned to shareholders is "Net Investment" (capital expenditure minus depreciation).

From 1980 to mid-2022, the earnings/V ratio for the U.S. corporate sector declined steadily from 9.5% to 4.6%. Over the same period, however, the free cash flow/V ratio remained stable near its historical average of roughly 3.6%. In other words, from a free cash flow perspective, corporate valuations had not significantly deviated from the mean — it was just that relative valuation metrics may no longer be reliable. The paper used a vivid metaphor: the U.S. corporate sector is quietly shifting from the textbook "growth model" economy — one that requires continuous capital reinvestment to grow — into a Lucas tree economy (from Robert Lucas's 1978 asset pricing model): a tree that needs no fertilizer or irrigation, yet bears fruit year after year.

This invisible tree generates a steady stream of free cash flow, yet you can scour the balance sheet and find no trace of where this "tree" actually is.

## IAS 38: A Rulebook from the Last Century

Intellectual property advisory firm Ocean Tomo has tracked the market value composition of S&P 500 constituents since 2005: subtract net tangible assets from market capitalization, and the difference is "intangible asset market value." Their latest report, published in February 2026, showed that by the end of 2025, this difference accounted for 92% of total market capitalization; rewind to 1975, and tangible assets made up 83%. A complete reversal in half a century. Of course, this dataset needs to be taken with a grain of salt — it attributes the entire "market cap minus tangible assets" gap to intangibles, inevitably capturing noise from interest rate environments and valuation multiple changes. But even after discounting, one fundamental fact is clear: the way today's best companies create value is fundamentally different from half a century ago.

And the rules? The current intangible asset accounting standard, IAS 38, was issued by the International Accounting Standards Committee (IASC, the predecessor to the IASB) in 1998 and has been carried forward since the IASB took over in 2001. It underwent revisions in 2004, 2008, and 2014, but the core framework has never truly changed.

The basic logic of IAS 38 is not complicated. For an intangible asset to appear on the balance sheet, it must simultaneously satisfy three conditions: identifiability (it can be separated from goodwill and can be individually sold, transferred, or licensed), the entity has control over it, and its cost can be reliably measured. For purchased intangible assets — such as patents or customer relationships acquired through M&A — these three conditions are usually easy to meet, and the asset is recorded at fair value.

But for intangible assets created internally, the standard erects a wall that is nearly impossible to scale. Paragraph 63 of IAS 38 explicitly states: internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, customer lists, and "items similar in substance" shall not be recognized as assets. R&D expenditure is split into a research phase and a development phase — all research-phase spending is expensed, and development-phase spending can be capitalized only when six strict conditions are simultaneously met. Part VI of this series, *Schrödinger's Profit: The Two Fates of R&D Expenditure*, discussed in detail how this framework is applied in gray areas on the A-share market.

Here lies a seemingly strange contradiction: one and the same brand cannot be recognized on the balance sheet if cultivated internally, but can be recognized if acquired — why? The standard's answer is: in an acquisition, the brand's price is anchored by an actual market transaction — whatever the two parties agreed upon in the market constitutes a "reliable measurement basis." A brand built up gradually over time has no such market transaction and therefore no objective cost to record. So Coca-Cola's self-built brand is worth zero on its own books, but if a company were to acquire Coca-Cola, the acquirer could recognize that brand as an identifiable intangible asset worth tens of billions of dollars — valuation experts have well-established methodologies for this. In other words, the standard is not "unable" to value a brand; it chooses not to do so in the case of internal creation — preferring to omit real assets rather than letting management freely estimate values without a market transaction as an anchor. This is the principle of prudence that the standard has consistently upheld.

## The Blind Spot on the Balance Sheet

New York University accounting professor Baruch Lev argued in his 2016 book *The End of Accounting*, using over sixty years of empirical data, that the correlation between GAAP information (book value, net income, etc.) and stock prices has been declining, attributing this partly to the non-recognition of intangible assets. This argument has been influential in academia but has also drawn considerable pushback. The CFA Institute, in evaluating Lev's research, pointed out that declining correlation does not necessarily mean declining usefulness — even if financial statements cannot explain all stock price movements, they remain a cornerstone of investor analysis.

This rebuttal is fair. For DCF investors, the income statement and cash flow statement are the core inputs to valuation. A company's profits are real, its cash flows are real, and they are not distorted by an incomplete balance sheet. The "non-recognition" of intangible assets does not prevent investors from determining whether a company is profitable.

But blind spots do exist, and they are most pronounced in metrics that use the balance sheet as the denominator. Price-to-book ratio is the most direct example: when a company's true economic assets are only partially reflected on the books, P/B loses its original function as a "margin of safety anchor." ROIC and ROE suffer similarly — when the denominator is missing years of accumulated brand value, user networks, and organizational capabilities, the resulting return rates are systematically overstated. This distortion is especially pronounced in intangible-asset-intensive industries such as technology, consumer brands, and internet platforms. The problem is not that the income statement is lying — the income statement is honest — but that the balance sheet has a gaping hole, and all analytical tools that use assets as the denominator must be wielded with greater independent judgment.

## Between Reliability and Relevance

The dilemma facing standard setters is real.

Those opposing the recognition of internally generated intangible assets have strong reasons. Intangible assets lack active markets, comparable transactions are scarce, and valuations are highly subjective. Once management is allowed to decide which internal expenditures can be capitalized, at what amounts, and over how many years they should be amortized, the scope for earnings management expands dramatically. The R&D capitalization story discussed in Part VI already illustrated this: in the A-share market, within the same industry, some companies expense 100% of their R&D, while others capitalize as much as 80%. If the scope of recognition were extended from "development expenditure" to brands, data, algorithms, and organizational capabilities, the situation would only become more chaotic.

But proponents of reform also have compelling arguments. The most frequently cited is Lev's version: he is not asking for an imagined "total intangible asset valuation" to be stuffed into the balance sheet, but rather a step back — at least treat the amounts companies spend each year on R&D, brand building, and customer acquisition as investments, capitalizing them at cost with amortization. This involves no subjective valuation whatsoever; it simply achieves better matching of revenues and expenses and prevents the denominator from being completely blank. This is a far more moderate path than fair value measurement.

In fact, the market is already patching this crack on its own. Pharmaceutical companies provide detailed pipeline updates during earnings calls. SaaS companies voluntarily disclose annual recurring revenue (ARR) and churn rates. Internet platforms publish monthly active users and customer acquisition costs. These are all non-GAAP metrics that investors demand companies disclose — they cannot be found in formal financial statements, yet investors are often more sensitive to changes in these figures than to the financials themselves. They exist, and their importance is growing, because investors have realized that standard financial statements alone cannot fully reveal what a company is truly investing in, what it is accumulating, and what it is worth.

## The Sluggishness of International Standards and China's Data Asset Experiment

Standard setters are not unaware of the problem. In April 2024, the IASB formally added "Intangible Assets" as a research project, planning a comprehensive review of IAS 38. By May 2025, the IASB had identified two work streams: first, assessing investors' specific information needs regarding intangible assets — what exactly they want to see and whether this information belongs in financial statements; second, considering whether to update the definition of intangible assets and certain recognition criteria, starting with "test cases" such as cloud computing costs and agile software development before gradually expanding to areas like data resources and AI. According to the timeline, preliminary solutions will not be discussed until 2026.

In October 2025, the IASB and FASB held a Joint Education Session — a routine progress-sharing mechanism between the two major standard-setting bodies that does not produce formal decisions but reflects their latest positions. The session sent a noteworthy signal: investors' core demand is not "put more numbers on the balance sheet" but rather "help me understand how this company creates value." What they want is more granular disclosure, especially regarding which expenditures are maintenance-related and which are capital-building. In other words, rather than having management price brands and algorithms and place them on the balance sheet, investors may prefer mandatory supplementary disclosures — the direction and stage distribution of R&D spending, customer acquisition costs and retention rates, data asset use cases and monetization models — so they can judge for themselves what these intangible elements are worth. This is a more pragmatic reform path than comprehensive recognition.

By comparison, China has moved faster in the specific area of data assets. In August 2023, the Ministry of Finance issued the *Interim Provisions on Accounting Treatment of Enterprise Data Resources* (Caikuai [2023] No. 11), effective January 1, 2024, making China one of the first major economies in the world to attempt formally incorporating data assets into the accounting recognition framework.

The core approach is to provide detailed guidance within the existing standards framework: companies can recognize qualifying data resources as intangible assets or inventory based on their business model, with "of which: data resources" separately presented under the corresponding balance sheet line items. This is inherently prudent — it does not create new standards or introduce new measurement bases; it simply makes existing rules explicitly applicable to data.

According to two independent analyses — one by Houxue Research and one by the SAIF ThinkTank at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, based on Wind and Rongliang Technology data respectively — by the time 2024 annual reports were fully disclosed, 100 out of over 5,400 A-share listed companies had completed data asset recognition, with total disclosed amounts of approximately RMB 2.16 to 2.25 billion. The three major telecom operators (China Mobile at RMB 616 million, China Unicom at RMB 379 million, and China Telecom at RMB 371 million) accounted for a combined RMB 1.366 billion, or 61% of the total. The remainder was primarily distributed among local government financing vehicles, local state-owned enterprises, and a small number of technology-oriented private firms.

However, data asset recognition has also become a financing tool for some companies. The typical pathway is: a company first lists its data products on a data exchange for rights confirmation, obtains a registration certificate, completes accounting recognition, and then uses the recognized data assets as collateral to apply for bank credit. Digital China's approach in June 2024 was a landmark case: after recording its "JinFuYun" data product under the "Intangible Assets — Data Resources" line item on its balance sheet, it obtained a RMB 30 million credit facility from China Construction Bank's Shenzhen branch. Similar cases have continued to proliferate. According to the SAIF ThinkTank report, based on incomplete public information as of March 31, 2025, cumulative data asset financing by non-listed companies had reached RMB 1.417 billion, of which RMB 1.271 billion came from local state-owned enterprises and RMB 146 million from private enterprises.

The positive significance of this chain is obvious: it opens a new financing channel for asset-light technology SMEs that lack factories and land for collateral — data assets fill that gap. But from an accounting standards perspective, the concerns are equally apparent: when the primary motivation for recognition is not "accurately reflecting asset conditions" but rather "inflating asset size and improving the debt-to-asset ratio for financing purposes," accounting recognition risks being instrumentalized.

## How Should Investors Deal with Invisible Assets?

For value investors, the incompleteness of intangible asset accounting should not be treated as an external problem waiting for standard setters to solve. It should be incorporated into one's own analytical framework and actively addressed.

First, the applicable boundaries of price-to-book ratio. When Graham wrote *The Intelligent Investor*, P/B was one of the most important anchors in value investing, because tangible assets constituted the bulk of enterprise value at that time. In which industries does this metric remain effective today? Banking and insurance, because their assets are predominantly financial assets with small differences between book value and realizable value; real estate and infrastructure, because the underlying assets have clear market comparables. But in intangible-asset-intensive industries such as technology, consumer brands, pharmaceutical R&D, and internet platforms, P/B conveys very limited information. Tencent's P/B has long hovered around 4–5x — not because it is "expensive," but because the B is missing WeChat's network effects, the licensing value of its gaming IP, and the synergies of its investment portfolio. Using P/B to judge whether Tencent is overvalued or undervalued is worse than not looking at it at all. For such companies, EV/EBIT, EV/FCFF, or DCF are far more meaningful.

Second, adjusting ROIC. As discussed above, ROIC for asset-light companies is overstated because the denominator excludes internally generated intangible assets. In practice, investors can capitalize and add back to invested capital several years of expensed R&D and customer acquisition costs using reasonable assumptions, then recalculate. This adjustment need not be precise, but it helps you distinguish whether a company's high return on capital truly comes from competitive advantage or is merely an artifact of accounting rules.

Third, pay attention to the intangible asset information already available in financial statement notes. Even though the intangible asset figures on the balance sheet may be limited, the notes typically disclose total R&D expenditure and the capitalization ratio, details and amortization policies for purchased intangible assets, and opening and closing balances of development expenditure along with amounts transferred to intangible assets. Additionally, as China's data asset recognition progresses, changes in the "data resources" sub-line item, amortization life assumptions, and whether assets are immediately pledged for financing after recognition are all signals worth tracking.

Buffett wrote in his 1983 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter that he had "a strong preference for businesses that possess large amounts of enduring economic goodwill and that utilize a minimum of tangible assets." He deliberately distinguished "accounting goodwill" (the book record of acquisition premiums) from "economic goodwill" (the real economic value of brands, reputation, and customer loyalty) — the former is a product of rules, while the latter is the essence of the business. See's Candies was his favorite example: when Blue Chip Stamps acquired it in 1972 for $25 million, See's had only $8 million in net tangible assets and roughly $2 million in after-tax earnings — a 25% return on tangible assets, with an enormous economic goodwill clearly sitting off the books. Buffett paid three times net tangible assets. Years later, he reflected that without See's teaching him the power of economic goodwill, the investment in Coca-Cola would never have happened.

At the 2018 Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, he revisited the theme: "The four largest companies today by market value [at the time: Apple, Google, Microsoft, and Amazon] require virtually no net tangible assets — they are not like AT&T, General Motors, or Exxon Mobil, which needed enormous amounts of capital to make money. We have become an asset-light economy." Whether investors can follow Buffett's line of thinking and identify companies with enduring economic goodwill behind the absent balance sheet may be the greatest cognitive premium available to value investors today.

## Empty Balance Sheets, Overflowing Cash Flows

The most valuable investments of modern enterprises — brands, data, algorithms, user networks, organizational capabilities — largely cannot make their way onto the balance sheet. This goes far beyond "R&D being expensed." R&D at least has an identifiable expenditure, and under IFRS there is still partial room for capitalization (the subject of Part VI). The truly intractable category is a different class of intangible assets — those that can neither be capitalized nor often traced to a clear expense. The rise of a brand is the compound interest of decades of advertising, pricing strategy, product refinement, and user trust, with every small amount scattered across selling expenses, channel costs, and labor costs impossible to individually tag as "brand building." A network effect is users attracting users, years of platform subsidies and governance accumulated over time — no journal entry can be written as "purchased network effect of X dollars today." An organizational capability is a composite of systems, culture, talent pipelines, and decision-making habits, never corresponding to any single attributable expenditure. These things are real within a company — they happen every day and are the true explanatory variables behind pricing and ROIC.

Nor is this entirely a matter of "standard setters doing nothing." The IASB has been re-examining IAS 38 since 2024 and has clearly expressed its intent to reform. Many of the measurement challenges for intangible assets may inherently push against the boundaries of what accounting can do — for an asset to be recognized under accounting standards, it needs at minimum the basic attributes of identifiability, controllability, and reliable measurability, yet brands, network effects, and organizational capabilities often cannot even pass the "identifiability" test. This is not for lack of effort by the standards; it is that certain characteristics of these assets may simply be unsuited to being captured on a balance sheet.

The inadequacy of accounting thus takes on a structural character: the balance sheet's function as a snapshot of "what the company owns" is weakening, and all analytical tools that use it as a denominator — P/B, ROIC, ROE, leverage ratios — require investors to exercise greater independent judgment.
