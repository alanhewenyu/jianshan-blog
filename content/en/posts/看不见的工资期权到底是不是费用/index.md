*The Story of Accounting Standards (Part V)*

Open the financial statements of almost any tech company listed in the U.S. or Hong Kong, and you will notice a pattern: the same company reports two sets of profits, and the "adjusted" version is almost always higher. Roughly 80% of Dow Jones component companies provide non-GAAP profit figures, averaging 31% above their GAAP equivalents. One of the most common — and most controversial — adjustments behind this gap is treating stock-based compensation (SBC) paid to employees as an expense that can be added back. Tech companies are especially prone to this: they depend on innovation and human capital, often substituting equity incentives for cash compensation to attract talent, making SBC a far larger share of total expenses than in traditional industries.

In February 2026, Nvidia announced a notable change alongside its fiscal year results: starting the next quarter, it would no longer exclude SBC as an adjustment in its non-GAAP metrics — the exact opposite of what most tech companies do. Nvidia could afford this largely because its profits had grown so large that SBC barely moved the needle — with full-year after-tax profit of roughly $116 billion and SBC of about $6 billion, the impact was less than 5%. But analysts estimate that if peers like Broadcom, AMD, and Marvell did the same, their non-GAAP profits would shrink by 14% to 20%.

Yet today's debate over SBC treatment in non-GAAP metrics has its roots somewhere deeper — in GAAP itself. The accounting treatment of share-based payments is the product of a protracted, decades-long battle in the history of standard-setting. An expense that seems self-evident today — "compensation given to employees should be recorded as an expense" — took more than thirty years to be written into U.S. financial reporting rules. This history involves genuine technical difficulties, intense political intervention, and deep conflicts of interest, and its lessons remain directly relevant to understanding today's debates around non-GAAP and SBC.

## When Valuation Models Did Not Yet Exist

The history of stock options as compensation dates back to the 1950s. The U.S. Revenue Act of 1950 allowed option gains to be taxed at the lower capital gains rate, but tax benefits were only one reason options took root in Silicon Valley. The deeper driver was the practical reality facing startups: early-stage tech companies were often cash-strapped and unable to compete head-to-head with large corporations on salary, but they could use options to promise employees future upside. Fairchild Semiconductor in the 1960s and Intel, founded in 1968, embedded employee option ownership into their corporate DNA — it was precisely this "trade the future for the present" compensation model that allowed Silicon Valley to win the talent war against established tech hubs like Boston and New York.

Accounting standards lagged far behind. ARB 37 in 1948 was the earliest standard to address option accounting, but it was APB Opinion 25, issued in 1972, that truly set the framework for the next half-century. The core logic was the "intrinsic value method": an expense was recognized only when the exercise price was below the market price on the grant date (i.e., an "in-the-money" option), and only for the difference. If the exercise price equaled the market price ("at the money") or exceeded it ("out of the money"), intrinsic value was zero or negative, and no expense was recognized.

In practice, virtually all companies set the exercise price at or above the grant-date market price, so zero option expense became the norm. Looking back, the rule ignored the "time value" of options — the possibility that the stock price might rise in the future is itself worth something. But there is a timeline detail often overlooked: **when APB 25 was issued in 1972, modern option pricing theory did not yet exist.**

Black and Scholes published their paper in May 1973 — exactly one year after APB 25. The Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE) did not open until April 1973 either. Before then, the time value of options was a concept people intuitively knew existed but lacked a widely accepted quantitative framework to measure. In the 1960s, Samuelson published theoretical research on warrant pricing, and Thorp and Kassouf produced a practical guide to warrant arbitrage, but it was not until Black-Scholes assembled these pieces into a complete pricing model — and it subsequently gained broad adoption — that option valuation moved from guesswork to actionable methodology.

In other words, APB 25's choice in 1972 was not entirely a "loophole" but reflected the technical limitations of the time. Without good valuation tools, the intrinsic value method was an understandable compromise.

The problem was what happened next. After Black-Scholes was published, Wall Street quickly adopted it for pricing exchange-traded options. Accounting standards, however, did not budge. Although FASB (the Financial Accounting Standards Board) began reexamining the issue as early as 1984, political resistance — which we will discuss shortly — meant APB 25's zero-expense rule effectively remained in force until new standards took effect in 2006. Under this regime, a company could grant employees options worth billions of dollars without recording a single cent of expense on the income statement. Options diluted existing shareholders' equity — a real transfer of wealth — yet were completely invisible in the financial statements.

Buffett cut through this in his 1998 shareholder letter with three questions: "If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it? And if expenses shouldn't go into the calculation of earnings, where in the world should they go?"

It is worth noting that another form of share-based payment — restricted stock — always required expense recognition under APB 25. This reflects a fundamental difference in the economic characteristics of the two instruments. Restricted stock's incentive effect comes from the discount: the company grants shares to employees at below-market price (or even for zero consideration), and the discount itself is the compensation. Without a discount, employees could simply buy shares on the open market — there would be no incentive. So restricted stock naturally had positive intrinsic value on the grant date (the difference between market price and the price paid by the employee) and had to be recognized as an expense under APB 25. Options work differently: they give employees the right to buy stock at a fixed price in the future, and even when the exercise price equals the current market price (zero intrinsic value), options are still attractive to employees because of their "time value." It was precisely this characteristic that allowed companies to grant options with real economic value under APB 25's intrinsic value method without recognizing any expense. The expensing debate was therefore, from the very beginning, specifically about stock options.

## FASB's Two Charges

In 1984, FASB set out to reexamine option accounting. By then, Black-Scholes had been around for over a decade, and valuation tools were no longer an obstacle. After nearly ten years of research and discussion, FASB issued an exposure draft in June 1993, proposing that companies use option pricing models such as Black-Scholes to recognize option expenses at fair value.

Opponents still trotted out the "cannot be accurately valued" argument. To be fair, employee options do have some unique features — non-transferability, potential early exercise, vesting periods — that require adjustments to the standard Black-Scholes model. But these technical adjustments were quite mature by the 1990s. Moreover, accounting standards are full of items requiring estimation — bad debt provisions, pension liabilities, asset impairments — and no one ever argued that imprecision meant an item should not be recognized at all. Valuation difficulty was a legitimate technical concern but insufficient to justify complete non-recognition of expenses.

The truly fierce opposition came from vested interests. Led by the American Electronics Association, Silicon Valley tech companies quickly assembled a lobbying coalition. Their core argument was that expensing would devastate reported profits, stifle innovation, and harm America's technological competitiveness. Senator Lieberman of Connecticut introduced a bill attempting to legally block FASB. Senator Levin later recalled the scene in the 2002 Congressional Record: on the most intense day of lobbying, 100 CEOs flew to Washington on the same day. An accounting standards issue was successfully reframed as a political issue concerning national competitiveness.

In May 1994, the Senate voted 88 to 9 to pass a non-binding resolution "recommending" that FASB not proceed with its expensing proposal. This was an extraordinarily rare instance of the U.S. Senate publicly pressuring an independent standard-setting body. SEC Chairman Arthur Levitt supported expensing but faced a difficult judgment call under Congressional pressure: under the U.S. institutional framework, FASB's standard-setting authority derives from SEC recognition, and Congress, as the legislative body, has the power to pass laws that could fundamentally restructure this arrangement. If FASB pushed ahead, Congress might alter the very institutional architecture of standard-setting. Levitt chose to persuade FASB to back down temporarily, preserving the institution itself. Years later, in his memoir *Take on the Street*, he wrote: "I consider this the biggest mistake I made during my tenure."

In 1995, FASB issued the compromised SFAS 123: it encouraged but did not mandate expensing. Companies that chose not to expense only needed to disclose the impact of options in footnotes. The result was predictable — virtually all companies chose not to recognize expenses on the income statement.

The turning point came after the dot-com bubble burst at the turn of the millennium. From 2001 to 2002, a series of corporate financial scandals came to light. The opaque option compensation culture was part of the problem: some executives exercised massive options to cash out before their companies headed for bankruptcy. Public sentiment reversed sharply, and the same senators who had voted to block FASB in 1994 now reversed course. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 strengthened FASB's independence and financial foundation.

In 2004, FASB issued another exposure draft. The business community itself was now divided — nearly 300 companies had voluntarily adopted expensing, Greenspan and Buffett publicly supported FASB, and the major accounting firms that had previously opposed expensing changed their position. Microsoft announced in 2003 that it would stop granting new options and shift to restricted stock — SFAS 123(R) had not yet been passed, but the market widely expected expensing was imminent, and Microsoft's shift was a proactive embrace of the trend rather than a forced adjustment.

In December 2004, FASB issued SFAS 123(R), requiring all public companies to recognize share-based payment expenses at fair value — applying uniformly to both options and restricted stock. From the initial project launch in 1984 to final implementation, the process took a full twenty years. After that, the industry's structural shift from options to restricted stock accelerated — once the expensing difference between the two disappeared, restricted stock's advantages in incentive certainty became more prominent: employees did not need to understand option pricing models to evaluate the value of their compensation, and companies did not have to bear the risk that options might become "underwater" (exercise price above market price) and lose their incentive effect due to stock price declines.

Meanwhile, international standards were progressing in parallel. The IASB (International Accounting Standards Board) added share-based payments to its agenda in 2001 and issued an exposure draft in November 2002, receiving over 240 comment letters. In its Basis for Conclusions, the IASB explicitly discussed and rejected the argument that "option costs are borne by shareholders, not the company" — reasoning that it is the company (not shareholders) that establishes the option plan and receives employee services in return, so the expense belongs to the company. IFRS 2 was formally issued in February 2004 and became effective in 2005. Throughout the process, the IASB and FASB maintained close convergence coordination, with the two sets of standards highly consistent on core principles and differing only on certain technical details (such as the method of allocating expenses for graded vesting).

## The Arguments and Motivations of Each Side

This battle lasted twenty years, and the arguments and motivations of each side are worth examining.

Tech companies opposed expensing on two stated grounds: options have no cash cost, so they are not a "real" expense; and options cannot be accurately valued. Buffett offered an elegant rebuttal to the first argument on multiple occasions — including his 1998 shareholder letter and Berkshire annual meetings: if options truly have no cost, companies should try paying for everything with options — utilities, insurance — and see if the counterparty accepts. That said, whether options constitute an expense of the company was genuinely debated in academic circles. One view held that options are essentially a redistribution of wealth among shareholders (existing shareholders transferring equity to employees) and do not directly consume company resources — the IASB specifically discussed this "gain-sharing" argument in IFRS 2's Basis for Conclusions before ultimately rejecting it. What truly poisoned the debate was the injection of self-interest: expensing meant lower profits, lower profits could mean falling stock prices, and management's own option values were tied directly to stock prices. A debate with a reasonable academic core was amplified by conflicts of interest into a political campaign.

Investors were not monolithic either. The CFA Institute, representing institutional investors, firmly supported expensing — they needed transparent, comparable financial information for investment decisions. But some Wall Street analysts, accustomed to using non-GAAP metrics, were not entirely sold on adding a volatile non-cash expense to GAAP earnings. This divide persists to this day.

The accounting profession's own stance is also worth noting. In 1994, the then-Big Six accounting firms jointly wrote a letter expressing concerns about expensing — they should have been the standard-setter's most natural allies. By 2004, those same firms had switched to supporting FASB. The shift, rather than reflecting an evolution in understanding, was driven by the enormous pressure on the profession's public trust and reputation following the dot-com bust and a string of corporate accounting scandals.

FASB's own experience may be the most thought-provoking. Jim Leisenring, former IASB Vice Chairman and one of only two FASB board members who refused to compromise in the 1994 internal vote, later acknowledged: "FASB was wrong to back down, but the decision was made at a time when virtually no one supported it." Being technically correct does not mean being politically viable. Arthur Levitt's choice — a tactical retreat to preserve the institution — preserved FASB's independence in hindsight, but the cost was a decade of opacity in financial statements regarding option compensation.

## The China Perspective

In 2006, China's Ministry of Finance issued CAS 11 — *Share-based Payments*, substantially converged with IFRS 2, requiring share-based payment expenses to be recognized at fair value. Chinese standards on this topic directly adopted the mature experience of international standards.

CAS 11 has its own practical challenges. The most prominent is determining fair value for unlisted companies — a universal challenge worldwide. Listed companies' options can be reasonably valued using models like Black-Scholes, but pre-IPO companies — especially those implementing equity incentives through employee stock ownership platforms — face a dilemma: without an active market for the stock, what should fair value be based on? In practice, some companies reference the most recent PE/VC investment price, others use net asset value, and the resulting valuations can differ significantly. Chinese regulators have a clear stance on this: the CSRC requires IPO applicants to recognize expenses for below-market equity transfers under the share-based payment standard, using contemporaneous PE investment prices as the primary basis for fair value. The core practical controversy is this: when multiple equity transactions at different prices exist in the same period (PE investments, management incentives, transfers between existing shareholders), which price best represents fair value? Different choices lead to vastly different expense amounts, leaving considerable room for judgment by companies and auditors.

Among listed companies, the STAR Market's share-based payment disclosure practices are showing new characteristics. A large number of tech companies use equity incentives to attract talent, making share-based payments a significant line item on the income statement. BeiGene (688235.SH), dual-listed on the STAR Market and the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, is a typical case: its 2024 GAAP-basis financial statements still showed a loss, but its adjusted operating profit — excluding share-based payments and depreciation/amortization — turned positive for three consecutive quarters, reaching RMB 528 million for the full year. The Shanghai Stock Exchange noted in its 2024 STAR Market performance overview that over 30 companies had begun disclosing non-GAAP financial data.

Notably, the "net profit after deducting non-recurring gains and losses" mandatorily disclosed by A-share listed companies (commonly called "koufe") is a different system with different adjustment criteria from non-GAAP metrics under international standards. According to the CSRC's *Regulatory Guidelines — Accounting Category No. 1* and *Accounting Regulatory Communications* Issue 4 of 2016, equity-settled share-based payment expenses are classified as **recurring gains and losses** — on the grounds that they represent normal employee compensation expenses, related to operations and recurring in nature. Therefore, A-share "koufe" does not exclude SBC. The non-GAAP adjustments made by companies like BeiGene are voluntary supplemental disclosures beyond "koufe." This is similar to U.S. tech companies excluding SBC in non-GAAP metrics, but it is still a new phenomenon in China's capital markets, with disclosure standards and investor awareness both in early stages of development.

## How Should Investors Approach Share-Based Payments?

For value investors, share-based payments are not just an accounting technicality — they are key to understanding true earning power.

First, it must be acknowledged that excluding SBC from non-GAAP metrics is not entirely unreasonable. SBC is a non-cash expense, heavily influenced by option pricing model parameters, and its quarterly fluctuations do not fully reflect operating rhythm. Meta and Google also excluded SBC in their early years, then voluntarily stopped treating it as a non-GAAP adjustment in 2017 — Nvidia's 2026 move is the same, choosing a more conservative disclosure approach once profitability reached sufficient scale. For companies in high-growth phases that make heavy use of equity incentives, non-GAAP can help investors focus on cash-level operating performance. But investors must also see the other side of the coin: while SBC does not consume cash, it consumes shareholder equity.

The key principle for properly handling SBC in valuation is consistency. If you add back SBC when calculating free cash flow (treating it as a non-cash item, thereby inflating the cash flow figure), then the share count in the denominator when calculating per-share value must reflect the fully diluted number to account for the potential dilution from options and restricted stock. Conversely, if you use the current outstanding share count to calculate per-share value, SBC should be treated as a normal expense in the cash flow calculation, with no adjustment. The two approaches are mathematically equivalent — the key is that you cannot enjoy the benefit of adding back SBC in the numerator while ignoring the cost of dilution in the denominator — that amounts to double-counting the overstatement of per-share value.

Another question is how to view share buybacks by listed companies. Many tech companies spend large amounts of cash repurchasing stock each year, but not all repurchased shares are retired — a significant portion is retained for future equity incentive plans. This means buybacks are essentially using cash to "fund" share-based payments, without truly reducing the share count or enhancing per-share value. Therefore, monitoring a company's net dilution rate (annual new share grants minus shares repurchased and retired) is more informative than looking at either the buyback amount or SBC expense alone.

When reading annual reports, several items deserve special attention: the share-based payment details under administrative expenses and R&D expenses on the income statement (A-share companies typically disclose these in the notes), the non-cash adjustment for SBC added back in operating cash flow on the cash flow statement, and the footnote disclosures on option pricing model parameters — choices of expected volatility, risk-free rate, and expected term significantly affect SBC expense amounts and are potential tools for earnings management.

## Conclusion

From APB 25 in 1972 to SFAS 123(R) taking effect in 2006, the seemingly simple logic that "options are compensation, and compensation is an expense" took more than thirty years to be written into U.S. financial reporting rules. This history should not be reduced to a simple narrative of "vested interests blocking progress." The early intrinsic value method was genuinely constrained by technical conditions; the debate over whether options constitute a company expense rather than a redistribution among shareholders was, for a considerable period, a sincere academic disagreement. And the political intervention of the 1990s, while ultimately serving commercial interests, did exploit a legitimate tension in institutional design — the balance between independent standard-setting and democratic accountability.

But this history does reveal a pattern worth heeding: when a technical issue involves sufficiently large economic stakes, reasonable disagreements can easily be amplified by self-interest into political confrontation, and once institutional compromises are made, they create path dependency — even after the original rationale has faded.

SFAS 123(R) and IFRS 2 resolved the expense recognition question, but the controversy surrounding share-based payments did not end — it lives on today in the form of voluntary non-GAAP adjustments disclosed by public companies. SBC as a non-GAAP reconciling item does have its merits; it provides investors with a supplementary lens for observing operating performance. But Buffett's triple question — "If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it?" — remains a mental tool worth carrying when reading financial statements and calculating valuations.
