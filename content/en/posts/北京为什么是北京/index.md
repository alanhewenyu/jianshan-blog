During the Spring Festival holiday, many friends came to visit Beijing. But if you only see the Forbidden City and the Great Wall without understanding why this city has been China's political center for over 800 years, you're missing half the story.

Today I'll share some historical stories about Beijing, hoping to help everyone understand this city a little better.

## Where Does the Water Come From?

For anyone wanting to understand Beijing's history, the most recommended book is Hou Renzhi's *Historical Geography of Beiping*. Written in the 1940s as Hou's doctoral thesis while studying in London — when Beijing was still called Beiping — it remains a classic despite its age.

The book provides a comprehensive analysis of Beijing's historical geography, with particular focus on its water systems. I had always wondered: why was the earliest capital here, the Jin Dynasty's Zhongdu, located at the current city's position rather than further north? After all, modern Beijing's urban area is still quite far from the Yanshan Mountains to the north. The answer, I later realized, was entirely about water — Lianhua Pond near present-day Beijing West Railway Station was a crucial water source, and the Yongding River flowed not far to the south. This very location opened up a millennium of development space for the city.

By the Yuan Dynasty, when the capital shifted northward, Lianhua Pond was no longer sufficient. Guo Shoujing supervised hydraulic engineering projects to channel water from the Baifu Spring in Changping, with Wengshan Lake at the foot of the Western Hills — today's Kunming Lake at the Summer Palace — serving as a key reservoir node. Today, with Beijing's population, these ancient water sources are long inadequate; the city mainly relies on the Miyun Reservoir and the South-to-North Water Diversion Project.

## From Beiping to Beijing

Returning to the name "Beiping" — after the Northern Expedition in 1928, the Republican government, having made Nanjing its capital, renamed Beijing to Beiping.

But this wasn't a new invention. In the early Ming Dynasty, Zhu Yuanzhang had already renamed the Yuan Dynasty's Dadu to Beiping. His son Zhu Di, after seizing the throne through the Jingnan Campaign — having been the Prince of Yan with his fief right here — renamed Beiping to Beijing and formally moved the capital.

The rationale for making Beijing the capital surely included Zhu Di's personal attachment to his fief, but more importantly, it was Beijing's strategic position. Court officials jointly memorialized: Beijing "rests on Juyong Pass to the north, faces the Taihang Mountains to the west, connects to Shanhai Pass to the east, and overlooks the Central Plains to the south — with fertile lands stretching a thousand li and mountain-river terrain sufficient to command all directions. Truly an imperial capital for ten thousand generations."

Visitors who've never been might assume Beijing is bitterly cold. Compared to southern China, it certainly is. But precisely because Beijing is sheltered by mountains to the west and north, blocking cold air from the Mongolian Plateau, it's not as cold as you'd think. Beijing is a city of sunshine with dry winters — the perceived temperature is actually more comfortable. Measured by whether daytime highs stay above freezing, Beijing's winter is not as endlessly harsh as people imagine.

## The An Lushan Rebellion: A Turning Point

Beijing's strategic importance actually became apparent as early as the Tang Dynasty. Emperor Taizong Li Shimin, returning from his eastern campaign against Goguryeo in the Korean Peninsula, stopped in Beijing — then called Youzhou — and ordered the construction of Minyou Temple to commemorate fallen soldiers. This temple is today's Fayuan Temple, a witness to Beijing's ancient city location.

The An Lushan Rebellion in the mid-Tang was an even greater turning point, not only affecting the Tang Dynasty's fortunes but highlighting Beijing's importance for the first time.

An Lushan launched his rebellion from Youzhou — present-day Beijing. He was Military Governor of Fanyang, Pinglu, and Hedong, with Fanyang (where Youzhou was located) as his base. Why did An Lushan rebel? A recently published book, *The An Lushan Rebellion: History, Propaganda, and Myth*, points to an overlooked factor — the economic center of gravity had already been shifting south. The Jianghuai region was prosperous while Hebei faced fiscal difficulties and mounting social tensions. The north was no longer the empire's economic core, yet still bore the heaviest frontier defense burden.

Qian Mu expressed a similar view in *A General History of China*: before the mid-Tang, China's economic center was in the north; after the mid-Tang, it shifted to the south.

## The Emperor Guards the Border

If the northern economy was declining, why did Beijing become even more important? Because in the centuries following the An Lushan Rebellion, China's border threats consistently came from north of Beijing — nomadic peoples rising on the Mongolian Plateau beyond the Great Wall. The emperor had to guard the border, pushing the strategic center to the front lines. And Beijing had natural mountain defenses. In this respect, Emperor Chengzu of Ming showed extraordinary strategic vision.

The Song Dynasty makes this even clearer. The Northern Song's capital Kaifeng had no natural defenses, ultimately leading to the Treaty of Chanyuan with the Liao along the Yellow River. When the Jin destroyed the Liao and then the Northern Song, the Southern Song retreated to Hangzhou, and the entire North China Plain fell under Jin occupation.

From a strategic standpoint, Beijing has two crucial passes: Nankou to the northwest — the northernmost of the Eight Passes of Taihang and the boundary between the Taihang and Yanshan ranges — and Shanhai Pass to the east, built at the narrowest point of the Liaoxi Corridor where mountains meet the sea, the critical gateway between North China and Manchuria.

## Economic Shift South, Beijing Becomes More Important

Although China's economic center has remained in the south for centuries, counterintuitively, making Beijing the capital actually promoted north-south economic exchange and increased northern vitality. The Grand Canal is a prime example — grain and goods from the south were transported to Beijing by canal. In today's terms, this facilitated domestic economic circulation.

Beijing is Beijing not because of any single relocation of the capital, but because geography, warfare, and economic structure repeatedly pushed it to the center over 800 years.

For those interested in Beijing's historical sites and classical gardens, I recommend *A Brief Account of the Old Capital's Cultural Relics*, published in the 1930s. Though old, it's exquisitely detailed in its coverage of Beijing's historical sites — some of which no longer exist. More recent books worth reading include Wang Nan's *Ancient Capital Beijing* and Li Weiwen's *Layered Beijing* from the Palace Museum.
