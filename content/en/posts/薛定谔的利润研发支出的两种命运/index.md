*Stories of Accounting Standards (Part Six)*

On November 20, 2021, Hengrui Medicine issued an announcement about a change in accounting estimates. The core message boiled down to a single sentence: the company would shift from expensing all R&D spending to distinguishing between the research phase and the development phase, capitalizing qualifying development expenditures as assets, specifically after a drug enters Phase III clinical trials (or pivotal clinical trials). Since its IPO, Hengrui had maintained a consistent practice of expensing all R&D spending for over twenty consecutive years. Among A-share pharmaceutical manufacturers, R&D capitalization is actually quite common — according to iFinD data, around 2020, 142 out of 291 sample companies capitalized development costs, accounting for nearly half, with an average industry capitalization rate of about 22%. Hengrui's refusal to capitalize had earned it a reputation as one of the most conservative players in the sector.

The timing of the announcement was telling. In the first three quarters of 2021, Hengrui's net profit growth was -1.21% — the company's first decline since 2010. Under the hammer of centralized procurement (VBP), generic drug revenues had plummeted. At that point, a total of 28 products had been included in VBP, with winning bid prices dropping by an average of 72.6%. The heavy R&D spending was becoming unbearable for the income statement. Right at this sensitive moment of the company's first earnings decline, Hengrui — which had never capitalized R&D — chose to loosen the reins.

The effect was immediate. In 2022, Hengrui's capitalized R&D jumped to approximately 1.5 billion yuan. By the 2024 annual report, out of 8.228 billion yuan in total R&D spending, about 1.645 billion was capitalized (a capitalization rate of roughly 20%), with net profit at 6.337 billion yuan. Auditor EY listed "capitalization of development expenditures" as a Key Audit Matter in its audit report — a designation that itself signals the auditor considers management's judgment on this item to carry significant uncertainty.

Nine days after the announcement, on November 29, the Shanghai Stock Exchange issued a regulatory inquiry letter to Hengrui, requiring the company to prudently assess whether capitalization conditions were met and to ensure accurate and complete classification of R&D expenditures.

In the second half of 2025, another star technology company, iFlytek, received an inquiry letter from the Shenzhen Stock Exchange (regarding the review of its directed share issuance). One focus of the inquiry: the "development expenditures" on the company's balance sheet had ballooned from 717 million yuan at the end of 2022 to 2.189 billion yuan by mid-2025 — tripling in three years. The Shenzhen Stock Exchange required the company to explain item by item whether capitalization conditions were met and whether any long-term unresolved items existed. Since its listing, iFlytek had been controversial for its capitalization rate far exceeding industry peers — averaging about 46% from 2008 to 2021 — while Hundsun Technologies, also in the software industry, had long expensed all R&D, and Yonyou Network's capitalization rate over the same period was only about 13%.

The money has been spent; the cash flow is identical. Yet the same spending can make profits look completely different — this is not accounting magic; this is a gray area explicitly permitted by accounting standards in black and white.

To use a not-entirely-serious analogy: profit seems to have fallen into a Schrödinger-like superposition. The same R&D expenditure is suspended between capitalization and expensing, and the "observer" is management — their judgment determines which number profit ultimately collapses to.

## What the Standards Say

To understand the origins of this gray area, we must first clarify two fundamentally different sets of rules.

The American approach is extremely blunt. In 1974, the FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board) issued Statement No. 2 (SFAS 2), imposing a blanket requirement that all R&D expenditures be expensed in the period incurred. Whether your project has a 30% or 80% probability of success, whether you're conducting basic research or already hold a Phase III drug approval — everything is recorded as a current-period expense. The only exception is software development costs, which can be capitalized after technological feasibility is established (ASC 985-20 and ASC 350-40). Beyond that, all R&D spending by U.S. public companies is expensed. This rule has remained essentially unchanged since 1974.

International accounting standards took a different path. The IASB's (International Accounting Standards Board) IAS 38 (Intangible Assets) divides R&D activities into a research phase and a development phase. Research-phase expenditures must be expensed — consistent with the U.S. approach. But development-phase expenditures, if they simultaneously meet six conditions, must not only be capitalized but are *required* to be capitalized: technical feasibility, intention to complete, ability to use or sell, capacity to generate future economic benefits, sufficient resources to complete development, and the ability to reliably measure the expenditure.

China's enterprise accounting standards converged with international standards in 2006, essentially adopting the IAS 38 framework. Accounting Standard for Business Enterprises No. 6 — Intangible Assets explicitly stipulates that research-phase expenditures are expensed while development-phase expenditures meeting the conditions are capitalized. This means Chinese A-share listed companies, like their global peers under the IFRS system, have considerable discretionary room in how they treat R&D spending.

The six capitalization conditions appear strict, but in practice, almost every one is elastic. What constitutes "technical feasibility"? Does a new drug passing Phase II clinical trials count? Does completing the core architecture design of a piece of software count? "Intention to complete" is a subjective state that cannot be externally verified. As for "ability to generate future economic benefits" — if management were not optimistic about this, the project should never have been approved in the first place. When auditors assess whether R&D expenditures meet capitalization conditions, they can often only rely on internal documentation provided by management — leaving ample room for profit adjustment.

How large can the differences between the two sets of standards be within the same industry? The automotive sector provides an excellent observation window. In July 2025, NIO founder William Li said during a livestream from the company's Hefei factory — a remark that was widely reported: "We record R&D spending as current-period expenses. We lose money in the open, and our balance sheet is very clean." He specifically emphasized that NIO had cumulatively invested 60 billion yuan in R&D, yet intangible assets on the balance sheet were only $4 million — "because we hold ourselves to the highest financial standards."

This sounds admirable, but a key fact needs unpacking: NIO is dual-listed on the NYSE and HKEX, with financial statements prepared under US GAAP. Under US GAAP, ASC 730 requires all R&D expenditures to be expensed in the period incurred — this is not a "high standard" NIO voluntarily chose; it is a mandatory requirement of U.S. standards. Similarly, Li Auto (NASDAQ-listed) and XPeng (NYSE-listed) also report under US GAAP and likewise expense everything. Among "NIO, Li Auto, and XPeng," there is no difference whatsoever in R&D treatment.

Li's real comparison targets were likely the traditional automakers listed on A-shares. Companies like BYD, GAC Group, and SAIC Motor report under Chinese accounting standards, which allow capitalization of qualifying development expenditures. In practice, however, most mainstream A-share automakers are quite prudent: BYD's 2024 R&D spending was approximately 54.2 billion yuan, of which only about 1 billion was capitalized — a capitalization rate of just 1.8%; SAIC's capitalization rate was similarly near zero. The real outlier is GAC Group — its 2024 annual report shows that out of 7.5 billion yuan in R&D spending, the capitalization rate was as high as 80.83%, a ratio that has remained virtually unchanged since 2016. That same year, GAC's net profit was only 824 million yuan (down 81% year-over-year), and net profit excluding non-recurring items was a loss of 4.351 billion yuan. If GAC had expensed all R&D like BYD or SAIC, its reported profit would have deteriorated even further — the 80.83% capitalization rate provided substantial support to GAC's reported earnings. Within the same industry, differences in accounting treatment can be enormous.

Li's framing was a clever switch: packaging a US GAAP mandatory requirement as a voluntarily chosen "highest financial standard," implying that competitors were "beautifying" profits through capitalization. Factually, every sentence he said was correct. But what he didn't say was: this is what GAAP requires, not what he chose. This case is itself a lesson in "management narrative" — investors cannot just listen to what management says; they must understand the institutional logic behind it.

## What Constitutes Better Accounting Information

When the FASB made its "blanket expensing" decision in 1974, it was not without careful deliberation. Quite the opposite — there is an intellectual history worth examining behind this decision.

In the early 1970s in the United States, accounting treatment of R&D expenditures was all over the map. Some companies expensed everything, others capitalized heavily, and still others switched back and forth between years. This lack of consistency severely undermined the comparability of financial statements. The FASB listed R&D accounting as a priority issue from its inception, publishing a discussion memorandum in late 1973, holding a public hearing in March 1974, and receiving 74 position papers and 168 comment letters.

The core debate was a conflict between the matching principle and the prudence principle. Proponents of capitalization invoked the matching principle — the benefits of R&D investment are realized over future years, and concentrating all costs in the current period would systematically understate current profits for R&D-intensive companies. Proponents of expensing appealed to prudence — the future benefits of R&D projects are highly uncertain, and if capitalization were permitted, management would inevitably exploit this room to inflate profits.

The FASB ultimately chose the latter. In the Basis for Conclusions of SFAS 2, the Board acknowledged that R&D activities do generate future economic benefits but concluded that "there is a high degree of uncertainty about the future economic benefits of individual R&D projects," and that the accounting system cannot reliably distinguish which R&D expenditures will succeed and which will fail. Critics worried that blanket expensing would suppress corporate R&D investment, but subsequent empirical research failed to confirm this concern.

International accounting standards took a different path. In 1978, the IASC (predecessor to the IASB) issued IAS 9, which for the first time at the international level distinguished between research and development and allowed qualifying development expenditures to be capitalized. In 1998, IAS 38 replaced IAS 9 and further refined the six capitalization conditions. The Basis for Conclusions of IAS 38 argues that when a development project has crossed key milestones such as technical feasibility, the uncertainty of future economic benefits has been substantially reduced, and continuing to expense such outlays would fail to faithfully represent the entity's financial position — mandatory capitalization ensures the completeness of the balance sheet.

The deeper divergence between the two positions reflects different answers to the question: "What constitutes better accounting information?" The U.S. model pursues comparability and anti-manipulation — all companies treat R&D the same way, and investors don't need to guess whether management's judgments are reliable. International standards pursue faithful representation — a development project that has passed technical verification and a basic research effort still in the exploratory stage have different economic substance, and their accounting treatment should differ accordingly.

Interestingly, an interview study of buy-side and sell-side analysts under the IFRS system found that investors broadly support the principle of conditionally mandating capitalization of development expenditures and explicitly oppose U.S.-style blanket expensing. The analysts' core reasoning: the "development expenditures" line item after capitalization is itself an informationally valuable signal — it tells investors which projects management believes have crossed the threshold of technical feasibility, providing more decision-useful information than blanket expensing. However, other studies also show that actual capitalization under IAS 38 is not more value-relevant than full expensing, because the noise from earnings management offsets the informational gains.

Notably, in April 2024 the IASB officially launched a comprehensive review of IAS 38. In May 2025, the IASB identified two initial work streams: assessing investor demand for information about intangible assets (both recognized and unrecognized), and considering whether to update the definition of intangible assets and certain recognition criteria. The first test cases focus on software under cloud computing and agile development models — agile development features short iteration cycles and rapid version updates, making the traditional standard's determination of the "technical feasibility" point increasingly difficult to pin down. Whether AI and data resources should also be included in the testing scope is still under evaluation.

## The Battle of Interests Under Standards Flexibility

Under IFRS and Chinese standards, R&D capitalization provides management with a profit adjustment tool that is nearly impossible for outsiders to fully constrain. The mechanism is simple: expensing immediately hits current-period profit, while capitalization "moves" the same expenditure onto the balance sheet for gradual amortization in the future. Cash flow doesn't change; what changes is the allocation of profit across accounting periods.

This means management faces an ongoing temptation: expense everything generously during boom times when profits are ample; capitalize during downturns, using it as an earnings management toolkit to shift some expenses off the income statement. Hengrui's 2021 change in accounting estimates — timed precisely at the window of its first earnings decline — is difficult, from a timing perspective, not to associate with this kind of "counter-cyclical" usage.

In 2021, the Shenzhen Stock Exchange published a study based on A-share data from 2013 to 2019. The researchers divided companies into "suspect" and "non-suspect" groups based on earnings management motives — "suspect" meaning companies whose profits just barely exceeded zero, just barely exceeded the prior year, or just barely met analyst expectations, exhibiting obvious signs of "threshold-hugging." The study found that for non-suspect companies, the capitalization rate was positively correlated with R&D intensity — intuitive, since more R&D-intensive companies naturally have more projects entering the capitalizable stage. But for suspect companies, the capitalization rate had no correlation with R&D intensity, suggesting their capitalization decisions were driven more by profit targets. More crucially, the researchers used regression analysis to decompose stock prices into components including net assets, earnings, and capitalized R&D expenditures, examining each factor's explanatory power on stock prices. The results showed that the market priced the capitalized R&D of the two groups very differently: for non-suspect companies, capitalized R&D was assigned a positive valuation weight — the market recognized its signal of R&D progress; for suspect companies, the same line item was assigned a negative valuation weight — after identifying the suspicion of earnings management, the market applied a discount instead.

If profit adjustment via R&D expenses is a problem shared across international accounting standards, China's A-share market layers on two additional institutional factors.

The first is the incentive to "expand" R&D spending driven by High and New Technology Enterprise (HNTE) certification. Under the Administrative Measures for HNTE Certification, to obtain the preferential corporate income tax rate of 15% (versus the standard 25%), a company's R&D expenses as a percentage of revenue must meet minimum thresholds (no less than 3% for companies with annual revenue above 200 million yuan). The HNTE definition of R&D expenses includes both expensed and capitalized portions, so capitalization per se doesn't affect qualification. But the threshold motivates companies to classify as much spending as possible as "R&D" to meet the ratio — academic research has directly confirmed this: after the 2015 expansion of the super deduction policy's scope, a large number of companies with low innovation intent were also included, and adverse selection behavior of "padding R&D" increased significantly.

The second is the tax benefit of the R&D super deduction. Since 2023, for all qualifying companies outside the negative list, the R&D super deduction rate has been unified at 100% — for expenses not forming intangible assets, a 100% additional deduction is applied on top of the actual amount (i.e., 200% total pre-tax deduction in the current year); for expenses that form intangible assets, amortization is calculated at 200% of the intangible asset's cost. The total pre-tax deduction is the same either way: 200% of R&D spending. But the crucial difference is timing: under the expensing path, the full 200% deduction is taken in the current year; under the capitalization path, the 200% deduction is spread over future years (depending on the amortization period of the intangible asset). From a time-value-of-money perspective, expensing is more tax-efficient — saving 500,000 yuan in taxes this year versus saving it five years from now has very different cash flow implications. This creates a dilemma: expensing is better for tax savings, but capitalization makes the income statement look better. Companies must weigh "saving taxes" against "beautifying profits."

Multiple overlapping incentives — profit adjustment, R&D "expansion," and tax planning — make R&D capitalization on A-shares more complex than in most other markets. The cautious attitude during IPOs serves as a useful counter-reference: according to statistics from Lianhe Accounting Firm, among 852 A-share IPO companies from early 2021 to October 2022, only 33 had any capitalized R&D expenditures — less than 4%. The vast majority of IPO companies chose to expense everything — intermediaries and regulators generally believe that during the review stage, the audit risk from capitalization far outweighs its benefit to the income statement. If a listed company's R&D capitalization rate is far higher than the IPO standard in its industry, investors have good reason to ask: why did the company suddenly become so confident about its R&D projects after listing?

## Seeing Through the Gray Area: The Investor's Toolkit

For value investors, the treatment of R&D spending directly affects several core valuation variables.

First is earnings comparability. A company with a 50% capitalization rate compared to a peer at 0%, at the same level of R&D spending, will report significantly higher operating profit. This is not a difference in operating efficiency — it is purely a difference in accounting treatment.

Second is ROIC comparability. The treatment of R&D spending simultaneously affects both the numerator and denominator of ROIC — capitalization increases reported operating profit (larger numerator) while also increasing the invested capital base (larger denominator). The net effect depends on the growth rate of R&D spending: when R&D spending is continuously growing, capitalization tends to initially boost ROIC; when R&D spending slows or declines, heavy amortization of historically capitalized amounts can actually depress ROIC.

Aswath Damodaran, the renowned U.S. valuation scholar, holds a clear position on this issue: regardless of what accounting standards prescribe, R&D spending should be treated as capital expenditure for valuation purposes. His logic is that R&D is not an expense for maintaining current operations but an investment in creating future growth. In a series of papers and teaching materials, he details the adjustment method: remove current-year R&D spending from expenses and treat it as capital expenditure, while adding the unamortized balance of cumulative R&D spending from prior years back to the balance sheet as an asset (which he calls the "research asset"). This simultaneously changes operating profit (adding back current R&D spending, subtracting amortization) and invested capital (adding the research asset), making ROIC and reinvestment rate calculations more consistent. Amortization period assumptions vary by industry: typically 10 years for pharmaceutical companies, 3 years for software companies. Importantly, this adjustment does not fundamentally change free cash flow — because the increase in NOPAT exactly equals the increase in capital expenditure, and the two offset each other. What the adjustment changes is the decomposition of value creation, making ROIC and reinvestment rates more accurately reflect the company's true investment efficiency.

Baruch Lev, Professor of Accounting and Finance at New York University, has raised a similar critique from a more macro perspective. His co-authored book with Feng Gu, *The End of Accounting and the Path Forward for Investors and Managers* (2016, no official Chinese translation yet), is one of the sharpest academic critiques of the financial reporting system in recent years. Using extensive empirical data, Lev demonstrates that the correlation between GAAP financial information and stock prices has been declining continuously over the past 60 years. The core reason is that the economy has shifted from being driven by tangible assets to intangible assets — from 1977 to 2012, U.S. corporate investment in tangible capital declined by 35% while investment in intangible assets rose by 60% — yet accounting standards still treat R&D, brands, and human capital as expenses. The result is an increasingly incomplete balance sheet and diminishing reference value of financial statements for investors.

Facing this systemic gray area, rather than complaining about the flexibility of the standards, investors should build their own analytical framework.

**Peer comparison** is the most basic warning signal. Compare the target company's R&D capitalization rate against industry peers. If it is significantly above the industry average, investors should probe the specific capitalized projects, their development stages, and expected timelines for revenue contribution. Simply stating "it complies with accounting standards" is not enough — the standards provide a framework, not an answer.

**Track changes in capitalization rates over time.** If a company suddenly raises its rate sharply after years of stability, this signal warrants more vigilance than consistently high capitalization. "Counter-cyclical" accounting policy changes — expensing everything during booms, suddenly capitalizing during downturns — are a classic earnings management signal.

**Examine the stock and flow of the "development expenditures" line item.** On the balance sheet, "development expenditures" represent capitalized R&D that has not yet been transferred to intangible assets. If this item keeps swelling but few projects are ultimately converted to intangible assets or generate revenue, management's expectations for project success may be overly optimistic, and the risk of concentrated impairment in the future cannot be ignored. iFlytek's development expenditures ballooning from over 700 million to 2.2 billion is exactly this kind of signal.

**Be especially vigilant when comparing across standards.** As the NIO example illustrates, when you try to compare a US GAAP-reporting automaker (all R&D expensed) with a Chinese GAAP-reporting automaker (R&D partially capitalized), income statement differences may stem primarily from the standards framework rather than operating capability. Standardizing both companies' R&D as capital expenditure before comparing ROIC and growth rates is necessary to strip out standards noise and get back to business fundamentals.

**Finally, pay attention to footnote details when reading annual reports.** The timing judgments for R&D capitalization, project-level amounts and progress, and impairment testing methods for development expenditures — this information is typically disclosed in the "Intangible Assets" and "Development Expenditures" footnotes. If a company provides only aggregate capitalization totals without project-level detail, that itself is a signal of poor information quality.

## Conclusion

When the FASB made its "blanket expensing" decision in 1974, the U.S. economy was still an industrial economy dominated by manufacturing. Half a century later, the global economic landscape has been transformed beyond recognition. The core resources through which companies create value have shifted from plants and equipment to patents, algorithms, brands, and human capital — nearly all of which are excluded from the balance sheet by accounting standards.

The IASB's comprehensive review of IAS 38, launched in 2024, represents the most serious reconsideration of intangible asset accounting in half a century. But if history is any guide — the debate over goodwill amortization versus impairment has dragged on for twenty years without resolution, and lease accounting reform took thirty years to land — investors should not expect breakthrough changes in the near term.

Until the standards change, what investors can do is stay clear-headed: understand the accounting treatment differences in R&D spending, eliminate those differences when comparing companies, and adopt consistent methods in valuation. Schrödinger's profit must eventually collapse to a definite number, and the investor's task is to make their own independent "observation" — outside of management's.

Accounting is the language of business. But if this language permits two entirely different expressions for the same thing, investors must learn to translate — not just understanding what management said, but why they chose to say it that way.
