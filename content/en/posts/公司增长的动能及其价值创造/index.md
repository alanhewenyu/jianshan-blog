To understand the drivers of corporate growth, we must first examine whether the products or services a company offers are in a growing market or a mature market. Whether in a growing or mature market, the growth a company achieves is organic growth. Beyond organic growth, companies can also rapidly scale through mergers and acquisitions. These three distinct paths create different levels of value for a company.

## Growing Markets

A growing market is one that is expanding rapidly, where every company has the opportunity to achieve organic growth in its products or services simply by riding the overall market expansion. Such markets are often in sunrise industries -- the darlings of the market, attracting intense interest. For example, Huawei's launch of its 5G smartphones has created a tremendous catalytic effect across the supply chain, sending numerous related stocks soaring. Similarly, although the growth rate in the new energy vehicle sector has slowed somewhat and price wars have intensified, it remains a growing market where all participants still have opportunities for rapid growth.

As discussed in a previous article, revenue growth and operating profit margins are the core drivers determining a company's intrinsic value. Therefore, the growth expectations arising from an expanding market can create significant value for a company.

## Mature Markets

A mature market is one that is growing slowly or not at all -- this characterizes the vast majority of industries. In a mature market, a company can only grow by raising prices or gaining market share. But such growth comes at a cost. Raising prices comes at the expense of customer interests and may lead to customer attrition, while gaining market share encroaches on competitors' territory, typically provoking retaliatory actions such as price cuts to defend their positions. Consider today's online retail landscape: user-side traffic has peaked, and Pinduoduo has achieved sustained growth through its low-price strategy, forcing JD.com and Taobao to follow suit with their own price cuts to retain users.

Winning the battle is hard, but holding ground is harder. Competing in mature markets makes it extremely difficult to achieve profit growth and value creation. Revenue growth without profit growth -- where revenue rises but margins decline -- is a common outcome of competition in mature markets. Lasting value can only be created when smaller competitors are fully driven out of the market or when a company introduces differentiated products or services that competitors cannot easily replicate.

## Mergers and Acquisitions

When companies cannot achieve growth in mature markets and lack new products or services to tap into growing markets, many turn to M&A. For publicly listed companies, this is often a reluctant choice. After all, stock prices reflect expectations. If growth expectations evaporate, the stock price becomes difficult to sustain.

Compared to a company's internal or organic growth, M&A typically creates relatively little value. This is because the acquisition price already reflects the future cash flow value of the acquired assets, and buyers usually pay a premium -- i.e., goodwill.

Synergies are the most commonly cited rationale for corporate M&A. In fact, only synergies can create value in an acquisition. However, synergies are often little more than a fairy tale, with reality frequently falling short of expectations.

Buffett shared his views on acquisitions in his 1997 letter to shareholders:

> "In any acquisition, it never makes sense for the acquirer to pay a premium unless: (a) the acquirer's stock is overvalued relative to the acquiree's; or (b) the combined enterprise will generate higher earnings than the two entities would separately. Predictably, acquirers tend to insist on the second rationale, since few are willing to admit their stock is overvalued. Yet voracious acquirers -- those issuing stock as fast as a printing press -- implicitly concede this point. In some mergers, significant synergies do exist -- though the acquirer often overpays for them -- while in others, the anticipated cost and revenue benefits prove illusory. One thing, however, is certain: if a CEO is enthusiastic about a particularly foolish acquisition, his internal staff and outside advisors will produce whatever projections are needed to justify his position. Only in fairy tales are emperors told they are naked."

While Buffett's observations are primarily drawn from the U.S. market, the situation in China's A-share market is similar: post-acquisition performance deterioration and declining return on equity are far from uncommon among listed companies, and goodwill impairments are a frequent occurrence. Therefore, when evaluating a company's price-to-book ratio, a low P/B is not necessarily genuinely low. In such cases, it is important to check whether the company carries a significant goodwill balance. If there is substantial goodwill, a low P/B ratio may indicate that the market does not believe the goodwill is worth its stated value.
