*The Story of Accounting Standards (Part 8)*

Meituan just released its 2025 annual report, showing delivery service revenue of RMB 96.067 billion, a year-over-year decline of 2.0%. Meituan attributed the decrease "primarily to increased subsidies deducted from revenue."

This statement conceals an accounting judgment—should subsidies paid to users and riders be deducted from revenue, or reported separately as selling expenses? The two approaches produce identical profits, but revenue figures can differ dramatically. Here's how revenue recognition standards address this: if the subsidy constitutes "consideration payable to a customer" (money the company pays back to the customer to facilitate a transaction), it should be deducted from the transaction price. Only when the company receives a "distinct good or service" from the customer in return can it be treated as an expense. Meituan classified food delivery subsidies as the former, so the subsidy portion of each order is directly deducted from revenue.

But that's only the first fork in revenue recognition. A closer look at the notes to the annual report reveals that Meituan's food delivery business uses the "gross method"—the full delivery fee charged to consumers is recorded as revenue, while payments to riders go into cost of sales. The in-store group-buying business uses a different approach—when you buy a RMB 100 voucher for a restaurant on Meituan, Meituan takes a few yuan in commission and records only that commission as revenue. The RMB 100 you pay to the restaurant never appears on Meituan's income statement. This is called the "net method."

Two business lines, two accounting treatments—the difference isn't about industry convention but about judging "who bears primary responsibility to the customer." In food delivery, Meituan assumes primary responsibility for order dispatching, rider management, and fulfillment guarantees, so it recognizes revenue on a gross basis. For the in-store business, Meituan merely connects consumers to restaurants; the dining service is provided by the restaurant. Meituan neither controls the food nor bears responsibility for dining quality—it is an agent, not the principal, so it recognizes revenue on a net basis.

If Meituan treated food delivery on a net basis too, most of that RMB 96.1 billion would vanish from the "revenue" line. Profits wouldn't change (costs would disappear correspondingly), but the scale of revenue would shrink dramatically. Price-to-sales-based valuations would shift accordingly.

Within a single company, we see subsidies deducted from revenue, plus both the gross and net methods coexisting—three accounting judgments collectively determine the height of Meituan's revenue curve. They all point to the most fundamental question: what is revenue?

This seemingly simple accounting question is actually the deepest quagmire in the world of financial reporting. Revenue is the first line of the income statement, the most important input to valuation models, and the first number investors look at—yet precisely this number lacked a unified definition for decades.

Before IFRS 15 and ASC 606 were issued in May 2014, the global understanding of "what counts as revenue and when to count it" was all over the map. In the U.S. standards system alone, there were over 100 rules on revenue recognition, scattered across different industry guidance—one set for telecom, another for software, yet another for construction. International standards (IAS 18 and IAS 11) took a different route: a few principle-based statements that left enormous judgment to individual companies. Business is inherently complex, and principle-based guidance isn't a flaw per se. But when the same transaction can yield completely different revenue figures under different standards and industry guidance, cross-company financial comparisons become nearly impossible.

The chaos of revenue recognition also had a more direct consequence: it became the most common vehicle for financial fraud. SEC statistics show that over 40% of accounting enforcement actions involved improper revenue recognition, ranking first among all fraud categories in 8 out of the dozen-plus years studied.

Against this backdrop, in 2002, the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) added revenue recognition to its agenda, explicitly noting that "revenue recognition issues are the leading cause of financial statement restatements." That same year, the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) joined in, and the two most important standard-setting bodies in the world launched a joint initiative. Twelve years later, in May 2014, IFRS 15 and ASC 606 were issued simultaneously—the largest unification effort in accounting standards history, taking effect in 2018. In July 2017, China's Ministry of Finance issued a converged version: the revised Accounting Standard for Business Enterprises No. 14—Revenue.

This new set of rules accomplished three things, each of which changed the face of corporate financial statements.

## One Ruler for All Businesses

The first major achievement of the new standard was replacing the old "multi-track system" with a single unified framework.

In the old world, if you were a company selling phones, revenue recognition followed the rules for "sale of goods." If you were a construction company building for others, you followed the rules for "construction contracts"—the well-known "percentage of completion method," recognizing revenue as construction progressed.

But real-world business models come in endless varieties, and revenue recognition is often far from straightforward. Is a real estate developer "selling a house" or "building a house"? When a shipbuilder takes an order for a custom-built freighter, is it "selling a ship" or "building a ship"? Different answers lead to completely different timing and amounts of revenue recognition. Under the old standards, these boundary questions frequently sparked disputes.

The new standard unified everything with a single question: when does the customer obtain "control"?

Control means: the customer can direct the use of the good or service and obtain substantially all of its economic benefits. If the customer progressively obtains control while you're working—say, you're building a custom factory on the customer's land, and halfway through the customer already owns that half-built structure—then you can recognize revenue as you go. If the customer obtains control only when you've completed everything and formally delivered—say, you're producing a batch of standardized products in your own factory—then you can only recognize revenue at the point of delivery.

No more distinguishing whether a transaction is a sale of goods or a construction project. No more separate rules by industry. One ruler for all businesses. This represents a fundamental conceptual shift: the old standard asked "have the risks and rewards been transferred?" while the new standard asks "has control been transferred?" It sounds like just different wording, but in many scenarios the two questions yield different answers. For example, a ship custom-built to a client's specifications: under the old standard, revenue recognition had to wait until the ship was built, delivered, and risks transferred. Under the new standard, if the contract terms meet certain conditions—the ship has no alternative use, and the entity has an enforceable right to payment for work completed to date—revenue can be recognized progressively during construction. The same transaction, but the timing of revenue recognition could differ by several years.

## Multiple Promises Hidden in One Contract

The second major achievement of the new standard was requiring companies to unbundle different promises within a single contract and recognize revenue for each separately. The standard calls these promises "performance obligations"—essentially, each distinct thing you've committed to deliver to the customer.

The most intuitive example comes from the telecom industry. You walk into a carrier's store and sign up for a two-year plan, and the carrier "gives" you a phone. Under the old standard, the phone was typically treated as a marketing cost—the carrier recognized all plan revenue evenly over the two-year service period, absorbing the phone cost within it.

The new standard says: that's wrong. You've made two promises to the customer—to give them a phone and to provide two years of telecom service. The total contract price must be allocated based on the standalone selling prices of the phone and the telecom service, respectively. The result: a portion of revenue that was previously spread over two years now must be recognized in the month the phone is delivered. The carrier's revenue curve changes: more recognized early in the contract, less later.

Behind this lies a deeper issue. The phone might sell for RMB 4,000 standalone, but it shows as RMB 0 in the bundled plan. The telecom service might be RMB 80/month without a bundle but RMB 120/month with one. The new standard requires allocation based on "standalone selling prices," but often these aren't directly observable and must be estimated. Estimation means judgment, and judgment means different companies can make different choices.

## When a Price Hides a Loan

The third major achievement of the new standard was addressing financing arrangements embedded in contracts.

An equipment manufacturer sells a RMB 1 million machine and allows the customer to pay over three years, interest-free. On the surface, this is a RMB 1 million sale. But in reality, the manufacturer has effectively provided the customer with a three-year interest-free loan. Part of that RMB 1 million "price" is actually implicit interest on this hidden loan—if the customer paid cash upfront, the manufacturer might only ask for RMB 930,000.

The new standard requires separating these two elements: equipment revenue is recognized at its present value of RMB 930,000, while the remaining RMB 70,000 is recognized as interest income over three years. The reverse applies too. If a customer prepays three years of service fees upfront, the company receives money early—the customer is effectively providing financing to the company. The recognized revenue should be less than the cash received, with the difference treated as a financing cost.

This might sound like accountants making trouble for themselves. But it solves a real comparability problem. Two companies sell the same thing—one requires cash payment, the other allows three-year installments. Without adjustment, the latter's "revenue" would be inflated because its price includes financing consideration. Stripping out the financing component reveals the true sales revenue.

## The Gray Areas

No matter how detailed the standard's framework, when applied to specific businesses, there will always be room for management judgment—this is an objective reflection of business complexity itself, not a flaw in the standards. For investors, the key isn't to understand every rule, but to know where the judgment spaces lie and how to spot when someone is exploiting them.

**Channel Stuffing: Profit Manipulation Through Timing**

A consumer goods company pushes large volumes of product to distributors every December—offering bigger discounts, more lenient payment terms, even promising to accept returns for unsold goods. From a standards perspective, as long as control of the goods has transferred to the distributor, revenue can be recognized. But if the distributor has no real end-customer demand, these goods will eventually be returned or become dead stock—this year's "growth" is merely borrowing from next year's sales.

China's baijiu (liquor) industry played out a delayed version of this story. Since the real estate crisis in 2022, domestic consumer demand has remained relatively weak, yet baijiu companies' financial performance lagged behind—in 2024, the 20 A-share listed baijiu companies still reported combined revenue growth of about 7% year-over-year, with most reporting growth through the first half of 2024. The reason: the pressure from slowing retail sales was absorbed along the distribution chain. Distilleries continued shipping to distributors and recognizing revenue as usual; distributors bore the burden of unsold inventory. It wasn't until distributors' cash flows became strained, prices inverted, and channel inventory piled up beyond capacity that the reality at the retail level finally flooded back into the baijiu companies' financial statements.

The truth emerged in force in 2025. A joint report by the China Alcoholic Drinks Association and KPMG showed that in the first half of 2025, over 58% of distributors reported that inventory was still increasing, industry-average inventory turnover days had reached 900, and more than half of distributors faced price inversion—forced to sell at a loss to recover cash. During the same period, 14 out of 20 A-share listed baijiu companies saw revenue decline, with industry-wide profits falling approximately 11% year-over-year. Among the steepest declines: Jiugui Liquor reported first-half revenue of RMB 561 million (down 43.54% YoY) with net profit attributable to shareholders of just RMB 8.95 million (down 92.6%); Swellfun reported revenue of RMB 1.498 billion (down 12.84%) with net profit falling 56.5%. Yanghe Distillery, once ranked among the industry's top three, saw Q2 standalone revenue drop 43.7% YoY—the steepest decline among leading liquor companies. Its chairman, Zhang Liandong, resigned in July 2025 before his term was up.

Under pressure, companies that had previously pursued aggressive channel strategies began publicly correcting course. Yanghe Distillery explicitly made "destocking, building momentum, and stabilizing prices" its strategic priority in 2025, proactively reducing shipments to distributors. Luzhou Laojiao, at its year-end 2024 distributor conference, encouraged distributors to focus on profits rather than volume, emphasizing genuine consumer cultivation over mere channel stuffing. These statements were themselves a belated admission—the seemingly steady growth in financial reports over the past several years did include a substantial portion absorbed by channel inventory.

But baijiu companies' channel pressure doesn't necessarily show up first in accounts receivable. The baijiu industry operates on a typical "payment before delivery" model: distributors pay first, then distilleries ship. On the balance sheet, this shows up as an increase in "contract liabilities" (commonly known as advance receipts). So the warning signal for the baijiu industry is actually the reverse—a decline in contract liabilities. Yanghe Distillery's contract liabilities dropped from approximately RMB 15.8 billion at the end of 2021 to about RMB 5 billion by the end of Q3 2024—shrinking by nearly two-thirds in less than three years. Long before the dismal H1 2025 report was released, the contract liability curve had been quietly foretelling the future.

For "delivery before payment" businesses, the warning signal appears in accounts receivable. Dong-E-E-Jiao provides a recent example. In Q1 2025, the company reported an 18% year-over-year revenue increase—looking fine on the surface. But accounts receivable surged 583%, ballooning from under RMB 100 million to RMB 541 million; operating cash flow turned negative at minus RMB 304 million. The company explained it had extended credit terms for strategic customers—in other words, goods were shipped, but cash hadn't come back. After the annual report was released, the stock hit the daily limit down.

Dong-E-E-Jiao is no stranger to this situation. Between 2012 and 2018, the company raised its wholesale price for ejiao (donkey-hide gelatin) year after year, and distributors stockpiled heavily. In 2019, severe channel inventory buildup led to a 59.68% revenue collapse and a loss of RMB 455 million. It then took three years to clear channel inventory and resume growth. History is rhyming.

Two models, two detection methods. But the underlying logic is the same: is revenue growth corroborated by cash flow and channel indicators? If not, the "growth" may simply be goods moving from the company's warehouse to the distributor's warehouse—disappearing from the distillery's financial statements but not from the industry's total inventory.

**Construction Contracts: Completion Progress Is Hard to Measure Accurately**

If channel stuffing is the primary risk for point-in-time recognition, construction contracts are the disaster zone for over-time recognition.

Under the new standard, qualifying construction contracts can recognize revenue based on progress toward completion, typically measured by the ratio of costs incurred to estimated total costs. The problem is that "estimated total costs" is itself a management estimate. Underestimating total costs inflates the completion percentage for a given level of costs incurred, causing revenue to be recognized prematurely. Moreover, this bias is hard to detect—investors see only a percentage figure in the annual report and can hardly judge whether it's reasonable.

The new standard introduced two related balance sheet items that happen to serve as cross-checks: contract assets and accounts receivable. Both correspond to revenue already recognized, differing in the strength of the right. Accounts receivable represents the portion where the company has invoiced the customer and holds an unconditional right to payment. Contract assets represent the portion where revenue has been recognized but unconditional payment rights haven't yet been obtained (perhaps the contractual billing milestone hasn't been reached, or a certain milestone must first be met). During the execution of a construction contract, recognized revenue first sits in contract assets, then transfers to accounts receivable once the contractual billing milestone is reached.

For a healthy construction-type company, contract assets, accounts receivable, and operating cash flow should grow roughly in sync—revenue recognition, invoicing, and cash collection forming a relatively stable chain. There are two levels of warning signs in this chain. First level: accounts receivable growing significantly faster than revenue, or operating cash flow significantly lagging behind revenue—meaning invoices have been issued but customers aren't paying on time. Second level (more alarming): contract assets significantly outpacing accounts receivable—meaning revenue recognition is running ahead of even the invoicing milestone, and unconditional payment rights haven't yet been obtained. When this happens, either progress estimates are overly optimistic or there are problems with how contract milestones are being assessed. Either way, the distance between revenue and cash is being stretched further.

**Are You Selling or Helping Others Sell: A Question Standards Are Still Debating**

Returning to Meituan's story from the beginning. One of the most difficult judgments in the new standard is "principal vs. agent"—are you "directly providing" the good or service, or "arranging for someone else to provide it"? The former uses the gross method, the latter the net method. Profits are identical, but revenue can differ by several multiples. Meituan's food delivery and in-store businesses using opposite treatments within the same financial report is a real-world illustration of this judgment.

The standard's criterion is: before the good or service is transferred to the customer, did you obtain control over it? Do you bear primary fulfillment responsibility, inventory risk, and pricing discretion?

For physical goods, this judgment is usually straightforward—you purchased inventory, stored it, set the price, and bore the risk of it not selling. Then you're the principal. But when the subject of the transaction shifts from goods to services, things get complicated. In its 2024 post-implementation review, the IASB admitted that determining who "controls" a service is far more difficult than determining who controls a physical good—services have no physical form, and the concept of "controlling a service that hasn't yet been provided before it's transferred to the customer" is inherently abstract.

The greater challenge comes from the platform economy. These business models hadn't emerged at scale during the decade-plus when IFRS 15 was being developed, and standard-setters couldn't fully anticipate them. The IASB also acknowledged that difficulties mainly arise in new business models that emerged after IFRS 15 was published.

The challenges that platform economics pose to the standard go beyond "principal vs. agent." As mentioned at the beginning, Meituan deducts food delivery subsidies from revenue, which involves another IFRS 15 judgment: what constitutes "consideration payable to a customer"? When a platform both charges and subsidizes consumers, and sometimes also gives rebates to merchants, the direction and nature of each payment must be assessed individually. Two platform companies with similar business scale may report "revenue" figures differing by an order of magnitude simply because of slight differences in how subsidies are treated (deducted from revenue vs. classified as selling expenses).

In September 2024, the IASB completed the post-implementation review of IFRS 15, confirming that the standard is generally working well, but identified "principal vs. agent" as the foremost application challenge, to be included in the next agenda consultation. The IASB itself was divided—some members favored more specific application guidance, others thought enhanced disclosure requirements would suffice, and still others believed the current principle-based framework needed no changes. The issue was classified as "medium priority"—acknowledging the problem exists but without having reached a solution.

For investors, the conclusion is practical: when you see a platform company's announced revenue figure, your first move should be to open the notes to the annual report and confirm whether it's gross or net, and how subsidies are treated. If two companies use different methods, any valuation comparison based on revenue multiples is meaningless.

## Evergrande and Regulation

In July 2017, China's Ministry of Finance issued the revised revenue standard (new CAS 14), maintaining a high degree of convergence with IFRS 15. The five-step model, transfer of control, unbundling of performance obligations—the core framework was entirely consistent.

The new standard impacted every industry in China. Real estate deserves particular attention—it is directly linked to the largest revenue fraud case in China's capital markets.

You buy a pre-sale apartment, sign the contract, and pay the down payment. The developer starts building. When can the developer recognize this money as revenue? The key lies in the new standard's criteria—whether the property being built has "no alternative use" and whether the developer has an enforceable right to payment for work completed to date. Under China's pre-sale system, contract terms typically stipulate that if the buyer defaults and cancels, the developer can only retain the deposit. In other words, the developer doesn't have an enforceable right to payment for the portion already built—this fails one of the core conditions for "over-time" recognition under the new standard. Based on this judgment, the vast majority of A-share real estate companies adopted "point-in-time" recognition—revenue is recognized only when keys are handed over. Before delivery, all pre-sale proceeds are recorded under "contract liabilities"—liabilities, not revenue.

It was precisely this rule that made the following story possible.

In May 2024, the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) issued an administrative penalty decision against Evergrande Real Estate, uncovering the largest-ever revenue fraud in Chinese real estate history. The CSRC determined that Evergrande inflated revenue by RMB 213.989 billion in 2019, accounting for 50.14% of that year's operating revenue, with corresponding inflated profits of RMB 40.722 billion; and inflated revenue by RMB 350.157 billion in 2020, accounting for 78.54% of that year's operating revenue, with corresponding inflated profits of RMB 51.289 billion. Over two years combined, revenue was inflated by RMB 564.1 billion and profits by RMB 92 billion.

The CSRC penalty decision provided more details on the specific fraud methods: unlike pure fabrication of business activities, Evergrande Real Estate violated accounting standards by "inflating financial statements through premature revenue recognition and similar methods, borrowing against future revenue." In execution, this was jointly carried out by the headquarters and project companies, "employing methods such as modifying and adjusting the year's delivery lists and altering delivery dates in the Mingyuan system to fabricate material facts." The Mingyuan system is business management software widely used in China's real estate industry, recording the entire process of sales, contracts, deliveries, and payments—it's also the primary source from which auditors extract delivery evidence. Evergrande had been using the same "point-in-time" recognition method as most A-share real estate companies—recognizing revenue upon delivery. But by tampering with delivery lists and delivery dates in the system, properties that hadn't actually been delivered were shown as "already delivered," and the corresponding pre-sale proceeds were transferred from contract liabilities to operating revenue.

This wasn't exploiting a gray area in the standards or making aggressive accounting policy choices. This was breaching the standard's boundaries by fabricating business facts when revenue recognition conditions weren't met—outright fraud, not a matter of judgment.

Xu Jiayin (Hui Ka Yan) was found to have "decided on and organized the implementation of financial fraud, with particularly egregious methods." He was permanently barred from the securities market and fined RMB 47 million. Evergrande Real Estate was fined RMB 4.175 billion—the maximum penalty under the new Securities Law for fraudulent issuance. PricewaterhouseCoopers, as auditor, was found to have "known that Evergrande Real Estate prematurely recognized revenue in 2020 and failed to flag it." PwC was fined a total of RMB 441 million by the Ministry of Finance and CSRC, suspended from operations for six months, its Guangzhou office was revoked, and four signing CPAs had their licenses revoked.

As for revenue fraud more broadly, the Chinese Institute of Certified Public Accountants (CICPA) issued a comprehensive checklist in its 2024 annual audit work notice. The CICPA requires auditors to work "based on the presumption that fraud risk exists in revenue recognition," specifically focusing on whether revenue changes align with market trends and cash flows, whether special payment arrangements and return conditions exist, whether principal and agent roles in contracts are properly distinguished, whether the conditions for over-time recognition are truly met, and the risk of "empty circulation" fraud in supply chain finance and financing-related trade. Auditing standards have gone so far as to codify "the presumption that fraud risk exists in revenue"—investors reading annual reports would do well to exercise the same vigilance.

## How Investors Should Read Revenue

The revenue recognition policy disclosure in the notes to annual reports is the starting point for understanding a company's financial reporting quality. Differences in choices between companies in the same industry often reveal management's degree of aggressiveness. Is revenue recognized over time or at a point in time? How are performance obligations unbundled? Are subsidies and rebates deducted from revenue or classified as expenses? If a company's choices consistently lean toward "recognizing more revenue sooner," that's worth at least one more "why."

The alignment between revenue and cash flow is the most fundamental and reliable verification tool. But which specific line item to watch depends on the business model: for "payment before delivery" companies (such as baijiu, certain software subscriptions, prepaid services), watch changes in contract liabilities—declining advance receipts often precede revenue declines. For "delivery before payment" companies (such as pharmaceuticals, consumer goods, manufacturing), watch whether accounts receivable growth exceeds revenue growth, and changes in accounts receivable turnover days. For construction-type companies, also watch contract assets—the portion where revenue has been recognized but invoicing milestones haven't been reached. Regardless of the model, everything ultimately comes back to operating cash flow: if "cash received from selling goods and providing services" consistently lags behind revenue growth, that widening gap is the strongest warning signal.

When comparing platform companies, the first step is confirming whether their revenue is recorded using the gross or net method. If two companies use different methods, revenue multiple comparisons are meaningless.

## Conclusion

The story of revenue recognition standards is, at its core, a story about "when can you say you've earned it."

There's a common interpretation of this standards transition within the industry: the old standards leaned toward the seller's perspective—you shipped the goods, risks transferred, so it counts as earned. The new standards shift to the buyer's perspective—only when the buyer has actually received it, can use it, and controls it, does it count as your earnings. This shift in perspective brought many previously overlooked details to the surface: how many promises are actually in a single contract? Is there financing hidden in the price? Are you selling yourself or helping someone else sell?

IFRS 15 took 12 years to develop and passed its post-implementation review in 2024, deemed to be "operating as intended." But standard-setters also acknowledged that the "principal vs. agent" judgment remains the most prominent application challenge—particularly in service transactions and new platform economies, where the boundaries of "control" are still being explored. Evergrande Real Estate's RMB 564.1 billion in inflated revenue reminds us from a different angle: standards define the correct way to record transactions, but they can't stop someone from circumventing rules by fabricating business facts. Standards aren't omnipotent—they can govern "how to record the accounts" but can't govern "whether the events actually occurred." The latter is the job of auditing, internal controls, and regulators.

For investors, no matter how standards change, one principle remains constant: look at the substance of financial statements, not just the surface. The number on the first line of the income statement is the surface. Whether there's real cash flowing in behind it, whether receivables and contract assets are reasonable, whether customers have a sustainable willingness to pay—that's the substance. A set of financial statements with all surface and no substance will eventually make investors pay back what they thought they earned.
