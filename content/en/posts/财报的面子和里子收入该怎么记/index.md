*The Story of Accounting Standards (Part 8)*

Meituan just released its 2025 annual report, showing delivery service revenue of RMB 96.067 billion, a year-over-year decline of 2.0%. Meituan attributed the decline to "primarily the increase in subsidies deducted from revenue."

This statement implies an accounting judgment — should subsidies paid to users and riders be deducted from revenue, or recorded separately as selling expenses? The two treatments produce exactly the same profit, but can yield very different revenue figures. Here's how the revenue recognition standards approach the question: if a subsidy constitutes "consideration payable to a customer" (money the company pays back to a customer to facilitate a transaction), it should be deducted from the transaction price. Only when the company receives a "distinct good or service" in return from the customer can it be treated as an expense. Meituan classified its food delivery subsidies as the former, so the portion of each order used for subsidies is directly deducted from revenue.

But that's only the first fork in the road of revenue recognition. A closer look at the notes to the annual report reveals that Meituan's food delivery business uses the "gross method" — the full delivery fee charged to consumers is recorded as revenue, while payments to riders go into cost of sales. Its in-store group-buying business uses a different approach — when you buy a RMB 100 dining voucher on Meituan, Meituan takes a few yuan in commission and records only that commission as revenue. The RMB 100 you pay to the restaurant never appears on Meituan's income statement. This is called the "net method."

Two business lines, two recording methods — the difference lies not in industry convention, but in the judgment of "who bears primary responsibility to the customer." In food delivery, Meituan assumes primary responsibilities including order dispatch, rider management, and fulfillment guarantees, so revenue is recognized on a gross basis. In the in-store business, Meituan merely directs consumers to restaurants; the dining service is provided by the restaurant. Meituan neither controls the food nor bears responsibility for dining quality — it is a facilitator, not the principal, so revenue is recognized on a net basis.

If Meituan applied the net method to food delivery as well, most of the RMB 96.1 billion would vanish from the "revenue" line. Profit wouldn't change (costs would disappear correspondingly), but revenue would shrink dramatically. Valuations based on price-to-sales ratios would shift accordingly.

Within a single company, we see subsidies deducted from revenue, plus both gross and net methods coexisting — three accounting judgments collectively determine the height of Meituan's revenue curve. They point to the most fundamental question: what is revenue?

This seemingly simplest of accounting questions is actually the deepest quagmire in the world of financial reporting. Revenue is the first line of the income statement, the most important input to valuation models, and the first number investors look at — yet this is precisely the number that, for decades, lacked a unified definition.

Before IFRS 15 and ASC 606 were issued in May 2014, the global understanding of "what counts as revenue and when to recognize it" was all over the map. In the U.S. standards system alone, there were over 100 rules on revenue recognition, scattered across industry-specific guidance — one set for telecom, another for software, yet another for construction. International standards (IAS 18 and IAS 11) took a different path: primarily principle-based statements with broad language, leaving substantial judgment to individual companies. Business itself is complex, and principle-based guidance isn't inherently flawed. But when different standards and industry guides allow the same transaction to produce entirely different revenue figures, financial data becomes difficult to compare across companies.

The chaos of revenue recognition also had a more direct consequence: it became the most common vehicle for financial fraud. SEC statistics showed that over 40% of accounting enforcement actions involved improper revenue recognition, ranking first among all types of fraud in 8 out of the roughly dozen years observed.

It was against this backdrop that, in 2002, the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) placed revenue recognition on its agenda, explicitly noting that "revenue recognition issues are the leading cause of financial statement restatements." That same year, the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) joined, and the two most important standard-setting bodies in the world began a joint effort. Twelve years later, in May 2014, IFRS 15 and ASC 606 were issued simultaneously — the largest unification project in the history of accounting standards, taking effect in 2018. In July 2017, China's Ministry of Finance issued the converged new *Accounting Standards for Business Enterprises No. 14 — Revenue*.

This new set of rules accomplished three things, each of which changed the face of corporate financial reporting.

## One Ruler to Measure All Businesses

The first major achievement of the new standards was replacing the old "multi-track system" with a single unified framework.

In the old world, if you were a company selling phones, revenue recognition followed the rules for "sale of goods." If you were a construction company building for someone, you followed "construction contract" rules — the commonly known "percentage of completion method," recognizing revenue as construction progressed.

But real-world business models are endlessly varied, and revenue recognition is often far from straightforward. Is a real estate developer "selling houses" or "building houses"? When a shipbuilder takes an order for a custom-designed cargo vessel, is it "selling a ship" or "building a ship"? Different answers lead to entirely different timing and amounts of revenue recognition. Under the old standards, these boundary issues frequently sparked disputes.

The new standards unified everything with a single question: when does the customer obtain "control"?

Control means the customer can direct the use of the good or service and obtain substantially all its economic benefits. If the customer progressively obtains control as you perform — for example, you're building a custom factory on the customer's land, and halfway through the customer already owns that half-built structure — then you can recognize revenue as you go. If the customer obtains control only when you finish everything and formally deliver — for example, you're producing a batch of standardized products in your own factory — then you can only recognize revenue at the point of delivery.

No more distinguishing whether a transaction involves selling goods or performing construction. No more industry-specific rules. One ruler to measure all businesses. This was a fundamental conceptual shift: the old standards asked "have the risks and rewards transferred?" while the new standards ask "has control transferred?" It sounds like merely different wording, but in many scenarios the two questions yield different answers. For instance, a ship custom-built to a client's specifications — under the old standards, you'd wait until the ship was completed and delivered for risk to transfer before recognizing revenue. Under the new standards, if contract terms meet the criteria — such as the vessel having no alternative use and the company having an enforceable right to payment for work completed — revenue can be recognized progressively as construction proceeds. For the same transaction, the timing of revenue recognition could differ by several years.

## Multiple Promises Hidden in a Single Contract

The second major achievement of the new standards was requiring companies to unbundle the different promises within a single contract and recognize revenue for each separately. The standards call these promises "performance obligations" — essentially each distinct thing you've promised to do for the customer.

The most intuitive example comes from the telecom industry. You visit a carrier's store and sign up for a two-year plan, and the carrier "gives" you a phone. Under the old standards, the phone was typically treated as a marketing cost — the carrier recognized the full plan revenue evenly over the two-year service period, with the phone cost absorbed within it.

The new standards say: that's not right. You've made two promises to the customer — to give them a phone and to provide two years of communication services. The total contract price must be allocated based on the relative standalone selling prices of the phone and the communication services. The result is that a portion of revenue previously spread over two years must now be recognized in the month the phone is delivered. The carrier's revenue curve changes: more recognized at the start of the contract, less later.

Behind this lies a deeper issue. The phone might sell standalone for RMB 4,000, but shows as RMB 0 in the bundle; the communication service might cost RMB 80 per month without a bundle but RMB 120 per month with one. The new standards require allocation based on "standalone selling prices," but in many cases standalone prices aren't directly observable and must be estimated by the company. Estimation means judgment, and judgment means different companies can make different choices.

## When a Loan Is Hidden in the Price

The third major achievement of the new standards was addressing financing arrangements embedded in contracts.

An equipment manufacturer sells a RMB 1 million machine and allows the customer to pay over three years, interest-free. On the surface, this is a RMB 1 million sale. But in reality, the manufacturer has effectively given the customer a three-year interest-free loan. Part of the RMB 1 million "price" is actually interest on this implicit loan — if the customer had paid cash on the spot, the manufacturer might have asked for only RMB 930,000.

The new standards require separating these two elements: equipment revenue is recognized at the present value of RMB 930,000, and the remaining RMB 70,000 is gradually recognized as interest income over three years. The reverse applies too. If a customer prepays three years of service fees in one lump sum, the company gets the money early, meaning the customer is effectively providing financing to the company — the recognized revenue should be less than the cash received, with the difference treated as a financing cost.

This sounds like accountants making extra work for themselves. But it solves a real comparability problem. Two companies sell the same thing: one requires cash payment, the other allows three-year installments — without adjustment, the latter's "revenue" would be inflated because its price includes consideration for financing. Stripping out the financing component is the only way to see the true sales revenue.

## Gray Areas

No matter how detailed the standards framework, when applied to specific businesses, there will always be areas requiring management judgment — this is an objective reflection of the inherent complexity of business, not a flaw in the standards. For investors, the key isn't to understand every standard, but to know where the judgment areas are and how to identify when someone is exploiting them.

**Channel Stuffing: Profit Manipulation by Borrowing from the Future**

A consumer goods company ships large quantities to distributors every December — offering bigger discounts, more lenient payment terms, even promising returns for unsold inventory. From a standards perspective, as long as control of the goods has transferred to the distributor, revenue can be recognized. But if the distributor has no real end-consumer demand, those goods will eventually be returned or become dead stock — this year's "growth" is merely borrowing next year's sales.

China's baijiu (Chinese liquor) industry played out a delayed version of this story. Since the real estate crisis began in 2022, overall domestic consumer demand has been subdued, yet listed baijiu companies' performance lagged — in 2024, the combined revenue of 20 A-share listed baijiu companies still grew approximately 7% year-over-year, with most reporting growth through 2023 and the first half of 2024. The reason: the pressure from slowing retail sell-through was absorbed along the distribution chain. Distilleries continued shipping to distributors and recognizing revenue; distributors shouldered the unsold inventory. It wasn't until distributors' cash flow began tightening, prices inverted, and channel inventory piled up beyond capacity that the truth at the retail level finally flooded back into the baijiu companies' financial statements.

The truth came to a head in 2025. A joint report by the China Alcoholic Drinks Association and KPMG showed that in the first half of 2025, over 58% of distributors reported that inventory was still increasing, the industry's average inventory turnover days had reached 900, and more than half of distributors were experiencing price inversion — forced to sell at a loss to recover cash. During the same period, 14 of the 20 A-share listed baijiu companies saw revenue decline, with industry-wide profits falling approximately 11% year-over-year. The steepest declines: Jiugui Liquor posted first-half revenue of RMB 561 million (down 43.54% YoY), with net profit attributable to parent company shareholders of just RMB 8.95 million (down 92.6%); Swellfun reported revenue of RMB 1.498 billion (down 12.84%), with net profit falling 56.5%. Yanghe Distillery, once ranked among the industry's top three, saw second-quarter revenue plunge 43.7% YoY — the steepest drop among leading baijiu companies — and its chairman Zhang Liandong resigned in July 2025 before his term expired.

Under pressure, baijiu companies that had previously pursued aggressive channel strategies began publicly course-correcting. Yanghe Distillery explicitly made "destocking, building momentum, and stabilizing prices" its strategic priority in 2025, proactively reducing shipments to distributors. Luzhou Laojiao, at its year-end 2024 distributor conference, encouraged distributors to focus on profits rather than volume, emphasizing genuine consumer cultivation over mere channel stuffing. These statements were themselves a belated admission — a significant portion of the seemingly steady growth on financial statements over the past few years had indeed been absorbed by channel inventory.

However, the channel pressure on baijiu companies doesn't necessarily show up first in accounts receivable. The baijiu industry operates on a "payment before delivery" model: distributors pay first, then the distillery ships, which appears on the balance sheet as an increase in "contract liabilities" (commonly known as advances from customers). So the warning signal for the baijiu industry is exactly the reverse — a decline in contract liabilities. Yanghe Distillery's contract liabilities fell from approximately RMB 15.8 billion at the end of 2021 to approximately RMB 5 billion by the end of Q3 2024, shrinking by nearly two-thirds in less than three years. Long before that dismal first-half 2025 report was released, the contract liability curve had been quietly foretelling the future.

For companies operating on a "delivery before payment" model, the warning signal shows up in accounts receivable instead. Dong-E-E-Jiao provides a recent example. In Q1 2025, the company reported 18% revenue growth year-over-year — looking solid. But accounts receivable surged 583%, ballooning from under RMB 100 million to RMB 541 million; operating cash flow turned negative at RMB -304 million. The company's explanation was that it had extended credit terms for strategic customers — in other words, goods were shipped but cash hadn't been collected. After the annual report was released, the stock hit its daily limit down.

Dong-E-E-Jiao is no stranger to this situation. Between 2012 and 2018, the company raised its donkey-hide gelatin factory prices year after year, and distributors stockpiled heavily. In 2019, severe channel inventory buildup led to a 59.68% revenue collapse and a loss of RMB 455 million, and it took three years to clear channel inventory and resume growth. History is rhyming.

Two models, two ways to detect problems. But the underlying logic is the same: is revenue growth corroborated by cash flow and channel indicators? If not, then "growth" may simply mean goods were moved from the company's warehouse to the distributor's warehouse — disappearing from the distillery's financial statements but not from the industry's total inventory.

**Construction Contracts: Percentage of Completion Is Hard to Measure Accurately**

If channel stuffing is the primary risk in point-in-time recognition, construction contracts are the disaster zone for over-time recognition.

Under the new standards, qualifying construction contracts can recognize revenue based on the progress of satisfaction, typically measured as costs incurred to date relative to estimated total costs. The problem is that "estimated total costs" is itself a management estimate. Underestimate total costs, and the same amount of costs incurred yields a higher percentage of completion, causing revenue to be recognized prematurely. Moreover, this bias is highly concealed — all investors see in the annual report is a percentage figure, making it difficult to judge whether it's reasonable.

The new standards introduced two related balance sheet items that happen to be useful for cross-verification: contract assets and accounts receivable. Both correspond to revenue already recognized, but differ in the strength of the right to payment. Accounts receivable represents amounts for which the company has invoiced the customer and holds an unconditional right to payment. Contract assets represent revenue already recognized but for which the company hasn't yet obtained an unconditional right to payment (perhaps the contractual billing milestone hasn't been reached, or a specific milestone must be achieved first). During a construction contract's execution, recognized revenue first sits in contract assets, then transfers to accounts receivable once the contractual billing milestone is reached.

In a healthy construction-type company, contract assets, accounts receivable, and operating cash flow should grow roughly in tandem — recognizing revenue, invoicing, and collecting cash form a relatively stable chain. There are two levels of warning signals along this chain. First level: accounts receivable growing significantly faster than revenue, or operating cash flow significantly lagging behind revenue — meaning invoices were issued but customers aren't paying on time. Second level (more alarming): contract assets significantly outpacing accounts receivable — meaning revenue recognition is running ahead of even the invoicing milestone, and the unconditional right to payment hasn't been obtained yet. When this happens, either progress estimates are overly optimistic or there are issues with how contract milestones are being assessed. Either way, it means the distance between revenue and cash is being stretched further.

**Are You Selling or Helping Someone Else Sell: A Question Standards Are Still Debating**

Returning to Meituan's story from the beginning. One of the trickiest judgments in the new standards is "principal vs. agent" — are you "directly providing" the good or service, or "arranging for someone else to provide it"? The former uses the gross method, the latter the net method. Profit is identical, but revenue can differ by several multiples. Meituan's food delivery and in-store businesses using opposite methods within the same financial report is a real-world manifestation of this judgment.

The new standards' criterion is: before the good or service is transferred to the customer, did you obtain control of it? Do you bear the primary fulfillment responsibility, inventory risk, and pricing discretion?

For physical goods, this judgment usually isn't difficult — you purchased inventory, stored it in your warehouse, set the price, and bore the risk of unsold stock, so you're the principal. But when the subject of the transaction shifts from goods to services, things get complicated. In its 2024 post-implementation review, the IASB acknowledged that determining who "controls" a service is far more difficult than determining who controls a physical good — services have no physical form, and the concept of "controlling a service not yet provided before it is transferred to the customer" is inherently abstract.

The greater challenge comes from the platform economy. These business models hadn't emerged at scale during the decade-plus when IFRS 15 was being developed, and the standard-setters couldn't fully anticipate them. The IASB also acknowledged that the difficulties primarily arise in new business models that emerged after IFRS 15 was issued.

The challenges the platform economy poses to the standards go beyond "principal vs. agent." As mentioned at the beginning, Meituan deducts food delivery subsidies from revenue, which involves another judgment in IFRS 15: what constitutes "consideration payable to a customer"? When a platform both charges consumers and subsidizes them, and sometimes also gives rebates to merchants, the direction and nature of each payment must be assessed individually. Two platform companies may have similar business scales, but if their subsidy treatment differs slightly (deducted from revenue vs. recorded as selling expenses), the disclosed "revenue" could differ by an order of magnitude.

In September 2024, the IASB completed its post-implementation review of IFRS 15, confirming that the standard is generally working well, but listed "principal vs. agent" as the foremost application challenge, to be included in the next agenda consultation. There was internal disagreement at the IASB — some board members thought more specific application guidance should be provided, some thought enhanced disclosure requirements would suffice, and others believed the current principle-based framework didn't need changing. The issue was classified as "medium priority" — acknowledging the problem exists, but without a consensus on a solution.

For investors, the takeaway is practical: when you see a platform company's reported revenue figure, your first move should be to open the notes to the annual report and confirm whether it's gross or net, and how subsidies are treated. If two companies use different methods, any valuation comparison based on revenue multiples is meaningless.

## Evergrande and Regulation

In July 2017, China's Ministry of Finance issued the revised new revenue standard (new CAS 14), maintaining a high degree of convergence with IFRS 15. The five-step model, transfer of control, unbundling of performance obligations — the core framework was entirely consistent.

The new standards impacted industries across China. Real estate deserves special attention — it is directly linked to the largest revenue fraud case in China's capital markets.

You buy a pre-sale apartment, sign the contract, and make the down payment, and the developer starts construction. When can the developer recognize this money as revenue? The key lies in the new standards' criteria — whether the property being built has "no alternative use" and whether the developer has an "enforceable right to payment for performance completed to date." Under China's pre-sale system, pre-sale contract terms typically stipulate that if the buyer defaults and cancels, the developer can only retain the deposit. In other words, the developer has no corresponding enforceable right to payment for the completed portion — this fails to meet one of the core conditions for the "over time" method in the new standards. Based on this judgment, the vast majority of A-share real estate companies adopted "point-in-time recognition" — revenue is recognized only when the keys are handed over. Before delivery, all pre-sale receipts sit under "contract liabilities" on the balance sheet — they are liabilities, not revenue.

It is precisely this rule that makes the following story possible.

In May 2024, the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) issued its administrative penalty decision against Evergrande Real Estate, exposing the largest revenue fraud in Chinese real estate history. The CSRC determined that Evergrande Real Estate inflated revenue by RMB 213.989 billion in 2019, representing 50.14% of that period's operating revenue, with corresponding inflated profits of RMB 40.722 billion. In 2020, it inflated revenue by RMB 350.157 billion, representing 78.54% of that period's operating revenue, with corresponding inflated profits of RMB 51.289 billion. Over the two years combined, revenue was inflated by RMB 564.1 billion, and profits by RMB 92 billion.

Regarding the specific fraud methods, the CSRC's penalty decision provided further details: unlike pure fabrication of business transactions, Evergrande Real Estate violated accounting standards by "inflating financial statements through premature revenue recognition and other methods, borrowing against future revenue." In terms of execution, the company's headquarters and project companies worked in concert, "employing methods such as modifying and adjusting the current year's delivery lists and altering delivery dates in the Mingyuan system to fabricate material facts." The Mingyuan system is a business management software widely used in the real estate industry, recording the entire process of sales, contract signing, delivery, and payment collection, and serving as the primary source from which auditors extract delivery documentation. Evergrande Real Estate had been using the same "point-in-time recognition" method as most A-share real estate companies — recognizing revenue upon delivery. But by falsifying delivery lists and delivery dates in the system, properties that hadn't actually been delivered appeared as "already delivered" in the system, and the corresponding pre-sale receipts were transferred from contract liabilities to operating revenue.

This was not exploiting gray areas in the standards, nor an aggressive accounting policy choice. This was fabricating business facts to breach the standards' boundaries when revenue recognition conditions were not met — it was outright fraud, not a matter of judgment.

Xu Jiayin (Hui Ka Yan) was found to have "decided upon and organized the implementation of the financial fraud, using particularly egregious methods," and was given a lifetime ban from the securities market and fined RMB 47 million. Evergrande Real Estate was fined RMB 4.175 billion — the maximum penalty under the new Securities Law for fraudulent issuance. PricewaterhouseCoopers, as the auditor, was found to have "known that Evergrande Real Estate prematurely recognized revenue in 2020 and failed to flag it," and was fined a total of RMB 441 million by the Ministry of Finance and the CSRC combined, had its operations suspended for six months, its Guangzhou office was revoked, and four signing CPAs had their certificates revoked.

As for the issue of revenue fraud, the Chinese Institute of Certified Public Accountants (CICPA) provided a comprehensive checklist in its 2024 annual report audit work notice. The CICPA required auditors to "work based on the presumption that revenue recognition carries a fraud risk," specifically examining whether revenue changes are consistent with market trends and cash flows, whether there are special payment arrangements and return conditions, whether the distinction between principal and agent in contracts is appropriate, whether the conditions for over-time recognition are truly met, and the "empty cycling of transactions" fraud risk in supply chain finance and financing trades. When auditing standards go so far as to write in "presume that revenue carries fraud risk," investors reading annual reports would do well to bring the same vigilance.

## How Investors Should Read Revenue

The revenue recognition policy disclosures in the notes to the annual report are the starting point for understanding a company's financial reporting quality. Differences in choices among companies in the same industry often reveal how aggressive management is. Is revenue recognized over time or at a point in time? How are performance obligations unbundled? Are subsidies and rebates deducted from revenue or recorded as expenses? If a company's choices consistently lean toward "recognizing more revenue earlier," that's worth at least one additional question about why.

The alignment between revenue and cash flow is the most fundamental and reliable verification tool. But which specific line item to watch depends on the business model: for "payment before delivery" businesses (such as baijiu, some software subscriptions, and prepaid services), watch changes in contract liabilities — declining advances from customers often precede revenue declines. For "delivery before payment" businesses (such as pharmaceuticals, consumer goods, and manufacturing), watch whether accounts receivable growth outpaces revenue, and track changes in accounts receivable turnover days. Construction-type companies require additionally watching contract assets — the portion where revenue has been recognized but the billing milestone hasn't been reached. Regardless of the model, it all comes back to operating cash flow: if "cash received from selling goods and providing services" chronically lags behind revenue growth, that widening gap is the strongest warning signal.

When comparing platform-type companies, the first thing to do is confirm whether their revenue is recorded using the gross method or the net method. If two companies use different methods, comparing revenue multiples is meaningless.

## Conclusion

The story of revenue recognition standards is ultimately a story about "when can you say you've earned it."

There's a common interpretation of this standards evolution within the industry: the old standards leaned toward the seller's perspective — once you shipped the goods and transferred the risks, you'd earned it. The new standards shift to the buyer's perspective — only when the buyer has truly received it, can use it, and controls it, have you earned it. This shift in perspective brought many previously overlooked details to the surface: how many promises does a single contract actually contain? Is there financing hidden in the price? Are you actually selling, or helping someone else sell?

IFRS 15 took 12 years to develop, passed its post-implementation review in 2024, and was deemed to be "operating as intended." But the standard-setters also acknowledged that the judgment around "principal vs. agent" remains the most prominent application challenge — particularly in service transactions and new platform economy models, where the boundaries of "control" are still being explored. Evergrande Real Estate's RMB 564.1 billion in inflated revenue reminds us from another direction: standards define the correct way to keep the books, but they can't stop someone from fabricating business facts to circumvent the rules. Standards aren't omnipotent — they can govern "how the books should be kept," but they can't ensure "whether the events actually occurred." The latter is the job of auditing, internal controls, and regulation.

For investors, however standards may change, one principle remains constant: financial statements must be read for their substance, not just their surface. The number on the first line of the income statement is the surface; whether there are real cash inflows behind it, whether receivables and contract assets are reasonable, whether customers have a sustainable willingness to pay — that's the substance. A financial report that is all surface and no substance will eventually make investors pay it back.
