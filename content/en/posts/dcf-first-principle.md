## Back to Basics

What is the essence of investing? It is purchasing an asset with the expectation that its future cash flow returns will exceed the price paid today.

This is the core idea behind DCF (Discounted Cash Flow): **The value of an asset equals the present value of all its future cash flows.**

## The Three Core Variables of DCF

Every DCF model essentially answers three questions:

### 1. Free Cash Flow (FCF)

How much cash can the company truly distribute to shareholders? This is not accounting profit, but the genuinely "free" cash remaining after deducting capital expenditures needed to maintain operations.

### 2. Growth Rate (g)

At what rate will these cash flows grow in the future? How long can growth be sustained? This requires understanding the company's competitive advantages, addressable market, and management's capital allocation ability.

### 3. Discount Rate (r)

How much are future cash flows worth relative to today? The discount rate reflects the time value of money and uncertainty — the more uncertain the cash flows, the higher the discount rate needed to compensate for risk.

## Roughly Right

DCF is not about calculating a precise number. It is a thinking framework.

Warren Buffett said: "It is better to be roughly right than precisely wrong." We use DCF not to prove a stock is worth exactly $87.34, but to determine it is worth approximately $80-100, while the current price is only $50.

The key is the **margin of safety** — when the price is far below your conservative estimate, even if your assumptions are somewhat off, it is very difficult to lose money.

## Practical Advice

1. **Start from financial statements**: Learn to extract free cash flow from income statements and cash flow statements
2. **Conservative assumptions**: Better to underestimate growth than overestimate
3. **Scenario analysis**: Calculate with optimistic, neutral, and pessimistic assumptions to understand the valuation range
4. **Focus on certainty**: Prioritize companies with highly predictable cash flows

DCF is not omnipotent, but it is the most important thinking tool for value investors. Master it, and you have the fundamental framework for evaluating any business.
