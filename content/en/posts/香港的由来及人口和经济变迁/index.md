## **There Is a "Hong Kong" Within Hong Kong**

![eccb582889f1254d026442a28bd5ebdc.png](eccb582889f1254d026442a28bd5ebdc.png)

Open a map of Hong Kong and you will discover that within Hong Kong there is a place actually called Hong Kong — this is Hong Kong's true financial center, commonly known as Hong Kong Island. This small island stretches approximately 18 kilometers from east to west and 3 to 8 kilometers from north to south. When people say Hong Kong was under British colonial rule for over 150 years, the starting point is 1842, when Hong Kong Island was permanently ceded to Britain by the Qing government under the Treaty of Nanking.

But Hong Kong also includes two other territories: the Kowloon Peninsula and the New Territories. Kowloon was ceded under the Convention of Peking in 1860, while the New Territories were leased to Britain for ninety-nine years in 1898. Thus, Hong Kong's boundaries were actually formed incrementally over time.

Hong Kong Island and the Kowloon Peninsula face each other across Victoria Harbour. This harbor is deep and wide, surrounded by towering granite hills that serve as natural barriers, making it one of the world's three great natural harbors.

In its early days, Hong Kong Island was dismissed as a "Barren Rock." The New Territories' water sources, land, and agriculture were vital to Hong Kong's flourishing development. However, unlike Hong Kong Island and the Kowloon Peninsula — which were permanently ceded — the New Territories were leased on a long-term basis. The ninety-nine-year lease term inadvertently set the stage for what would become the 1997 handover.

This newly leased territory covers approximately 945 square kilometers — roughly ten times the combined area of Hong Kong Island and Kowloon — and includes some 230 outlying islands. The largest of these is Lantau Island, where Hong Kong International Airport is now located, and which is considerably larger than Hong Kong Island itself.

The leasing of the New Territories had its own historical context. China's defeat in the First Sino-Japanese War in 1895 triggered a scramble among foreign powers to carve up China between 1897 and 1899. Eager to secure its share, Britain demanded that the Qing government lease Weihaiwei in northern Shandong Peninsula. When France subsequently demanded a ninety-nine-year lease on Guangzhouwan — adjacent to Hainan Island and only about 300 kilometers from Hong Kong — the British promptly moved to secure the New Territories, the hinterland north of the Kowloon Peninsula.

## **Hong Kong's Economic Transformation**

Hong Kong's century-plus of colonial history ran parallel to over a century of turmoil in China. Hong Kong's immigration and economic development have always served as a bridge between East and West, closely intertwined with the mainland.

### **Early Opium Trade**

The opium trade is inseparable from early Hong Kong history. The colony was established because of opium, and it survived its difficult early years thanks to opium. From 1845 to 1941, opium was an enormously profitable business — so much so that a large portion of the colonial government's revenue came from the opium trade.

Britain used opium trade revenues to purchase Chinese tea and silk and to fund the occupation of India. Chinese merchants used profits from selling tea and silk to buy opium from British traders. Indian manufacturers, in turn, used proceeds from opium sales to China to purchase cotton from America.

Through its role as an opium trade entrepot, Hong Kong became a hub for five overlapping trade networks: China, Southeast Asia, India, Britain (extending to Europe), and the Americas.

### **A Refugee Society and Industrial Transformation**

Since the early days of British colonial rule, Hong Kong served as a refuge for people fleeing turmoil on the mainland — particularly after the 1911 Revolution and the turbulent 1920s, following the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War in 1937, during the Chinese Civil War from 1946 to 1949, and during the Great Leap Forward and Cultural Revolution in the 1960s and 1970s.

After the 1911 Revolution, Hong Kong became a haven for former Qing officials and loyalists, with large numbers of refugees streaming in daily. By 1914, Hong Kong's population had reached 500,000.

Before the 1930s, although Hong Kong had begun to develop a local industrial base, the British government — concerned that colonial imports would compete with British domestic goods — obstructed industrial development in its colonies. The large British-owned corporations, which held enormous influence in Hong Kong's Executive and Legislative Councils and in the British Parliament, showed little interest in industrialization.

After the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War in 1937, refugees from the mainland flooded in. By early 1941, the colony's population had surpassed 1.5 million. Chinese entrepreneurs brought not only capital but entire factories, some producing military equipment to support China's war effort. This helped build Hong Kong's industrial base and facilitated the colony's postwar economic recovery. After the Japanese blockaded Shanghai and other Chinese ports, half of China's foreign trade was redirected through Hong Kong. Banks such as the Bank of China and the Bank of Communications relocated their headquarters to Hong Kong, transforming it into a foreign exchange banking center for all of China.

In late 1941, Hong Kong fell to Japan. In early January 1942, the Japanese announced that anyone in Hong Kong without a registered address or occupation must leave. Within a year, the population dropped from over 1.5 million to 1 million. By the end of the Japanese occupation in August 1945, Hong Kong's population had fallen to fewer than 600,000.

During the Chinese Civil War from 1946 to 1949, mainland enterprises fleeing south injected new vitality into Hong Kong's industry. The colonial government later estimated that the influx of capital and business expertise from Shanghai gave the colony a ten-to-fifteen-year head start over the rest of East Asia.

From 1946 to the mid-1950s, approximately one million people migrated from the mainland to Hong Kong. By 1955, Hong Kong's population had reached roughly 2.5 million.

Leveraging the industrial base that migrated from the mainland before and after World War II, Hong Kong successfully transformed its economy — from one centered on entrepot trade to export-oriented manufacturing, and eventually to economic diversification. By the early 1970s, Hong Kong began its rise as a financial center of Asia. Its dual status as a British colony and an Asian entrepot laid the foundation for its emergence as an international financial hub, while political instability elsewhere in East Asia further strengthened Hong Kong's economic competitiveness.

Since 1960, Hong Kong's population surged from approximately 4 million to nearly 5 million. Most of this growth came from mainland migrants during the Great Leap Forward (1958–1961) and the subsequent three years of famine.

Before the 1960s and 1970s, most Chinese who moved to Hong Kong regarded it as "borrowed time, borrowed place." As Hong Kong's economy took off and the local Chinese population gained prominence, an increasing number of locally born Chinese began to consider Hong Kong home.

After the reform and opening-up policies of the 1980s, Hong Kong investors poured capital into neighboring Guangdong Province, establishing factories — particularly in the Shenzhen Special Economic Zone, designated in 1982. China's reform and opening-up transformed Hong Kong from a manufacturing base producing light industrial goods such as electronics into a leading global financial and services hub. In the late 1970s, the service sector accounted for less than 65% of Hong Kong's GDP; by the mid-1990s, it had risen to nearly 85%. By the mid-1990s, roughly 90% of Hong Kong's factories had relocated to mainland China, and manufacturing accounted for less than 10% of GDP.

As of 2023, Hong Kong's permanent resident population stands at approximately 7.2 million. Although some locals have been leaving Hong Kong, the city's talent recruitment programs have simultaneously attracted large numbers of mainland Chinese seeking opportunities there.

To understand the challenges Hong Kong faces today and to grasp its future prospects, one must first understand its past and trace the trajectory of its development through history.

Looking back, Hong Kong's greatest advantage has been its role as a bridge between East and West, connecting China with the rest of the world. The core competitive strengths underpinning this advantage are Hong Kong's common law legal system, its linked exchange rate pegged to the U.S. dollar with full currency convertibility, and its evolving role as an offshore RMB center.

Hong Kong's strengths are also the source of its current difficulties. As a nexus between East and West, Hong Kong is particularly exposed to geopolitical shocks. However, as long as its core competitive strengths remain intact, Hong Kong will continue to serve — over the long term — as the gateway for foreign investment into China and for mainland enterprises seeking overseas financing and international expansion.
