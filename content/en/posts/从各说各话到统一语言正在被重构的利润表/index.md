*The Accounting Standards Story (Part Two)*

When Shuanglu Pharma released its Q3 2025 earnings, investors saw a staggering number: net profit attributable to shareholders of RMB 141 million, up 943% year-over-year. Almost a tenfold increase. But a closer look at the report told a very different story — net profit after deducting non-recurring items came in at only RMB 29.33 million, down 62%. One metric up 943%, the other down 62% — two profit figures from the same company, the same reporting period, telling entirely opposite stories.

The 943% story came from stock investment gains. Shuanglu Pharma held shares in Shouyao Holdings and Xinghao Pharma, both of which surged in the first three quarters. Fair value gains on trading financial assets reached RMB 128 million, compared to a loss of RMB 122 million in the same period a year earlier. That RMB 250 million swing — from negative to positive — is the entire secret behind the jump from -62% to +943%. The -62% story is the truth about the core business: intensifying competition and centralized procurement price cuts were squeezing the pharmaceutical operations.

The sequel was even more brutal. Both stocks pulled back in Q4, and by the time the full-year earnings preview came out in late January 2026, the narrative had completely reversed: Shuanglu Pharma projected a full-year net loss of RMB 200-290 million. And the non-recurring-adjusted figure? A loss of RMB 40-70 million. The gap of over RMB 200 million between the two — entirely from investment gains and losses unrelated to the core pharma business.

We are in the thick of earnings season right now. If you follow listed company announcements, you will notice a pattern: headlines almost always lead with net profit — "Net profit up XX% year-over-year." As for the non-recurring-adjusted figure, it rarely makes the headline. Many retail investors may never look at it, or may not even know the metric exists. They make buy and sell decisions based on net profit growth and P/E ratios, unaware that a large chunk of the number may have nothing to do with the company's core operations.

Even more strangely, until the 2024 delisting rule reform, A-share delisting criteria did not incorporate the non-recurring-adjusted figure at all. Early delisting rules looked only at net profit: two consecutive years of losses triggered an ST designation, three years led to suspension. After multiple rounds of reform, the standard evolved into a combination of negative net profit plus revenue below a certain threshold — but "net profit" always meant unadjusted net profit. The implication? A company with chronically loss-making core operations could sell a building at year-end, dispose of an equity stake, or collect a government subsidy, push net profit just above zero, and stay listed. A 2018 Shenzhen Stock Exchange study found that of 18 companies applying to remove their ST designation that year, only 8 had positive non-recurring-adjusted net profit — more than half had survived on non-recurring items alone. The cycle of getting flagged one year and unflagged the next repeated over and over. It was not until the 2024 delisting reform incorporated "the lower of net profit before and after non-recurring deductions" that this loophole was finally closed.

The CSRC first required listed companies to disclose non-recurring-adjusted net profit back in 1999. Since then, the metric has gone through five revisions, each expanding and refining the list of items to be excluded: the first formal definition and enumeration came in 2001; in 2004 the approach shifted from pure enumeration to "definition plus enumeration," adding principled judgment; the 2007 and 2008 revisions aligned with the new accounting standards — for example, when the new standards allowed debt restructuring gains to flow through income, a wave of ST companies attempted turnarounds via debt forgiveness, prompting a corresponding tightening; the most recent major revision came in 2023, adding three judgment principles (economic substance, industry characteristics, materiality) and refining the treatment of government grants, financial assets, and share-based payments. The non-recurring-adjusted metric gradually became embedded in refinancing reviews, earnings commitments, and equity incentive programs. Yet in the one area where it should have mattered most — delisting — it was absent for over two decades.

Back to the more fundamental question: **If profit distortion is a widely acknowledged fact, why doesn't the income statement itself separate operating income from non-operating income? Why do investors need a supplementary metric outside the income statement to see the truth?**

## An Income Statement in Need of Improvement

If you have ever built a DCF model, you have probably had this experience: you open an A-share company's income statement looking for a clean operating profit as your valuation starting point, only to find that the "operating profit" line cannot be used directly.

The reason lies in the presentation format of A-share income statements. Revenue, minus cost of goods sold, taxes and surcharges, selling expenses, administrative expenses, R&D expenses — all reasonable so far. But then, before arriving at "operating profit," the statement subtracts finance costs (including both interest expense and interest income), adds investment income, adds fair value gains and losses, adds other income (mainly government grants), subtracts various impairment losses, and adds gains on asset disposals. After all these additions and subtractions, you get "operating profit" — yes, despite including items unrelated to core operations, it is still called "operating profit."

Under international practice, most companies present interest expense below operating profit, because it represents a financing cost unrelated to operating capability. But the standard A-share format places finance costs within the operating profit calculation. Investment income and fair value changes are treated the same way. The result: "operating profit" on an A-share income statement is a fundamentally different concept from what international investors understand as operating profit. Anyone building a DCF has to make manual adjustments — subtracting investment income and fair value gains, adding back net interest.

While international practice is somewhat better than the A-share format, it still has its own standards gaps and comparability challenges. IAS 1 — the core IFRS standard governing income statement presentation — does not even require an "operating profit" subtotal, let alone provide a uniform definition. Although most international companies do in practice present interest expense below operating profit, this is convention rather than a standards requirement. As for investment income, asset disposal gains, and similar items, treatment varies across companies. An IASB analysis of 100 listed companies found that some presented an operating profit subtotal while others did not; among those that did, the scope varied — some included investment income, others excluded it. Two companies in the same industry could have income statements that looked completely different.

To summarize: A-share income statements have a standardized format but lack an operating perspective, mixing operating and non-operating items together; income statements under international standards suffer from being too loose, lacking uniform definitions. The root causes differ, but the problem for investors is the same — there is no standardized, comparable operating profit figure to work with.

## IFRS 18: A Reform a Decade in the Making

The comparability problem with income statements is not new. The IASB and FASB launched a joint project around 2004 to comprehensively reform financial statement presentation, but it was too ambitious — spanning the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement simultaneously — and collapsed under the weight of its own scope and unresolved disagreements in the early 2010s.

The turning point came in 2014. In July of that year, the IASB put "performance reporting" back on its research agenda. This time the driving force came primarily from investors. CFA Institute — the most influential representative body in the investor community — had for years called on the IASB to address income statement comparability. The core demand was straightforward: give operating profit a uniform definition so that income statements of companies in the same industry can be meaningfully compared.

Having learned from the earlier failure, the IASB focused exclusively on the income statement this time, leaving the balance sheet untouched. The project was renamed "Primary Financial Statements" in 2015. An exposure draft was published in December 2019. After a COVID-related extension, 215 comment letters came in. Following extensive redeliberations and targeted outreach, IFRS 18 — *Presentation and Disclosure in Financial Statements* — was formally issued on April 9, 2024. From inception to completion, the project took a full decade. The IASB Chair called it "the most significant change to how companies report financial performance in over twenty years."

IFRS 18 does three core things.

First, it introduces three categories — operating, investing, and financing — into the income statement, and requires all entities to present an "operating profit" subtotal. The way the operating category is defined is worth noting — it uses a "residual approach": any income and expenses that do not belong to investing, financing, income tax, or discontinued operations fall into the operating category. Companies cannot push unflattering expenses outside operating profit. Volatile or infrequent expenses, as long as they are not investing or financing in nature, stay within operating profit. The operating profit line provides a complete picture of the business's operations, not a flattering one.

Second, it brings management performance measures (MPMs) — custom-defined metrics that management uses — into the audited financial statement notes. Previously, companies could freely publish various "adjusted" non-GAAP metrics in press releases and investor presentations, unconstrained by audit. Under IFRS 18, these metrics must be disclosed in the notes with their definitions, calculation methods, and a reconciliation to the standard IFRS metrics.

Third, it simultaneously amends IAS 7 (the cash flow statement standard), requiring that the indirect method start from operating profit and eliminating the classification choice for interest and dividend cash flows. The alignment between the income statement and the cash flow statement becomes much clearer.

## The Design Logic Behind It

The income statement structure in IFRS 18 reflects a clear design rationale.

In its project summary, the IASB explicitly stated that investors use the operating profit subtotal as a measure of operating performance and as a starting point for forecasting future cash flows. The implication runs deeper than it appears — operating profit is not merely an accounting number; it is the core entry point for margin analysis, return on invested capital (ROIC) calculations, enterprise value multiples (EV/EBIT), and free cash flow derivations in DCF valuations. Using Damodaran's FCFF framework: operating profit is the starting point of the entire valuation chain. Operating profit times (1 - tax rate) yields net operating profit after tax (NOPAT); subtract net reinvestment, and you get free cash flow to the firm. The operating profit defined by IFRS 18 is, in essence, a standardized, auditable, cross-company comparable foundation for financial analysis and valuation.

The IASB's Effects Analysis cites Barton et al. (2010) in *The Accounting Review*, demonstrating that operating profit is the performance metric most valued by global investors and has greater explanatory power for stock price movements than net income. This shows that the IASB, when designing the standard, genuinely considered investors' analytical needs and practical pain points.

Why use a residual approach to define the operating category? Because if you positively enumerate "what counts as operating," companies have an incentive to push unfavorable items outside operating profit. The residual approach flips the burden of proof: only items that clearly belong to investing or financing can leave operating profit; everything else stays. This is the exact opposite of the non-GAAP logic, where management decides which items to exclude.

Separating financing costs from operating profit is already widespread practice internationally, and in the United States it carries the additional weight of SEC comment letters. The same company with different capital structures — more equity or more debt — will have very different interest expense, but its operating capability is identical. IFRS 18's contribution is elevating this practice into a globally uniform standard. For China, this change is especially significant — because the standard A-share income statement format has always placed finance costs above operating profit, a practice that does not even align with what is widely followed internationally.

## Reining In Non-GAAP Metrics

IFRS 18's second major initiative — regulating management performance measures — addresses another long-standing issue.

Why are companies so eager to publish non-GAAP metrics? The fundamental motivation is simple: GAAP figures are constrained by standards and include items that may not look flattering; non-GAAP metrics, defined by management, allow for a more appealing earnings narrative. FactSet data from around 2016 showed that 75% of Dow Jones companies reported non-GAAP EPS higher than GAAP EPS, and roughly 97% of listed companies published some form of non-GAAP metric. When a metric almost always looks better than the standards-based figure, its objectivity is worth questioning.

Extreme cases have bordered on the absurd. When WeWork was racing toward its 2019 IPO, it invented a metric called "Community Adjusted EBITDA" — "community" referring to WeWork's co-working spaces. Starting from EBITDA, this metric further stripped out rent, marketing, G&A, and design costs — for a co-working company, these expenses are essentially the entire cost of operations. Using this metric, WeWork reported USD 233 million in "profit." The founder of bond research firm Covenant Review remarked that he had never encountered the term in his career. In 2011, Groupon before its IPO created "Adjusted Consolidated Segment Operating Income," turning a USD 420 million operating loss into USD 60 million of "profit" — the SEC ultimately stepped in and stopped it.

In the United States, the SEC has steadily tightened non-GAAP enforcement since 2016, with penalties exceeding USD 20 million since 2023. FASB also issued an exposure draft on expense disaggregation disclosure in 2023 (ASU Subtopic 220-40), requiring listed companies to disclose the nature-based composition of operating expenses in the notes. But FASB's approach touches only note disclosures, not the main structure of the income statement.

IFRS 18 goes further. It does not prohibit custom metrics (MPMs); instead, it brings them inside the tent. Companies can continue using their own defined metrics, but must disclose in the audited notes their definitions, calculation methods, and the reconciliation to IFRS standard metrics. Because MPM disclosures appear in the financial statement notes, they naturally fall within the scope of the annual audit. CFA Institute published a report titled "Bridging the Gap" as early as 2016, calling for exactly this. The MPM provisions in IFRS 18 are a substantive response to that call.

## China's Response: Faster Than Expected

On December 29, 2025, China's Ministry of Finance released a revised exposure draft of CAS 30 (*Accounting Standards for Business Enterprises No. 30 — Presentation of Financial Statements*), directly aligning with IFRS 18. Only 20 months elapsed between IFRS 18's issuance and China's exposure draft. According to the drafting notes, the Ministry began studying the standard immediately after IFRS 18 was published, conducted two rounds of simulation testing among A+H-listed companies, and held multiple rounds of field research and expert reviews — an unusually fast pace by the standards of China's convergence history.

One passage in the drafting notes deserves special attention: "The current presentation standard includes gains and losses from investing, financing, and other non-operating activities within operating profit, making it difficult for users to understand the different sources of a company's profits directly from the financial statements." This is the regulator formally acknowledging, in the context of international convergence, that the current income statement structure has room for improvement.

The revised income statement will classify income and expenses into five categories: operating, investing, financing, income tax, and discontinued operations, with two new subtotals — "operating profit" and "operating and investing profit (profit before financing and income tax)." Investment income and fair value changes previously lumped into operating profit will be separated into the investing category; interest expense within finance costs will move to the financing category. For the first time, A-share investors will see a "clean" operating profit in the body of the income statement — free of interest expense, investment income, and fair value fluctuations.

This is a significant step forward for A-share investors. Even before IFRS 18, most international companies in practice presented interest expense below operating profit. Under IFRS, this was merely convention — IAS 1 did not even require an operating profit subtotal. Under US GAAP, there was stronger discipline — while SEC Regulation S-X also does not explicitly require an "operating income" line, it prescribes the ordering of income statement line items (interest and investment income come after operating items), and the SEC actively uses comment letters to correct deviations. Either way, excluding interest from operating profit is widely followed practice internationally. China's current standard format has not even reached that baseline. After the CAS 30 revision, the A-share income statement will achieve a leap forward in presentation structure.

The Chinese characteristics show up in two areas. First, China continues its tradition of "standardized formats" — while IFRS 18 provides only principles-based guidance without prescribing specific formats, China is issuing three standard income statement templates: one for general enterprises, one for financial institutions, and one for consolidated statements. Second, implementation will be phased — the first wave covers A+H-listed companies and those that report under IFRS or CAS overseas, with an effective date of January 1, 2027, synchronized with IFRS 18's global effective date. As for when the remaining listed companies and non-listed enterprises will follow, the drafting notes state only that "the scope will be gradually expanded after the first batch of implementing enterprises have built up solid experience and demonstration cases," without giving a specific timeline.

The exposure draft simultaneously introduces MPM disclosure requirements. In the future, when A-share companies use custom metrics like "core profit" or "adjusted net profit" in earnings previews or investor communications, they will need to formally disclose the definitions, calculation methods, and reconciliation in the annual report notes.

China previously relied on its "non-recurring items" framework to address the problem of misleading net profit figures, but the non-recurring-adjusted net profit has always been a supplementary regulatory metric outside the income statement. After the CAS 30 revision, the income statement itself will distinguish between operating and non-operating income. Going forward, investors assessing a company's operating quality will no longer need to dig through non-recurring item schedules in the notes — the main structure of the income statement will provide the answer.

## What Should Investors Make of This?

For value investors who pay close attention to financial statements and valuation, this standards revision is genuinely good news.

The most direct impact is on DCF valuations. Previously, extracting a usable operating profit from an A-share income statement required manual adjustments. Once the new format is implemented, "operating profit" can in many cases be used directly as the valuation starting point. A caveat is warranted: the new operating profit may still include items that require investor judgment — for example, non-recurring asset impairment losses or restructuring charges will remain within operating profit (a consequence of the residual approach), and investors may need to make further adjustments depending on the circumstances. But compared to the current state of affairs, the noise will be significantly reduced.

ROIC analysis will also benefit. Currently, one of the biggest obstacles to cross-company ROIC comparisons is inconsistent operating profit definitions. With a unified definition, companies in the same industry will finally have a comparable basis for operating profit.

Risks worth watching remain. There is still judgment involved in classifying items between operating and investing — whether a company's real estate holdings count as operating assets or investment assets, for instance, will lead to different operating profit figures depending on the classification. For diversified conglomerates, classification judgments at the consolidated level are even more complex. And while MPMs are now within the audit scope, the choice of metric definition still rests with management — auditors verify only that the calculation is correct and the reconciliation is complete, not whether the metric itself is "reasonable."

## Conclusion

Accounting is the language of business. A well-designed income statement should let investors see at a glance a company's core operating capability — which revenue comes from the core business, which comes from investments, and how much goes to financing costs. For over two decades, neither Chinese nor international income statements have done this well.

IFRS 18 and China's CAS 30 revision aim to fundamentally change this. If all goes according to plan, from 2027 onward income statements will present a new structure: operating activities on top, investing and financing below, with operating profit clearly stated.

For users of financial statements, this is not a cosmetic tweak to presentation format. Enterprise value is the present value of future free cash flows, and cash flow forecasting begins with operating profit. What IFRS 18 and CAS 30 have done is make that starting point directly available from the income statement — no more assembling it yourself.
