## About Li Lu

When it comes to value investing, everyone knows Buffett and Charlie Munger. But who is Li Lu? Far fewer people are likely to know.

Li Lu was the late Charlie Munger's investment partner and the founder of Himalaya Capital Management. He has long been an active advocate for value investing principles. In 2009, he facilitated the translation of *Poor Charlie's Almanack* into Chinese and its publication in China. In 2020, Li Lu was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences.

Li Lu has two notable investment cases in China: one was convincing Munger to invest in BYD, and the other was taking a large position in the Postal Savings Bank of China in 2020.

In 2015 and 2019, Li Lu delivered two renowned speeches at Peking University's Guanghua School of Management: *The Prospects of Value Investing in China* and *Uniting Knowledge and Action in Value Investing*. Both speeches are included in his book *Civilization, Modernization, Value Investing, and China*.

In the 2015 speech *The Prospects of Value Investing in China*, Li Lu surveyed the evolution of human civilization from a sweeping perspective, introduced the concept of Civilization 3.0 — the age of science and technology — and addressed many of the concerns investors have about investing in China.

Although this speech was delivered in 2015, the challenges Li Lu raised and answered regarding value investing in China remain not only relevant today but are in fact a major reason behind the current lack of investor confidence.

For value investors feeling uncertain in today's environment, this speech is well worth reading. The version included in the book is slightly abridged; the full text can be found on Himalaya Capital's official website.

## Civilization 3.0 and Value Investing

One of the core arguments in Li Lu's speech is that sustained, cumulative, long-term compounding growth in the human economy is a phenomenon of only the past few hundred years. The fundamental driver has been the combination of modern science and technology with free market economics — the arrival of modernization, which he calls Civilization 3.0. The pursuit of modernization has been the defining theme of China's exploration over the past century-plus. Despite a winding path, the achievements since the Reform and Opening Up prove that China has found its way onto the road of Civilization 3.0.

Could China deviate from the main course of Civilization 3.0? Does value investing have a future in China? Li Lu believes:

> For a culture and a nation like China, if you have a good understanding of its history — especially once you grasp the true nature of modern civilization — the likelihood of turning back is extremely small. The probability of China leaving the global common market is virtually zero, and the chance of fundamentally changing the rules of the market economy is also an extremely low-probability event.

> As long as China continues on the path of Civilization 3.0 — committed to a predominantly free market economy and modern science and technology — the performance of its major asset classes will broadly follow the patterns observed in mature market economies over the past three hundred years. The economy will continue to grow cumulatively, stocks will continue to outperform other major financial asset classes, and value investing in China, just as in the United States, will remain the right path — delivering sustainable, stable, and more secure investment returns for clients.

It has been nearly ten years since Li Lu's speech. The most significant change during this period has been the emergence of the U.S.-China trade war, with Western nations actively pursuing so-called decoupling or de-risking — something he did not foresee. Yet China continues to champion free trade and deepen domestic reforms, with greater emphasis than ever on technological innovation. From this perspective, we remain on the main course of Civilization 3.0. So why do so many people still lack confidence in China's investment prospects?

To be fair, the economic environment we face today differs from that of 2015, with more short-term difficulties and greater internal and external challenges. Moreover, the concerns that foreign investors have about China's unique characteristics seem to have intensified compared to 2015.

## Civilization 3.0 and China's Unique Characteristics

Civilization 3.0 — the combination of modern science and technology with free market economics — is a product of Western modernization. China's uniqueness lies in its distinct culture and its different political institutional arrangements.

Some investors worry that China's unique characteristics may steer it away from the course of Civilization 3.0. This concern has certain intellectual roots, such as Fukuyama's "End of History" thesis.

Francis Fukuyama, a Japanese-American scholar, published his famous essay *The End of History?* in 1989. In 1992, he expanded it into the book *The End of History and the Last Man*. He argued that regardless of a nation's particular cultural traditions, the pursuit of modernization would ultimately converge on a universal common model. While modernized nations each have their own characteristics, they are broadly similar in their institutional arrangements.

Fukuyama laid out a three-stage inference for the universalization of modernization. Put simply: as long as humanity pursues modernization, it will develop science and technology; developing science and technology leads to adopting market economics; and developing market economics leads to liberal democracy.

China's remarkable development over the past several decades has powerfully challenged Fukuyama's theory. Fukuyama himself has taken China's development experience seriously and has recognized the unique traditions of Chinese political civilization, as well as China's profound influence on modernization across the Asian region. He has publicly acknowledged that the China model holds significant research value and may serve as an important test case for his theory.

Li Lu argues that the main road of Civilization 3.0 has little to do with politics or culture, and everything to do with free market economics and modern technology — that is its essence. This is the greatest misconception many investors, especially Western investors, hold about China.

In 2018, in his essay *Li Lu on Modernization: Understanding the Trajectory of U.S.-China Relations from the Perspective of Human Civilization*, he further elaborated on the relationship between political systems and Civilization 3.0:

> Different political systems do not have an inherent binding relationship with the economy in the era of Civilization 3.0. Because during economic development in the Civilization 3.0 era, politics is also constantly evolving. At the time of economic takeoff, virtually all countries had relatively centralized systems — even democracies were republican democracies of a very small elite. For example, Britain during the early Industrial Revolution was a constitutional monarchy, and its construction of a global market relied on colonial rule — a rather bloody form of politics. In the early stages of America's economic takeoff, fewer than ten percent of the population could vote, and although it was not a major colonial power, it was the largest slave-holding nation of its time. Not to mention countries like Japan and Germany, which went through fascism and foreign aggression. But as economies developed and living standards steadily improved, the major Western nations gradually moved toward constitutionalism, democracy, human rights, and freedom. This political evolution was a result, not a cause, of economic development in the Civilization 3.0 era.

## Insights from Li Lu's Speech

Whether or not you agree with Li Lu's views, his analysis of the essence of Civilization 3.0 and his perspective on the future of value investing in China deserve serious reflection.

As long as we stay committed to the direction of market economics and continue to harness the driving force of technological innovation, China will inevitably forge its own distinctive path to modernization. In the long run, value investing in China remains a broad and open road. In the current market environment, what we need most is confidence and rationality — which will help us break out of the negative economic cycle.
