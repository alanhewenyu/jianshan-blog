This morning, a light rain fell over Beijing. It seems that every year around the time of the Gaokao (China's national college entrance examination), rain comes to many parts of the country. This brings to mind a famous line by the Qing dynasty poet Gong Zizhen: "I urge the heavens to rouse their spirit once more, and bestow talent upon us in every form." One hopes that this weather brings a touch of coolness to the examinees, helping them perform at their best.

## Memories of the Gaokao

No other event commands the attention and cooperation of the entire society quite like the Gaokao. Although my residential compound does not host an exam venue, the neighborhood committee sent out early notices that there should be no renovation noise during exam days, along with contact information for on-duty personnel and relevant departments. Because all of us have been through the Gaokao, and as parents, we will sooner or later accompany our own children through it as well. The ritual significance of the Gaokao is thus passed down from one generation to the next.

Any hardship, in retrospect, seems to take on a certain beauty. The moments when we feel the most pain are often the times when we are growing the fastest. The austere, almost monastic lifestyle of high school is something few would willingly endure again. Even now, I occasionally dream of the anxiety of the examination hall -- it is etched deep in my memory. And yet, looking back on that chapter of life, it still feels beautiful. This is perhaps our way of cherishing youth, striving, and growth.

## The Fairness of the Gaokao

Despite its various shortcomings, no one denies that the Gaokao is the fairest mechanism for selecting talent. The prototype of the Gaokao system can be traced back to the imperial civil service examination (keju) in Chinese history. Since the establishment of the keju system during the Sui and Tang dynasties, China developed a framework for selecting officials through examinations, enabling social mobility and gradually transforming from an aristocratic society into a commoner-based one. Compared to Europe, which remained a feudal aristocratic society well into the modern era, China's keju system was undoubtedly a monumental innovation with far-reaching influence.

Education serves as the mechanism through which class differentiation is perpetuated -- and this is the root of much anxiety. Fortunately, we now live in the information age, where everyone has the opportunity to access quality educational resources. This has improved educational equity to a certain extent.

The disparity in Gaokao fairness today is primarily regional, especially between provinces. Populous provinces like Henan and Shandong had 1.36 million and 1 million examinees respectively in 2024. Meanwhile, the median number of Gaokao candidates across all 31 provinces and municipalities is approximately 400,000.

Gaokao admissions follow a province-based quota system. While the quotas account for population differences across provinces and municipalities, the uneven distribution of educational resources means that the probability of gaining university admission still varies enormously. For example, in 2023, the undergraduate admission rates in Beijing and Shanghai were approximately 80%, while those in Henan and Shandong were approximately 45%. Guangdong Province, despite being an economic powerhouse, has seen significant population inflows in recent years, and its undergraduate admission rate in 2023 was only about 40%.

## A Historical Perspective

The province-based quota system for admissions has its roots in the keju era and has a long history.

During the mid-Northern Song dynasty, the proportion of keju candidates admitted began to skew in favor of the south over the north. By the first year of the Zhiping era under Emperor Yingzong of Song (1064 CE), an unprecedented debate erupted over the regional imbalance in keju selection. The two sides were represented by Sima Guang and Ouyang Xiu, and the outcome was to maintain the existing practice -- admissions would continue to be determined solely by examination performance.

After the promulgation of the standardized keju regulations in the early Ming dynasty, the system became more formalized, with many rules differing from earlier dynasties. However, at the level of the metropolitan examination (huishi), the old system of open national competition was retained. Southern candidates continued to dominate, which led to the "Southern and Northern Rankings" incident in the 30th year of the Hongwu era (1397 CE). In the first year of the Hongxi era under Emperor Renzong (1425 CE), Grand Secretary Yang Shiqi proposed the idea of separate examination papers for southern and northern candidates. Two years later, this proposal became reality: the separate Southern and Northern papers system was formally implemented, with fixed quotas set at a ratio of 55:35:10 for the Southern, Northern, and Central papers (the last for regions that could not be clearly classified as either southern or northern). With only a few interruptions, this regional quota-based admission system persisted through the Qing dynasty.

In the 51st year of the Kangxi era (1712 CE), the Southern and Northern papers system was replaced by a province-based quota system, with metropolitan examination quotas determined by the number of examinees in each province. This province-based quota approach did not strictly follow examination scores to determine admissions, but it narrowed the gap between regions. In essence, today's Gaokao admissions system is a continuation of this very tradition.
