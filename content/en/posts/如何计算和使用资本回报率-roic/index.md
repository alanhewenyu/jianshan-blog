## How to Calculate Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)

Where does the money come from, and where does it go? This is what a company's balance sheet records. In terms of the nature of funding, a company's capital investment comes in only two forms: equity and interest-bearing debt — the right side of the balance sheet.

Return on Equity (ROE) is a common financial metric that measures the return attributable to a company's shareholders. Return on Invested Capital (ROIC), by contrast, measures the return on all invested capital, including both equity and interest-bearing debt. As such, ROIC eliminates differences arising from varying capital structures across companies. Compared to ROE, ROIC is better suited for comparing companies with different leverage ratios.

*Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) = Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT) / Invested Capital (IC)*

**Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT).** NOPAT represents profit before deducting financial expenses and non-operating gains or losses. This means NOPAT corresponds to capital that includes both equity and interest-bearing debt financing — in other words, total invested capital (IC).

**Invested Capital (IC).** Invested capital can be calculated using either side of the balance sheet. From the asset side, it is derived by summing the company's operating assets (short-term working capital, long-term fixed assets, etc.). From the liability side, it is calculated by adding interest-bearing debt and equity.

The accounting identity — Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity — ensures that both methods yield the same result.

## ROIC and Free Cash Flow, Economic Value Added, and Profit Growth

### Free Cash Flow

A company's free cash flow can be viewed from two perspectives. Similar to the capital return concepts discussed above, one is Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE), and the other is Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF).

When calculating company valuation, we typically use FCFF because it allows us to disregard the impact of capital structure, eliminating the need to forecast financing cash flows and financial expenses.

Free Cash Flow to the Firm is NOPAT less the capital investment required to maintain and grow the business. The investment needed to sustain and grow the business represents incremental invested capital (IC), which on the left side of the balance sheet manifests as changes in working capital, as well as additions to fixed assets and intangible assets.

### Economic Value Added

Accounting profit does not deduct the cost of equity capital. If a company's net profit falls short of the opportunity cost of equity, it is not truly creating value. Economic Value Added (EVA) is a measure of a company's residual income. It is called "residual" because it accounts for all costs — that is, the income remaining after deducting the cost of all invested capital (WACC). WACC measures the weighted cost of equity and interest-bearing debt combined. It shares the same measurement scope as ROIC: one reflects the cost of invested capital, while the other reflects the corresponding return. Therefore,

*Economic Value Added = (ROIC - WACC) x Invested Capital (IC) = NOPAT - (IC x WACC).*

### Profit Growth

ROIC is also related to growth. In the absence of new external financing, and assuming the company's operating efficiency remains unchanged, ROIC represents the maximum achievable profit growth rate. Dividends paid to shareholders reduce the funds available for reinvestment, correspondingly lowering the company's internally generated profit growth rate.

*Operating Profit Growth Rate = ROIC x (1 - Payout Ratio)*

When considering the dividend payout ratio, one must account for not only the company's future investment needs but also whether the ROIC on new investments can exceed WACC. If the return is inadequate, the result is growth in revenue without growth in profit — a type of expansion that may harm shareholder interests.

## ROIC and Strategic Analysis

Michael Porter's classic competitive strategies come in three forms: differentiation, cost leadership, and integration. Differentiation means a company can price its products or services at a premium relative to competitors. Cost leadership means a company can offer its products at relatively lower prices. Similar to DuPont analysis, decomposing a company's ROIC can reveal its underlying strategic choices.

*ROIC = NOPAT / Revenue x Revenue / Invested Capital*

Here, NOPAT / Revenue measures the operating profit margin, and Revenue / Invested Capital measures capital turnover efficiency.

Very few companies simultaneously achieve both a high operating profit margin and a high capital turnover ratio. If a company has a high operating profit margin, the focus of strategic analysis should be on differentiation. If the company's high returns come from high turnover, the strategic focus should be on cost leadership.
