## What Is the Margin of Safety

Benjamin Graham introduced the core concept of "margin of safety" in *The Intelligent Investor*. Simply put: **Only buy when the price is significantly below intrinsic value.**

Suppose your DCF analysis suggests a company is worth $100. You should not buy at $95, but instead wait until the price falls to $60-70. That 30%-40% difference is your margin of safety.

## Why We Need a Margin of Safety

### 1. Our Valuations Are Always Imprecise

No one can precisely calculate a company's intrinsic value. Our projections of future cash flows, assumptions about growth rates, and choices of discount rates may all contain errors. The margin of safety provides a buffer for these deviations.

### 2. The Future Is Full of Uncertainty

Black swan events, industry disruption, management mistakes — the future always holds risks we cannot foresee. A sufficiently large margin of safety helps us avoid losses even when the unexpected occurs.

### 3. Reducing the Probability of Permanent Capital Loss

The first rule of investing is don't lose money. The second rule is to remember the first. The margin of safety is the most reliable way to achieve this goal.

## How to Determine the Size of the Margin of Safety

The margin of safety should be proportional to uncertainty:

| Company Type | Suggested Margin |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Stable consumer staples leader | 20-30% |
| Cyclical industry company | 30-40% |
| High growth but unprofitable | 40-50%+ |
| Turnaround situation | 50%+ |

## Limitations of the Margin of Safety

The margin of safety is not a cure-all:

- **Value traps**: Some companies appear cheap, but their value is being continuously destroyed (e.g., disrupted traditional industries)
- **Opportunity cost**: Excessive pursuit of margin of safety may cause you to miss buying excellent companies at reasonable prices
- **Qualitative judgment**: The margin of safety is built upon a reasonable estimate of intrinsic value. If the valuation itself is wrong, no margin of safety is meaningful

The key is: first make sure you understand the business, then discuss the margin of safety.
