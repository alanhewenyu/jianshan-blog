WeChat has become an integral part of many people's work lives. When collaboration is needed, you can instantly create a group chat -- it's undeniably convenient. But this convenience comes with a clear downside: the boundary between work and personal life becomes blurred. Coming home after work, opening WeChat, and seeing work-related messages can be a source of anxiety.

In its early days, WeChat was essentially an email product at its core. Its creator, Allen Zhang (Zhang Xiaolong), built his career developing email -- he was the man behind Foxmail. However, WeChat was never originally designed to be a workplace tool. Instead, it leveraged the 4G era to offer instant voice and video communication.

One key reason many Chinese companies rely on WeChat for work communication is that China never went through a widespread email adoption phase. We leaped straight into the era of instant messaging. Unlike in Western countries where email became ubiquitous, many people in China didn't even own a computer, let alone have much need for an email account.

This appears to be a distinctly Chinese form of leapfrog development. While Western nations progressed from A to B to C, China jumped directly from A to C. But is C necessarily more advanced than B? Not always. Take workplace communication as an example -- email has its own unique advantages.

Email may be slower, but it creates a clear separation between work and personal life, free from the constant pressure of instant messaging. Consider this: when you visit a doctor, you might think how convenient it would be to add them on WeChat so you can reach out anytime. But from the doctor's perspective, this would be rather awkward. In fact, we've leapfrogged in this area too -- platforms like Haodf (Good Doctor Online) not only provide communication channels but also enable doctors to offer online consultations as a service.

Leapfrog development is a form of late-mover advantage. This concept should be familiar to most: when developing nations begin their industrialization, they can directly adopt mature technologies and business models from developed countries. There's no need to start from scratch, which saves enormous R&D and trial-and-error costs, enabling faster growth. Over the past several decades, China has enjoyed this late-mover advantage -- not only developing faster, but achieving true leapfrog development.

There are many other examples of such leapfrog development. Alipay and WeChat Pay, for instance, gave people the convenience of cashless payments. In many foreign countries, cashless payments are still far less prevalent than in China. A major reason behind this is that China never went through an era of widespread bank card and credit card adoption.

Another example is the rapid growth of mobile internet over the past decade or so, which produced a wave of companies that secured their ticket to ride and reaped the dividends -- Meituan and Douyin (TikTok) being the most prominent cases. The key driver was that many people in China skipped the PC era entirely and jumped straight into the age of 4G and smartphones. This was especially true in lower-tier markets, where PCs were not only expensive but also came with a steep learning curve -- the advantages of smartphones were self-evident.

Then there are new energy vehicles. China has the world's largest NEV market. Many people may have never driven a traditional gasoline car before entering the age of smart electric vehicles. Without old habits to break, people are more willing to embrace new things. This is a crucial reason why NEVs have grown so rapidly in China.

From these examples, we can see that leapfrog development is, on the whole, a positive phenomenon -- it has put China at the forefront in many areas. But tradition isn't necessarily a bad thing either; email communication is a case in point. Which approach is better depends on each individual's preferred way of living and working. What matters most is maintaining an open mind, understanding differences, and thinking about the reasons behind them.
