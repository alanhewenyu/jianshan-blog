## Starting with the Vanguard S&P 500 Index Fund

Vanguard is a world-renowned asset management firm, well known for offering low-cost index funds. John Bogle championed the philosophy of index investing when he founded Vanguard, creating the first index fund tracking the S&P 500.

The innovation of the Vanguard S&P 500 Index Fund lies in offering a simple, low-cost, broadly diversified investment vehicle. It broke from the traditional actively managed fund model and became the cornerstone of passive management and index investing.

The Vanguard S&P 500 Index Fund charges an expense ratio of just 0.02%, making it one of the best options available for investors looking to buy a U.S. index fund. Buffett himself recommended this fund to non-professional investors in his 2013 letter to shareholders:

> "The goal of the non-professional should not be to pick winners — neither he nor his 'helpers' can do that — but should rather be to own a cross-section of businesses that in aggregate are bound to do well. A low-cost S&P 500 index fund will achieve this goal." That is the "what" for non-professional investors.
>

> "The 'when' is also important. The main danger is that the timid or beginning investor will enter at a time of extreme exuberance and then become disillusioned when paper losses occur. (Remember Barton Biggs's observation: 'A bull market is like sex. It feels best just before it ends.') The antidote to that risk is to accumulate shares over a long period and never sell when the news is bad or the stock has run up substantially."
>

> "My money will be in index funds, so let me add a few more words on this: What I advise here is essentially identical to what I've put in my will. A bequest will place cash into a trust for my wife. (I have to use cash for individual bequests, because all of my Berkshire shares will be fully distributed to certain philanthropic organizations over the ten years following the closing of my estate.) My instructions to the trustee could not be more simple: Put 10% of the cash in short-term government bonds and 90% in a very low-cost S&P 500 index fund. (I suggest Vanguard's.) I believe the trust's long-term results from this policy will be superior to those attained by most investors — whether pension funds, institutions, or individuals — who employ high-fee managers."
>

## Why Active Funds Struggle to Beat Index Funds

Bogle was well known for his persistent advocacy — through media appearances and extensive writing — that index funds outperform traditional actively managed mutual funds. His book *The Little Book of Common Sense Investing* embodies his investment philosophy: **choose low-cost index funds, and do not overestimate the past performance of actively managed funds.**

Why is it so hard to make money with actively managed funds? Bogle argued that investors first pay a heavy timing penalty. When stocks offer good value, investors allocate too little to equity funds. Then, driven by the optimism and greed that characterized the era (referring to the 1980s and 1990s), and lured by mutual fund marketers as bull markets approached their peaks, they pour too much of their savings into equity funds.

This mirrors the current situation in China's domestic market remarkably well. When the market hits rock bottom — precisely when investment value emerges — it is also when equity funds are hardest to sell. On the other hand, most funds' outstanding track records prove difficult to sustain; the making of star fund managers is often attributable to the luck bestowed by a particular era or market cycle.

Second, investors pay a selection penalty — not only entering the market at the wrong time, but also choosing the wrong funds. These funds had delivered outstanding historical performance, but as we will soon see, they subsequently suffered steep declines. Why? Simply because high fund returns tend to revert to, or even fall below, the mean.

Mean reversion is a common market phenomenon. For example, both economic activity and stock price movements exhibit this characteristic, similar to the concept behind the Merrill Lynch Investment Clock. It is precisely because of being embedded within market cycles that the performance of actively managed funds rarely escapes the fate of mean reversion.

## **Do Not Overestimate Active Funds' Past Performance**

Bogle therefore offers this advice in his book: when screening for outstanding funds based on historical performance, always maintain a healthy skepticism. Before making an investment decision, ask yourself the following questions:

(1) How long can the fund manager, their team, and their strategy remain in place?

(2) As the fund grows in size, can it sustain the same results it achieved when it was smaller?

(3) To what extent have high expense ratios and/or high portfolio turnover rates weighed on the fund's performance — or conversely, have low fees and low turnover enhanced it?

(4) Will the stock market continue to favor the types of stocks that have been central to the fund manager's investment style?
