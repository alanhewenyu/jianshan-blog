The P/E ratio and EV/EBITDA multiple are the most commonly used relative valuation multiples among investors. On the numerator side, both P (price) and EV (enterprise value) reflect the present value of a company's future relevant cash flows. However, the denominator captures only the company's most recent earnings (E in P/E) or a cash flow proxy (EBITDA in EV/EBITDA).

Comparing a long-term-oriented numerator with a short-term denominator may seem unreasonable at first glance. In practice, this approach implicitly assumes that near-term E or EBITDA is representative of the future, particularly under a given growth rate. Take P/E as an example: its reciprocal represents the time required to recoup an investment at the current earnings level. Companies in a high-growth phase typically command higher P/E multiples because rapid growth shortens the payback period significantly.

However, it is dangerous to take this assumption of reasonableness for granted. In reality, short-term earnings (E) or cash flow proxies (EBITDA) do not always reflect the future.

## Economies of Scale

The magnitude of economies of scale varies across industries and is closely tied to the level of fixed assets. A company's operating leverage can be used to measure its economies of scale, referring to the sensitivity of profit changes relative to revenue changes. When fixed assets are substantial, revenue growth will significantly reduce the per-unit fixed cost of products, thereby materially improving the company's operating profit margin.

As discussed in previous articles, revenue growth and operating profit margin are the two core drivers of company valuation. As revenue grows, economies of scale can enhance operating profit margins, which in turn drive valuation higher. However, the denominators in relative valuation -- E or EBITDA -- struggle to capture differences in economies of scale across companies and industries.

Consider the automotive industry as an example. It is a capital-intensive sector with pronounced economies of scale. This means that as sales volumes increase, a company can quickly swing from losses to profitability, with operating margins improving steadily. Conversely, declining sales volumes can rapidly erode operating margins -- a challenge Tesla is currently facing. In such cases, current earnings (E) are a poor proxy for the future, making it difficult to identify an appropriate P/E multiple.

## Non-Recurring Items

In both of these metrics, the denominators E and EBITDA do not account for adjustments related to non-recurring items. Non-recurring items, as the name implies, are not directly related to the company's normal business operations, or, while related to normal operations, are special or infrequent in nature, and thus distort users' assessment of the company's operating performance and profitability. Clearly, short-term non-recurring items are not reflective of the future.

All A-share listed companies are required to disclose non-recurring gains and losses, a mandatory information disclosure requirement imposed by the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC). Common non-recurring items include gains or losses from the disposal of long-term assets, gains from financial asset disposals by non-financial enterprises, and government subsidies.

Similarly, U.S. listed companies typically disclose non-GAAP earnings to eliminate the effects of non-recurring items, though unlike China's disclosure requirements, there is no standardized set of rules. In addition, U.S. listed companies often further exclude non-cash charges such as depreciation and amortization and stock-based compensation expenses.

However, the P/E ratios commonly displayed on stock trading platforms typically do not exclude non-recurring items. When a P/E ratio appears anomalous, it is important to scrutinize the composition of net income attributable to the parent company. Ideally, one should recalculate using net income after excluding non-recurring items, or non-GAAP net income attributable to the parent company.

## The Depreciation Factor

EBITDA is a company's earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) plus depreciation and amortization (DA). Because it does not deduct financing costs and adds back non-cash charges, EBITDA is often used as a proxy for operating cash flow. However, EBITDA does not represent free cash flow to the firm (FCFF) -- deriving FCFF requires further deducting capital expenditures, i.e., reinvestment.

When using the EV/EBITDA multiple for valuation, one should pay close attention to how much of EBITDA is EBIT versus DA. The reason is that although DA is a non-cash expense, it implicitly signals the level of capital expenditure required for a company to maintain its operations. As discussed in the previous article on calculating free cash flow, if we view "capital expenditure minus depreciation" as a whole, capital expenditure must first cover the maintenance of existing assets, and only the portion exceeding depreciation represents net new reinvestment.

Therefore, for two companies with the same current EBITDA, the one with higher EBIT will have more distributable cash flow after accounting for future asset maintenance costs, and thus should be valued higher. This illustrates that EBITDA does not adequately capture a company's free cash flow.

How can we measure the relative differences in DA across companies and industries? One useful metric is the ratio of EBITDA to EBIT, also known as the depreciation factor. The depreciation factor reflects an industry's capital intensity and is influenced by a company's depreciation policy and M&A activity. Based on U.S. listed company data, the overall depreciation factor is approximately 1.4x, with significant variation across industries -- the consumer sector has a depreciation factor of around 1.3x, while utilities reach approximately 1.8x.

Clearly, industries with a lower depreciation factor tend to be more asset-light. Compared to asset-heavy industries, asset-light industries require less invested capital (IC) and achieve higher ROIC. This means that, given the same WACC, asset-light industries have a stronger capacity for value creation. Consequently, for a given growth rate, companies with a lower depreciation factor will command a higher EV/EBITDA valuation multiple than those with a higher depreciation factor.

## P/E vs. EV/EBITDA

For profitable companies, P/E is almost always higher than EV/EBITDA. Suppose a company has no debt, so that P equals EV. Meanwhile, E will be lower than EBITDA because it is an after-tax figure and does not add back interest and depreciation. Since the numerators are the same but the denominator is smaller, the P/E multiple will exceed the EV/EBITDA multiple.

P/E is effectively a leveraged ratio, since the stock price reflects equity value and earnings per share is calculated after deducting interest expense. In the EV/EBITDA multiple, EV includes both equity and debt value, and EBITDA is EBIT plus depreciation and amortization (DA), with no deduction for interest expense. Thus, EV/EBITDA is free from the effects of financial leverage.

For profitable companies, increasing financial leverage means that return on equity (ROE) will be boosted, which in turn raises the P/E multiple -- provided, of course, that the company does not incur liquidity or even bankruptcy risk from excessive leverage.

Both P/E and EV/EBITDA are important tools for investors to evaluate and compare company values, but they should not be regarded as absolute benchmarks. When using these ratios, investors must consider the specific circumstances of each company and the broader industry context to form a comprehensive judgment.
