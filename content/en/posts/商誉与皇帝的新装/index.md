On a Thursday evening in February 2019, American food giant Kraft Heinz released a devastating annual report after the close: $15.4 billion in goodwill and brand asset impairments. This meant that a significant portion of the brand synergies and cost savings touted when Kraft and Heinz merged four years earlier had never materialized. The next day, the stock plunged 27.5%. The company also disclosed that it had received an SEC subpoena.

Warren Buffett, who held a 27% stake, later admitted: "I was wrong in paying too much for Kraft."

Shift the lens to China. In 2024, the century-old Shanghai Jahwa delivered the worst report card in its two-decade-plus history as a public company — its first annual loss, totaling RMB 833 million. The core reason was a RMB 610 million goodwill impairment from Tommee Tippee, the British infant care brand acquired for roughly RMB 2 billion in 2017. Interestingly, Tommee Tippee had massively exceeded its earnout targets in 2019. But net profit suddenly dropped 70% in 2023, and in 2024 the business swung to a loss exceeding RMB 700 million. The Shanghai Stock Exchange's inquiry letter was blunt: are you taking a "big bath"?

Kraft Heinz and Shanghai Jahwa — one in New York, the other in Shanghai; one a global consumer products giant, the other a leading Chinese personal care company. But they stumbled over the same thing: **goodwill**.

As of 2021, total goodwill on the balance sheets of S&P 500 companies stood at approximately $3.6 trillion — far exceeding these companies' total cash holdings. If you stripped goodwill from net assets, 88 index constituents would have negative book value. On the A-share side, total goodwill peaked at around RMB 1.45 trillion in 2018 and still stood at approximately RMB 1.25 trillion by 2023.

Under current accounting standards, the acquisition premium is initially recognized on the balance sheet as an asset — goodwill. The question is: when it comes to subsequent measurement, **how should the overpayment from an acquisition be absorbed on the books? Should it be amortized year by year, or left sitting there until something goes wrong and then impaired?**

This is the multiple-choice question that global accounting standard-setters, investors, corporate management, and auditors have debated for twenty-five years — with no resolution in sight. This article tells the story behind that debate.

---

## What Exactly Is Goodwill?

You pay 1 billion to acquire a company whose identifiable net assets have a fair value of only 600 million. The extra 400 million is goodwill.

Goodwill is not a made-up number. It represents the price you are paying for the "invisible things" about that company — brand power, customer relationships, management capability, expected synergies. You are willing to pay the extra 400 million because you believe the company as a whole is worth more than the simple sum of its parts.

The problem comes afterward: **once the acquisition is complete, what do you do with that 400 million?**

Historically, there have been two main approaches.

**The first is amortization** — like depreciating a fixed asset, you write down the goodwill to zero over a set period (say, 10 or 20 years). The logic is intuitive: the premium you paid corresponds to a value that will diminish over time, so it should be gradually recognized as an expense.

**The second is impairment testing** — goodwill does not decrease automatically, but at least once a year you test whether the business you acquired is still worth the price you paid. If it is not, you take a one-time write-down. If it is, the goodwill stays on the books.

These two methods may look like a mere technical difference, but they conceal a deeper philosophical divide: **is goodwill a naturally wasting asset, or a form of value that can persist indefinitely as long as the business operates normally?**

How you answer that question determines which side you take in the global battle that follows.

## A Twenty-Five-Year History of Standard-Setting

#### The Turning Point: 2001 — America Abolishes Amortization

Before 2001, virtually every jurisdiction in the world required goodwill amortization. In the United States, the maximum period was 40 years.

What was the problem? The tech bubble of the late 1990s spawned a wave of high-premium acquisitions. Asset-light technology companies had very little in the way of "identifiable net assets," so the vast majority of the purchase price ended up as goodwill. Under the old rules, annual amortization produced persistent accounting losses — even when the business was performing well.

Corporate America was up in arms.

In June 2001, the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) made a historic decision: abolish goodwill amortization and replace it with at least annual impairment testing. In its Basis for Conclusions, FASB wrote, in essence: **straight-line amortization of goodwill over an arbitrary period does not reflect economic reality and therefore does not provide useful information.**

But there is a little-noticed detail. According to academic analysis of the comment letters FASB received, **the majority of respondents actually opposed the impairment-only model** — especially audit firms, which broadly considered impairment testing insufficiently reliable for practical application.

In other words, FASB's 2001 decision was made against the majority view from the start.

#### The Global Follow-On: 2004–2006

FASB's decision triggered a chain reaction. In 2004, the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) issued a new business combinations standard, IFRS 3, converging with the U.S. approach and adopting the impairment model. In 2006, China followed suit in its new accounting standards — prior to that, Chinese GAAP had treated goodwill as an intangible asset amortized over a period generally not exceeding 10 years.

By this point, the world's three major accounting frameworks — U.S. GAAP, International IFRS, and Chinese standards — had all entered the "impairment era."

The sole exception was Japan. Japanese GAAP has always retained goodwill amortization — straight-line over no more than 20 years, layered on top of impairment testing. This is a "belt and suspenders" hybrid model. Former IASB Chairman Hans Hoogervorst once made a vivid remark: "In Japanese GAAP, goodwill amortization is still alive. Many Japanese stakeholders like the prudence of goodwill amortization."

#### "Too Little, Too Late" — The Achilles' Heel of the Impairment Model

After more than a decade in operation, a structural flaw in the impairment model became increasingly hard to ignore.

If the future cash flow value of the acquired assets genuinely exceeds the purchase price, there is theoretically no impairment issue. But the problem is that impairment testing does not distinguish between "the value you originally paid for" and "the new value the subsidiary has created on its own since the acquisition." The brands, customer base, and team you bought may already be declining, but the subsidiary's organic growth has filled the hole. The result: **investors cannot tell from the financial statements whether the acquisition was a success or a failure — subsequent growth drowns out the signal.**

The IASB calls this phenomenon "headroom" — the new value created internally after the acquisition acts as a buffer for the purchased goodwill in impairment tests. The IASB itself has acknowledged that this buffering effect means impairment testing is almost inevitably "too little, too late" — not because the accounting treatment is wrong, but because the mechanism fails to hold management accountable for acquisition decisions effectively.

Beyond this structural issue, impairment testing faces a more practical execution problem: excessive management discretion. The core of impairment testing is estimating the "recoverable amount" of the acquired business, which relies on forecasts of future cash flows and the choice of discount rate — both of which are management's call. Kraft Heinz is a textbook case. After the 2015 merger, 3G Capital's aggressive cost-cutting strategy maintained the appearance of healthy margins in the short term, but was actually eroding brand value and customer relationships. Management used optimistic growth projections and a low discount rate in its impairment tests, and this time bomb sat quietly on the balance sheet for three years. It was not until 2019 that the $15.4 billion impairment finally erupted — not because the problems had just emerged, but because management could no longer justify optimistic assumptions.

The structural buffering effect combined with management's optimism bias at the execution level — these two factors together gave critics of the impairment model all the ammunition they needed: **if impairment testing is so easy to circumvent and delay, why not simply bring back amortization?**

#### The Great Global Rethink: Who Won?

The "too little, too late" problem prompted all parties around the world to reconsider: should amortization be brought back?

From 2013 to 2025, FASB and IASB each went through years of internal deliberation, public consultation, and repeated votes. The final outcome: **both retained the impairment model**, without restoring amortization.

But the path to that result was full of drama.

On the FASB side, Chairman Richard Jones publicly stated in 2021 that a majority of the board favored restoring amortization — with a default period of 10 years and a maximum of 25. But CFA Institute, representing the investor community, pushed back forcefully, and FASB ultimately shelved the project in 2022.

At the IASB, a global consultation in 2020 drew approximately 300 comment letters. An interesting finding: **the number of responses supporting a return to amortization clearly exceeded those supporting the status quo.** But the IASB argued that the direction of standard-setting should not be determined by a simple headcount — "both sides have thoughtful, evidence-supported views that are unlikely to be reconciled." The November 2022 vote to retain impairment passed narrowly, 8 to 6. In February 2025, a subsequent vote unanimously rejected restoring amortization.

The irony is striking: when FASB abolished amortization in 2001, it went against the majority of comment letters; when it tried to restore amortization in 2022, it was blocked by investors. Neither decision was a simple majority rule. The lesson from this story: **standard-setting is never a democratic vote — it is an equilibrium of competing interests.**

## Why Did Investors Side with Impairment?

You might find this puzzling: isn't amortization more prudent? Shouldn't investors prefer prudence?

CFA Institute conducted a large-scale survey in 2020 — 1,607 CFA charterholders (research analysts and portfolio managers) responded. Key findings:

- 58% supported retaining impairment testing over restoring amortization
- 94% wanted a globally uniform approach
- 80% believed impairment testing holds management accountable for acquisition decisions
- 73% were willing to bear the cost of impairment testing

Viewed from the other side, roughly 42% of investors actually leaned toward amortization — this was not a landslide; the impairment camp held only a modest edge. But because CFA Institute as an organization firmly championed impairment, that 58% was amplified into a unified investor position.

CFA Institute's Sandy Peters offered a particularly incisive characterization: she called amortization "the zero information approach."

What does that mean? If you switch to amortization, mechanically deducting a fixed amount each year, investors lose the ability to distinguish "good acquisitions" from "bad acquisitions." A management team skilled at integrating acquisitions and one that overpays look exactly the same on the income statement — both are amortizing.

The impairment model may have its "too little, too late" problem, but when a large impairment actually hits, it sends a very clear signal: **management is admitting that the acquisition overpaid or that integration failed.** That signal has real value for investment decisions.

What investors are actually dissatisfied with is not the impairment model itself, but the **quality of its execution** — 70% of survey respondents said current goodwill and impairment disclosures are "not useful enough." What they want to see is: what valuation models and assumptions did management use? What are the key performance indicators for the acquired business post-deal? How does the board assess whether the acquisition met expectations?

In short, investors do not want goodwill to be "ground down" — they want to be able to **see the real performance of acquisitions**.

## Management's Convenient Calculus

Investors support retaining impairment testing because the impairment signal has decision-making value. But here is the ironic truth: **corporate management is also happy to see the impairment model survive — for entirely different reasons.**

Investors value the signal when impairment occurs, but what management values is precisely the discretion that impairment testing affords. Impairment testing gives management a "legitimate" option: as long as I can argue that future cash flows are sufficiently optimistic, goodwill does not need to be touched.

In a widely cited study, Harvard Business School's Ramanna and MIT's Watts examined companies where market signals indicated goodwill should be impaired (stock price trading below book value for extended periods) but management chose not to impair.

The finding was stark: **approximately 70% of these companies chose not to impair.**

And what was this decision correlated with? Not with a benign motivation of management "conveying private information," but rather with CEO compensation, CEO tenure, debt covenant constraints, and other agency cost variables.

In other words, impairment testing grants management enormous discretion, and a meaningful number of managers do exploit this discretion to delay bad news.

Revisit the Shanghai Jahwa case. Tommee Tippee's net profit had already dropped 70% to RMB 70.36 million in 2023, yet management chose not to impair — on the grounds that "based on historical experience and the then-current assessment of market trends, growth could be restored through increased brand spending." It was not until 2024, when the subsidiary swung to a loss exceeding RMB 700 million, that management "reformulated future strategy" and recognized the RMB 610 million impairment.

This sequence perfectly illustrates the core tension of the impairment model: **when should impairment be recognized? Is an optimistic forecast a reasonable business judgment or an excuse for delaying bad news?**

The answer depends on whom you ask. Management will say, "We had reasonable grounds to believe the business could recover." Investors will say, "You should have warned us much earlier." And standard-setters stand in the middle, offending neither side.

Buffett offered a biting commentary on this dynamic: "If a CEO is enthused about a particularly foolish acquisition, both his internal staff and his outside advisors will come up with whatever projections are needed to justify his stance. Only in fairy tales are emperors told that they are naked."

Impairment testing was supposed to play the role of the child who tells the truth. But in reality, the CEO decides, the advisors endorse, the auditors sign off, and the impairment test passes — no one in the chain has sufficient incentive to speak up.

## China's Unique Story

#### 2019: An Expert Discussion That Shook the Market

On January 7, 2019, China's Accounting Standards Committee under the Ministry of Finance published a bulletin: after soliciting advisory opinions on the IASB's "Goodwill and Its Impairment" agenda item, **a majority of the advisory committee members supported restoring goodwill amortization.**

The rationale: goodwill's value is inherently wasting; amortization would reflect this consumption more promptly and avoid the situation where a sudden impairment to zero retroactively distorts prior years' reported performance.

The market reaction was immediate and violent. Bear in mind that late 2018 was the peak of A-share goodwill blow-ups — on January 31 alone, 230 companies announced expected losses, totaling nearly RMB 200 billion. The market was on edge about "RMB 1.45 trillion in goodwill land mines."

Against this backdrop, the news that "goodwill may switch to amortization" was interpreted in every possible way. Optimists argued amortization was a major positive — it would "trade time for space" and avoid concentrated blow-ups. Pessimists countered: if amortized over 10 years, the A-share market would have to absorb RMB 145 billion in goodwill expense annually; CITIC Securities estimated that only an amortization period of 30 years or longer would produce a marginal improvement in aggregate A-share earnings.

**The very next day (January 8), the Standards Committee issued an emergency clarification: these were merely expert discussion opinions, and the accounting standards for goodwill had not changed.**

In the span of a single day — from "major standard overhaul" to "everything stays the same" — the market's emotional rollercoaster left a lasting impression.

To this day, Chinese standards continue to maintain the impairment model, staying converged with international practice.

#### The Thundering Sound of A-Share Goodwill Blow-Ups

The 2014–2015 bull market and M&A frenzy planted the seeds for the goodwill blow-ups that followed. The typical pattern: listed companies acquired asset-light targets at high premiums during the bull market (media, gaming, and pharmaceuticals were the hardest-hit sectors), signed three-year earnout agreements, the targets barely met or possibly manipulated earnings during the earnout period, and the moment the earnout expired, performance collapsed and goodwill impairments erupted en masse.

But if you think A-share goodwill blow-ups are simply about "overpaying" or "failed integration," you are only seeing the surface. In a significant number of cases, the transactions were never genuine arm's-length deals from the start — controlling shareholders or de facto controllers used high-premium acquisitions of their own related-party assets to siphon money from the listed company into their own pockets. Goodwill was merely the accounting trail this tunneling left on the balance sheet. The typical playbook: the controller incubates assets outside the listed entity, injects them at inflated prices, the target barely meets earnout targets during the lock-up period, and after expiry performance collapses, with minority shareholders ultimately footing the bill for the inflated goodwill. In these deals, the emperor not only knew he had no clothes — he never intended to wear any.

So when evaluating goodwill on the A-share market, you must ask not only "was this acquisition worth it?" but a more fundamental question: **was this acquisition a genuine arm's-length transaction?**

The most concentrated wave of blow-ups came during the 2018–2019 reporting season. At that time, approximately 160 A-share companies had goodwill exceeding 50% of net assets, with 21 exceeding 100%. By 2023, after several years of impairment digestion, total A-share goodwill had declined from its RMB 1.45 trillion peak to approximately RMB 1.25 trillion (per academic data and public statistics), but the risk was far from cleared — 46 companies still carried goodwill exceeding RMB 5 billion.

And goodwill blow-ups did not fade away after that initial wave. In 2023, Ninestar, a leading printer manufacturer, recognized approximately RMB 7.9 billion in goodwill impairment on its acquisition of U.S.-based Lexmark International, resulting in a massive full-year loss. In 2024, the Shanghai Jahwa–Tommee Tippee case unfolded as described above. As of January 2026, roughly 88 companies had warned that they would report their first loss in three years for 2025, with goodwill impairment cited repeatedly.

This is a persistent structural problem, not a passing storm.

## How Should Investors Approach Goodwill?

**First, goodwill itself is not a bad thing.** Excellent acquisitions can create tremendous value — many of Berkshire Hathaway's subsidiaries carry goodwill on the books. The issue is not that goodwill exists, but whether it corresponds to genuine excess earning power.

**Second, focus on proportions, not absolute amounts.** PetroChina's goodwill exceeds RMB 40 billion, but that is only 1.7% of total assets — a manageable risk. If goodwill represents an outsized share of total assets, you should exercise caution and vigilance when valuing the company.

Take Jin Jiang International Hotels as an example. This hotel group — the world's largest by room count — accumulated approximately RMB 11.6 billion in goodwill through acquisitions of Louvre Hotels (RMB 8.85 billion), Plateno, Vienna, and Radisson (approximately $2 billion), representing roughly 25% of total assets. Full-year 2025 net profit was RMB 925 million. A simple stress test: if goodwill were impaired by 30% — around RMB 3.5 billion — that would wipe out nearly four years of net profit in a single stroke. Jin Jiang has not recognized any large impairments to date, and the hotel business is indeed still expanding, but as an investor you need to maintain a clear-eyed awareness of that ratio.

**Third, goodwill distorts return on capital.** The real money spent on acquisitions increases invested capital (whether paid in cash, funded by debt, or financed through share issuance), and goodwill sits on the balance sheet as part of total assets. For a company that has expanded through heavy M&A, the inflated invested capital base can make ROIC look unimpressive. If you strip goodwill from invested capital to calculate "operating ROIC," you may find the underlying business is actually quite profitable. But the reverse also holds: a company whose ROIC looks reasonable on paper may, if goodwill is a large proportion, see its true capital returns fall far short of expectations once a major impairment hits earnings. For high-goodwill companies, looking at ROIC both with and without goodwill gives you a more complete picture.

## Conclusion: A Question with No Right Answer

Goodwill amortization versus impairment is, at its core, two philosophies of how accounting standards should handle uncertainty.

The amortization camp's logic: we are not sure when goodwill will disappear, but we are sure it eventually will, so we might as well write it down steadily each year. This is a form of **prudence that accepts being vaguely right**.

The impairment camp's logic: goodwill reflects a potentially perpetual excess earning power; as long as that power endures, it should not be artificially reduced. Only when it is genuinely impaired should the loss be recognized in a single stroke. This is a form of **precision that respects economic reality**.

Both logics have merit, and both carry costs. The cost of amortization is low information content — the fixed annual deduction tells you nothing. The cost of impairment is distorted execution — management's optimism bias turns impairment testing into a game of manipulable numbers.

In a 2022 internal document, the IASB offered a remarkably candid summary, writing in essence: "The conceptual debate over the most appropriate model for the subsequent accounting for goodwill continues. Both sides have thoughtful, evidence-backed views. These views reflect different understandings of the nature of goodwill and are unlikely to be reconciled."

That may be the most honest sentence you will find in any standard-setting document.

For investors, the good news is that both the IASB and FASB are pushing for improved disclosures — requiring companies to report post-acquisition actual performance versus management's original objectives, key performance indicators, and more. If these disclosure requirements ultimately take effect, investors may not get an early warning of impairment, but at least they will be able to figure it out for themselves.

And that, perhaps, is the most useful thing for investors: **not having the standards make the judgment for you, but giving you enough information to make the judgment yourself.**
