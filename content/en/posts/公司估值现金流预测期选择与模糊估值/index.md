When forecasting a company's future cash flows, the first question to address is the length of the forecast period. The standard approach divides this into two stages: the explicit forecast period and the terminal (perpetuity) period. During the explicit forecast period, each financial statement line item needs to be projected individually, such as gross margin, selling expenses, accounts receivable, and inventory. For the terminal period, the terminal value formula can be applied for a straightforward calculation.

The explicit forecast period is typically five years, though a ten-year horizon is also common. How should one choose the appropriate length? The answer fundamentally depends on the characteristics of the company's industry and its stage in the business lifecycle.

Industries undergoing rapid change, companies in their growth phase, or businesses experiencing significant transformations generally require a longer explicit forecast period. In contrast, stable, mature companies can work with a shorter one.

It is worth noting that the above assumes the company will operate as a going concern indefinitely. If the business has a finite operating life -- for example, toll roads held within listed REITs, where the underlying asset is essentially a toll collection right for a limited period that must be returned to the government upon expiration -- then the concept of a terminal period does not apply.

## Growth-Stage Companies

A shorter explicit forecast period, such as five years, often leads to a significant undervaluation of the company. This happens because the company is likely to still be growing at a rate well above the long-term perpetuity growth rate assumption at the end of year five, and jumping straight into the terminal period leaves no room for a transition.

As a general rule, a ten-year explicit forecast period is recommended. However, a longer forecast horizon introduces its own challenge: it is extremely difficult to predict individual financial statement items ten years into the future. To simplify the model and avoid the trap of precise but wrong projections -- garbage in, garbage out -- a hybrid approach can be used:

- Five years of detailed forecasting, involving full balance sheet and income statement projections with as many cross-references between line items as possible;
- Five years of simplified forecasting, focusing on a few key variables such as revenue growth, operating margin, and capital turnover. This simplified approach lets you concentrate on the company's long-term economics, freeing you from excessive detail.

## Mature Companies

Can mature companies simply be valued using the terminal value formula alone? Not quite. Although a mature company has entered a stable growth phase, its current stable growth rate may still exceed the long-term perpetuity growth rate -- meaning a two-stage framework is still appropriate.

Compared to growth-stage companies, the explicit forecast period for mature companies can be shortened to five years, and it is advisable to adopt the simplified forecasting approach directly, skipping detailed financial statement projections and focusing instead on core variables like revenue growth and operating margin. After the five-year explicit forecast period, another five years can be used to gradually transition the core variables toward the terminal period targets.

## The Wisdom of Approximate Valuation

I call this valuation approach for mature companies "approximate valuation." As Warren Buffett put it, "It is better to be approximately right than precisely wrong." While this method is best suited for mature companies, it often produces good results for growth-stage companies as well.

For the average investor, even a five-year detailed financial statement forecast involves a substantial amount of work. Valuation is an art -- there is no such thing as precision. The beauty of approximate valuation lies in lowering the barrier to entry while capturing the core variables. This approach allows investors to identify the margin of safety more efficiently and with less effort.
