## Intrinsic Value

When reading Buffett's letters to shareholders, one term appears repeatedly: the intrinsic value of a stock. Clearly, intrinsic value is the benchmark Buffett uses to determine whether a stock is worth investing in. So what exactly is intrinsic value, and how should it be calculated?

In his 1994 letter to shareholders, Buffett explained what he has always meant by intrinsic value:

> "We define intrinsic value as the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life. Anyone calculating intrinsic value necessarily comes up with a highly subjective figure that will change both as estimates of future cash flows are revised and as interest rates move. Despite its fuzziness, however, intrinsic value is all-important and is the only logical way to evaluate the relative attractiveness of investments and businesses."
>

In plain terms, intrinsic value is simply the discounted cash flow (DCF) model that we often talk about. In academic textbooks, this is classified as an absolute valuation method, in contrast to relative valuation methods that rely on comparing metrics like P/E ratios and P/B ratios. In the field of asset appraisal, the DCF-based valuation approach is called the income approach, while its counterpart is the market approach — essentially the relative valuation method.

In the various company research reports from securities firms, you often see multi-year financial statement projections, including cash flow forecasts. The three financial statements are tightly interlinked through accounting logic, making accurate projections quite challenging to master.

## I Would Rather Be Vaguely Right Than Precisely Wrong

Does Buffett build detailed financial projections before making an investment? He has often said that he welcomes investment proposals and only needs a few minutes to decide whether to invest. Clearly, Buffett likely does not perform detailed financial modeling.

This does not mean he disregards cash flows. In fact, Buffett employs what he calls a "vaguely right" approach to make rough cash flow estimates. The underlying reason is, as mentioned above, that discounted cash flow estimation is inherently highly subjective. On the numerator side, cash flow projections require numerous assumptions — one crucial factor being future capital expenditure levels. On the denominator side, the discount rate itself is influenced by changes in the risk-free rate and other factors.

In his 1986 letter to shareholders, Buffett illustrated his cash flow estimation method through a case study and first introduced his valuation philosophy of being vaguely right:

> "If we think through these questions, we can gain some insights about what may be called 'owner earnings.' These represent (a) reported earnings plus (b) depreciation, depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges, less (c) the average annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment, etc. that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit volume. If the business requires additional working capital to maintain its competitive position and unit volume, the increment also should be included in (c)."
>

> "Our owner-earnings equation does not yield the deceptively precise figures provided by GAAP, since (c) must be a guess — and one sometimes very difficult to make. Despite this problem, we consider the owner earnings figure, not the GAAP figure, to be the relevant item for valuation purposes — both for investors in buying stocks and for managers in buying entire businesses. We agree with Keynes's observation: 'I would rather be vaguely right than precisely wrong.'"
>

In his 1993 and 2010 letters to shareholders, Buffett revisited the concept of being vaguely right. For example, in 1993, he used it to critique the academic establishment's reliance on Beta calculations:

> "In their hunger for a single statistic to measure risk, however, they forget a fundamental principle: it is better to be approximately right than precisely wrong. The academic community, however, insisted on giving risk a different definition, treating it as the relative volatility of a stock's price — that is, the degree to which an individual investment fluctuates compared to the market as a whole. Using databases and statistical methods, these scholars could calculate a 'precise' Beta for any stock — representing its historical relative volatility — and then build obscure investment and capital allocation theories around this formula."
>

Being vaguely right is both an investment wisdom and a life philosophy. It teaches us that the barrier to investing is not as high as many believe — ordinary investors can participate in investing too. However, improving at being vaguely right ultimately tests one's ability to enhance self-awareness and judgment.
