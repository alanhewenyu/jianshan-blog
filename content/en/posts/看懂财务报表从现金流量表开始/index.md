Between value investing and company valuation, there is a critical bridge: financial statements.

Accounting is the language of the business world, and financial statements are the concrete expression of that language. In today's digital age, data plays an increasingly important role in business decisions, and everyone should have at least a basic understanding of accounting.

For users of financial statements, it is not necessarily required to learn debits, credits, and journal entries. What matters is knowing how to use the statements. Start by understanding how accounting reflects a company's business and economic activities, grasp the logic behind financial statements, and develop the ability to analyze them.

Accounting, as a language, records the economic activities of a business. It addresses two core issues: recognition and measurement. If we add a third, that would be disclosure.

Recognition is the prerequisite for measurement. "Recognition" addresses the questions of when and how to recognize transactions. "Measurement" addresses the question of how to value them -- sometimes straightforward, sometimes highly complex, depending on the specific economic event. For beginners who simply want to read financial statements, starting with disclosure is a practical approach, setting aside the intricacies of recognition and measurement for the time being.

Regarding disclosure, what is first presented to users are the three primary statements. The balance sheet reflects a company's financial position at a specific point in time; the income statement and the cash flow statement reflect, respectively, the company's financial performance and cash flow situation over a period of time. Accounting policies and notes to the financial statements provide further explanation of the statements.

## The Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement covers three major categories of economic activity: operating, investing, and financing. These three activities provide a comprehensive summary of a company's economic activities and also represent the three core themes of financial management.

If we think of the cash flow statement as a building, operating activities are the roof, while investing and financing activities are the two pillars -- both serving the company's operations.

Let's start with financing activities. A company's operations first need to address the question of funding sources, which can come from equity capital or debt. Financing activities correspond to the right side of the balance sheet -- namely, liabilities and shareholders' equity.

Investing activities reflect the capital investment required for business operations. You need the chicken before you get the egg -- input comes before output. When a company has idle cash, it also faces the challenge of preserving and growing that capital, which generates investment returns and is also part of investing activities.

Operating activities represent the core business of a company, embodying its mission and value. This mission and value are realized through providing products or services that meet societal needs. The key management functions surrounding a company's business are all reflected in operating cash flows: sales, procurement, operations, and human resource management.

## The Income Statement and the Cash Flow Statement

Although the line items on the income statement may look different from those on the cash flow statement, they actually reflect the same three categories of economic activity. Let's revisit an example income statement prepared under IFRS 18 guidance:

A typical income statement, as shown in the example above, usually classifies costs and expenses by function -- such as cost of sales, selling expenses, R&D expenses, and administrative expenses. In comparison, the cash flow statement classifies items more by nature -- for example, it typically reports personnel costs separately. Of course, the income statement can also be classified by nature of expense, and companies may choose their preferred approach.

From the cash flow statement to the income statement, a critical difference lies in accrual accounting. Revenue is recognized when the company has a claim against the customer (accounts receivable), not when cash is received. Expenses are recognized when a liability to the supplier arises (accounts payable), not when cash is paid. Both of these bring future cash receipts and payments forward to the present. Accrual accounting even recognizes certain items that involve no cash outflow at all -- for example, share-based compensation, where employee expenses are recorded even though no cash leaves the company.

In the business world, credit sales and credit purchases are inevitable. Beyond long-term capital investment, companies often need to advance funds -- for instance, accounts receivable and inventory are essentially tied-up capital. At the same time, companies also utilize their downstream suppliers' funds through accounts payable. Working capital (current assets minus current liabilities) reflects the net capital tied up in the business. Therefore, as discussed in previous articles, both working capital and long-term capital investment are components of a company's reinvestment.

From this perspective, accrual accounting clearly provides a better reflection of the economic substance of a business enterprise. Conversely, this is also why non-profit organizations, such as public institutions and charitable organizations, do not need to adopt accrual accounting.

Accrual accounting falls under the "recognition" principle. If accrual accounting gives accounting a degree of forward-looking capability, then in terms of "measurement," the introduction of fair value has brought accounting out of the dusty archives of history and into the spotlight.

Within a company's investing cash flows, beyond the long-term capital expenditures required for operations, there are often purchases and sales of long-term equity investments and financial assets. In the vast majority of cases, these two types of assets are measured at fair value. For unrealized gains and losses, a company may choose to record them in the income statement or directly in shareholders' equity. If recorded in the income statement, they represent non-cash gains or losses -- another major source of difference between the income statement and the cash flow statement.

Financial statements disclose the reconciliation from net income on the income statement to operating cash flow. There are three main reconciling items: changes in working capital, non-cash charges for the period (depreciation and amortization), and non-operating gains and losses (such as investment income). Since the reconciliation targets operating cash flow, investment income -- including unrealized portions -- is typically adjusted as a non-operating item. Different paths, same destination.

## The Balance Sheet and the Cash Flow Statement

Assets are what a company owns, liabilities are what it owes, and the remainder is shareholders' equity -- reflecting the company's net worth.

Many companies present the balance sheet with assets on the left and liabilities plus shareholders' equity on the right, so the two sides balance. Intuitively, the right side reflects the company's financing structure -- "where the money comes from" -- while the left side reflects asset composition -- "where the money goes."

If we move non-interest-bearing liabilities to the left side of the balance sheet and net them against current assets, we get working capital. A balance sheet adjusted this way -- as in the diagram I drew at the beginning -- is more convenient for valuation calculations and easier to compare with the cash flow statement.

As we can see, financing cash flows are reflected in changes to interest-bearing liabilities and shareholders' equity on the right side of the balance sheet, while investing cash flows are reflected in changes to long-term assets and non-operating assets on the left side. The changes in working capital and depreciation on the left side of the balance sheet are reflected in the income statement -- and these are the main reconciling items between the income statement and the cash flow statement.

## Which Statement Is Most Important for Decision-Making

Unlike the balance sheet, the income statement and the cash flow statement reflect performance over a period, both stemming from the concept of accounting periods. Imagine if there were no accounting periods -- would the income statement and cash flow statement still matter? Perhaps the balance sheet alone would suffice.

When most people look at financial statements, their primary concern is a company's performance over the period: is it profitable, and how profitable is it? As a result, the income statement typically receives the most attention. However, the income statement is often unreliable. As mentioned earlier, unrealized gains and losses from financial assets measured at fair value can be included in the income statement, but these may be fleeting. Another issue is one-time items such as asset impairments, inventory write-downs, and restructuring charges. There is considerable room for discretion with these items -- companies can use them to shift income to future periods. Excessive impairments lead to lower future depreciation (thus boosting profit margins), and excessive inventory write-downs lead to lower future cost of sales (thus boosting gross margins). Newly appointed management teams often have an incentive to do this, commonly known as "taking a big bath."

Which statement matters most depends on the user. If you are analyzing a company's business model and future prospects, the income statement may be more important. If you are calculating a company's valuation, as previously discussed, practitioners can approach it from either the income statement or the cash flow statement, but regardless of the method, valuation cannot do without the balance sheet. If you are concerned about the reliability of the financials, the cash flow statement is clearly more trustworthy than the income statement.
