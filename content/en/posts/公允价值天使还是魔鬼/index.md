*The Story of Accounting Standards (Part III)*

## An Accounting Standard Rewritten by Politics in the Middle of a Financial Crisis

April 2, 2009, Norwalk, Connecticut. The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) voted to approve three emergency guidance documents. The gist: when markets are inactive, banks may rely more heavily on their own judgment and models to estimate the value of financial assets, rather than being forced to use a handful of distressed fire-sale prices as benchmarks. At the same time, asset impairments driven by liquidity panic — rather than genuine credit deterioration — could remain in Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) instead of flowing through the income statement.

This vote came just three weeks after a heated hearing before the House Financial Services Committee. From the release of the exposure draft to the final standard, only 15 days elapsed, with an extremely brief public comment period — FASB reviewed all responses in a single day. For an institution renowned for its deliberate, painstaking pace, revising a standard typically takes months or even years.

On the day the news broke, Citibank's stock rose 8.6%, Bank of America climbed 9.6%, Wells Fargo jumped 10.5%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average gained over 216 points. Analysts estimated that retroactive application to Q1 2009 could boost the banking sector's quarterly net income by more than 20%. Some scholars estimated that Citigroup's reported losses might shrink by 50% to 70%.

A single change to an accounting rule, worth tens of billions of dollars.

On the other side of the Pacific, the very same standard was producing equally dramatic — but opposite — effects. In 2018, Fangda Group, a curtain-wall and window manufacturer, reclassified Building No. 1 of Shenzhen Fangda Plaza as investment property and measured it at fair value, booking a fair value gain of RMB 2.917 billion that year — a manufacturing company whose largest profit source was the "appreciation" of a single building. Since 2022, as China's real estate market has been in a sustained downturn, companies holding large portfolios of investment properties have taken the hardest hit. Macalline, for example, recorded fair value losses of RMB 887 million in 2023, widening to RMB 2.029 billion in 2024.

The same standard — some use it to turn losses into profits; others are dragged into the abyss by it. Is fair value an honest mirror that faithfully reflects reality, or a distorted mirror that can warp at any moment?

## Reliability vs. Relevance: A Debate That Has Lasted Decades

To understand this debate, you first need to grasp the fundamental disagreement between fair value and its rival — historical cost.

Historical cost is straightforward: you record what you paid. A building purchased for 300 million in 2005 stays on the books at 300 million minus depreciation, even if it is now worth a billion. The numbers are backed by documentation, and auditors love it — no need to worry about "what is this thing actually worth today."

Fair value, on the other hand, requires assets to be recorded at the price they could fetch in an orderly market transaction — not in a panic-driven fire sale. In plain terms, it asks the market: what could this be sold for right now?

Historical cost champions reliability — you may not know what it is worth today, but you know exactly what you paid. Fair value champions relevance — investors making decisions need to know the current value of an asset, not its purchase price from a decade ago.

This debate had already been raging for decades before 2006. But before we get to the standard issued that year, it is worth revisiting two earlier crises — each revealed a different face of fair value and directly shaped the standard that followed.

## The Two Faces of Fair Value: The S&L Crisis and the Enron Collapse

**Face One: The Savings & Loan Crisis — The Cost of Historical Cost.** From the late 1980s to the early 1990s, over 1,000 savings institutions in the United States failed. Post-mortem investigations found that historical cost accounting was an accomplice in concealing the problems: these institutions appeared well-capitalized on paper, but the loans and investments they held had already deteriorated sharply in value. Because they used historical cost, financial statements revealed nothing. In 1991, the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) published a report urging the banking industry to adopt mark-to-market accounting across the board. The lesson was clear: historical cost let banks paint a rosy picture until problems grew too large to hide and then erupted all at once. Fair value was seen as the answer — a mirror that would reveal the truth early.

**Face Two: Enron — The Cost of Abusing Fair Value.** In 1992, Enron persuaded the SEC to approve mark-to-market accounting for its natural gas futures trading. For futures contracts, this made sense — futures have active market quotes and are essentially financial instruments. But Enron then gradually extended the method to contracts of an entirely different nature.

Take, for example, a 20-year natural gas supply agreement. The standard approach would be to recognize revenue as gas is delivered each year — you supply the gas, collect payment, and then record revenue. But Enron treated these contracts as "financial instruments": on the signing date, it ran a DCF on 20 years of projected cash flows and booked the entire net present value as current-period profit. In essence, it turned the output of a valuation model directly into a line item on the income statement.

The problem was obvious: the DCF depended entirely on management-chosen assumptions — future gas prices, discount rates, customer default probabilities. Optimistic assumptions yielded 10 billion in profit; conservative ones perhaps 3 billion; honest risk assessment might produce a loss. Enron systematically chose the most optimistic assumptions. According to a post-bankruptcy analysis by the Journal of Accountancy, more than half of Enron's pre-tax profit in 2000 came from "unrealized trading gains and losses." There was also a fatal "treadmill effect": if you have already booked future profits this year, how do you show growth next year? You have to sign more — and bigger — new contracts.

The accounting standards at the time were vague on the boundary between what counted as a "financial instrument" and what was an ordinary supply contract. Enron adopted the most aggressive interpretation in the gray zone, and Arthur Andersen, as auditor, went along with it. CFO Andrew Fastow later said, "I technically followed the rules but violated their spirit" — no rule explicitly prohibited it, but the practice completely departed from the original intent of fair value measurement. Enron filed for bankruptcy in late 2001, then the largest bankruptcy in American history.

## How to Measure Fair Value: A Framework Born from Two Crises

These two crises left behind two lessons pointing in opposite directions: without fair value, the S&L crisis showed that the truth gets buried; without constraints, Enron showed that fair value gets abused. Together, these lessons directly led to FASB's issuance of SFAS 157 (later codified as ASC 820) in 2006.

Before SFAS 157, the definition of fair value was scattered across different standards, with varying wording and emphasis — some stressed "exchange price," others stressed "market participants" — and there was no unified framework. SFAS 157 accomplished three critical things. First, it established a single, unified definition of fair value: "the price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date." Two key points here: it emphasizes the "exit price" (what you would receive upon sale) rather than the "entry price" (what you paid), and it specifies "orderly transaction," ruling out fire sales. Second, it created the three-level valuation hierarchy: Level 1 uses quoted prices in active markets for identical assets — the most transparent and reliable; Level 2 is derived from similar assets or observable market inputs; Level 3 relies on the entity's own models and assumptions. Third, it required companies to disclose the scale and movement of assets at each level.

The framework's design intent was clear: Enron's practice of plugging a self-built model's output directly into the income statement would, under the new framework, at minimum be classified as Level 3 and require full disclosure. But it is important to note that SFAS 157 itself does not dictate which assets must be measured at fair value — that is the job of other standards (such as those for financial instruments or investment property). It merely provides a unified methodology for "how to measure." IFRS 13, issued by the IASB in 2011, established an essentially equivalent fair value measurement framework under international standards.

Ironically, this framework — built to prevent Enron-style abuse — became the target of fierce criticism just two years later, in a crisis of an entirely different kind. Enron's problem was Level 3 valuations distorted by management; in 2008, the banks' complaint was the exact opposite — Level 1 market prices had become the engine of a "death spiral" amid panic.

## The 2008 Financial Crisis: When the Mirror Showed an Image Too Frightening to Bear

September 15, 2008: Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy. The six months that followed marked the most intense assault on fair value accounting.

When the market for mortgage-backed securities (MBS) evaporated virtually overnight, banks were caught in a nightmarish loop: the handful of remaining trades were heavily discounted fire sales -> these fire-sale prices became the mark-to-market benchmarks -> banks were forced to write down assets -> regulatory capital fell short -> banks had to sell more assets to raise capital -> further depressing market prices. Former FDIC Chairman William Isaac later estimated that mark-to-market accounting "destroyed more than $500 billion in capital at U.S. financial institutions" during the crisis.

A frequently confused detail needs clarification here. Not all financial assets held by banks were measured at fair value — the bulk of banks' core loan portfolios were carried at amortized cost and were not directly affected by market price fluctuations. However, banks also held substantial securities portfolios, which under the prevailing standards were classified into different categories: trading securities had their fair value changes flow directly into the income statement; "available-for-sale" (AFS) securities had fair value changes recorded in Other Comprehensive Income (OCI), bypassing net income. During the 2008 crisis, it was precisely these securities investments — especially MBS and related structured products — whose fair value collapse put banks under enormous pressure.

Although OCI does not affect the income statement, it does impact shareholders' equity on the balance sheet. Under the U.S. bank regulatory capital framework, Tier 1 capital for large banks included accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI). So when AFS securities plummeted in value, even if the income statement looked tolerable, regulatory capital was being materially eroded. Making things worse, the prevailing standard (SFAS 115) required that if an AFS security's impairment was judged to be "other-than-temporary," the loss had to be reclassified from OCI into the income statement. By early 2009, an increasing number of banks were finding it difficult to keep arguing that these losses were merely "temporary" — the OCI firewall was on the verge of collapse.

The banking industry's counterattack was swift and fierce. The American Bankers Association and the U.S. Chamber of Commerce jointly pressured regulators. Former House Speaker Newt Gingrich published a Forbes column demanding "an immediate suspension of mark-to-market" and even proposed creating a "Financial Accounting Oversight Board" — composed of the Fed Chairman, the Treasury Secretary, the SEC Chairman, and others — to replace FASB. On October 3, 2008, Congress embedded two accounting provisions in the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act (EESA): reaffirming the SEC's authority to suspend fair value rules and directing the SEC to complete a study on mark-to-market accounting by year-end.

Across the Atlantic, the pressure was even more direct. The European Commission and French President Nicolas Sarkozy pressured the IASB to allow European banks to reclassify financial assets out of fair value categories and into amortized cost. On October 13, 2008, the IASB bypassed its normal due process and issued an emergency amendment to IAS 39 — more remarkably, with an effective date retroactive to July 1, 2008. This meant European banks could, with full knowledge of the Q3 market crash, retroactively and selectively move already-depreciated assets out of the fair value framework. Scholar Peter Fiechter's study of 219 IFRS-reporting European banks found that roughly one-third took advantage of this opportunity, reclassifying amounts averaging 131% of book equity. After reclassification, these banks' average ROE flipped from -1.4% to +1.3% — a single accounting policy choice turned losses into profits.

On December 30, 2008, the SEC published the study mandated by Congress, and the conclusion surprised the banking industry: **fair value accounting was not the root cause of the crisis, and SFAS 157 should be improved, not suspended.** The SEC's judgment was that the banks' fundamental problems were excessive leverage and deteriorating asset quality — not how assets were measured. Fair value did cause problems to surface earlier and more starkly, but exposing problems is not the same as creating them.

Yet the political pressure did not subside. On March 12, 2009, the House Financial Services Committee held its hearing. The core demand from the banking industry and certain politicians was to relax the application of fair value in crisis conditions — not to abolish it entirely, but to give banks more room for judgment in valuations during market panics, reducing the impact of fire-sale prices on financial statements. Members of Congress explicitly warned FASB: if you do not change the rules, Congress will. Three weeks later came the vote described at the opening of this article.

A FASB spokesperson subsequently insisted that these changes had already been in the works and were unrelated to Congressional pressure. No one believed it.

## The Stakeholder Game: How Should Fair Value Be Used?

The positions of each stakeholder in this contest are worth examining closely.

What exactly were the banks demanding? The ability to use their own models and judgment for valuations during market panics, rather than being forced to adopt fire-sale prices. But think about it — isn't that precisely the intent of Level 3 in the SFAS 157 hierarchy? The standard already permits the use of unobservable inputs when no active market exists. What the banks were really unhappy about was not the standard's text, but auditors' and regulators' conservative interpretation of "when it is acceptable to deviate from market prices." Banks did not want to abolish fair value; they wanted greater discretionary power within the fair value framework — which was precisely what investors feared most.

On the investor side, the CFA Institute was actually a defender of fair value throughout the crisis. The logic was straightforward: if mark-to-market were eliminated, investors would lose the ability to assess banks' true financial condition. Had the S&L crisis not taught a painful enough lesson? But investor support was conditional — they demanded better disclosure, particularly detailed breakdowns and valuation assumptions for Level 3 assets. What they wanted was "fair value with constraints," not unchecked discretion.

Academia offered a dispassionate perspective. Christian Laux and Christian Leuz, in an NBER study, conducted a careful analysis and concluded that it is difficult to blame the deepening of the financial crisis on fair value accounting itself. The real drivers of systemic risk were excessive leverage, maturity mismatches, and deteriorating asset quality. Fair value at most amplified the signal — but the signal reflected genuinely existing problems.

Warren Buffett's position deserves close attention. In a March 2009 CNBC interview, he said he was "almost religious" about fair value — because he had seen too many managements manipulate financial statements when given the freedom to use their imagination. Any alternative to mark-to-market would trend toward being abused. But he also acknowledged that mark-to-market did "add gasoline to the fire" during the financial crisis. He then drew a highly insightful distinction: preserve fair value as a disclosure and information tool, but do not let regulators use those numbers to force banks to raise additional capital. The information objective and the regulatory objective should be kept separate.

## Income Statement or OCI: Where Should Fair Value Changes Go?

The 2008 crisis exposed a problem: fair value changes can exert excessive pressure on banks during a crisis. But looking back at the institutional design at the time, there was already a "relief valve" — fair value changes on financial assets did not necessarily have to hit the income statement. Under the old standards (SFAS 115 under US GAAP and IAS 39 under IFRS), fair value changes on AFS securities were recorded in OCI and did not affect net income. The 2009 FASB emergency amendments, mentioned earlier, centered in part on revising the criteria for "other-than-temporary impairment" — separating losses caused by credit deterioration from those caused by liquidity panic, and allowing the latter to remain in OCI rather than being reclassified to the income statement. In effect, just as the OCI firewall was about to fall, they reinforced it.

After the crisis, standard-setters reexamined the classification and measurement framework for financial assets. The IASB issued IFRS 9 in 2014 (effective January 1, 2018), and FASB issued ASU 2016-01 in 2016 (effective 2018). Both made major changes to where fair value changes end up.

The biggest change concerned equity investments. Under the old standards, companies could classify stock investments as AFS, parking market-value fluctuations in OCI and keeping them out of the income statement. After the new standards, the two frameworks diverged: under US GAAP (ASC 321), fair value changes on equity investments **must all flow through the income statement**, with no OCI option; under IFRS 9, an entity may make an irrevocable election at initial recognition to designate a non-trading equity investment as "fair value through other comprehensive income" (FVOCI) — and these OCI amounts are never "recycled" to the income statement, even upon disposal.

For debt investments, both frameworks retained an AFS-like OCI pathway. This means the 2008 scenario — banks under pressure from falling bond values — has not been fundamentally changed at the institutional level. The OCI relief valve is still in place.

The ASC 321 change for equity investments is precisely what Buffett has complained about for years. Berkshire Hathaway holds over $170 billion in public equities. Under the old standard, market-value swings on these holdings sat in OCI; under the new standard, they all flow through the income statement, producing extreme volatility in GAAP earnings — $4 billion in 2018, soaring to $81.4 billion in 2019, a 1,900% difference. Yet Berkshire's "operating earnings" (Buffett's non-GAAP metric, defined as net income minus investment and derivative gains/losses) were $24.8 billion and $24.0 billion, respectively — less than 4% variation.

Buffett's approach is essentially a demonstration for investors. He does not object to fair value measurement — he fully accepts that Berkshire's investment portfolio is disclosed at market value. What he objects to is stuffing these fluctuations into the income statement's bottom line, because it misleads investors who do not understand accounting rules. To that end, every Berkshire earnings release places "operating earnings" front and center, accompanied by a blunt disclaimer: "Investment gains/losses in any given quarter are usually meaningless and give figures for net income per share that can be extremely misleading to investors who have little or no knowledge of accounting rules."

## The China Perspective: From Investment Property to Tech Company Preferred Stock

The story of fair value in China's A-share market takes on a different complexion than in the United States.

In February 2006, China's Ministry of Finance issued a new set of Accounting Standards for Business Enterprises — of 38 specific standards, 22 were entirely new, marking the first substantive convergence between Chinese accounting standards and IFRS. In this systematic overhaul, fair value was introduced into 17 specific standards, covering areas such as financial instrument recognition and measurement, investment property, non-monetary asset exchanges, debt restructuring, and business combinations. But the standard-setters maintained a cautious stance from the outset. Liu Yuting, then Director of the Accounting Department at the Ministry of Finance, stated explicitly that measurement issues were a key focus of the revisions and that China could not simply copy IFRS's emphasis on fair value — China's market maturity and the professional standards of its appraisal industry lagged behind those of Europe and the United States, and reliability should take priority over relevance.

Investment property provides a clear example. CAS 3 (Accounting Standard for Business Enterprises No. 3) explicitly designates the cost model as the default, permitting the fair value model only when "conclusive evidence exists that fair value can be continuously and reliably obtained." In 2014, the Ministry of Finance issued CAS 39 (Fair Value Measurement), aligned with IFRS 13, establishing a Chinese version of the three-level fair value hierarchy and disclosure requirements. In 2017, the Ministry issued revised financial instruments standards (including CAS 22, Financial Instrument Recognition and Measurement), aligned with IFRS 9, adjusting the classification and measurement of financial assets.

Data from the first year of implementation in 2007 confirmed the wisdom of this cautious approach. According to Liu Yuting's publicly disclosed analysis, among 1,570 listed companies, 630 held investment property, but only 18 (2.86%) opted for the fair value model. In the financial assets domain, however, fair value's impact was already significant: 316 companies recognized fair value gains or losses that year, and for these companies, fair value changes averaged 59% of pre-tax profit (this figure comes from Liu Yuting's press briefing and covers only companies with such gains or losses, not the average across all listed companies). By 2008, when the stock market turned bearish, the picture reversed entirely.

Fair value in the A-share market has two areas of concentrated controversy.

The first is investment property. Companies using the fair value model do not depreciate or amortize investment property; instead, they adjust book value to appraised value at year-end, with the difference flowing directly into the income statement under "Fair Value Changes" — a standalone line item in A-share income statements, separate from "Investment Income" (the former reflects unrealized fair value fluctuations; the latter reflects realized investment gains/losses and equity-method earnings). During years of rising property prices, this meant companies could book substantial accounting profits merely by holding property — despite no actual cash inflow. In 2018, Fangda Group (000055), a curtain-wall manufacturer, saw its profits surge due to a RMB 2.917 billion fair value gain on a single building. The Shenzhen Stock Exchange's concern focused on Fangda's failure to adequately disclose in its earnings forecast that this gain was non-recurring — investors looking only at the profit figure could easily mistake it for an improvement in operating performance. The reliability of valuation assumptions also warrants scrutiny: a late-2024 regulatory letter from the Shenzhen Stock Exchange revealed that one company, when appraising investment property in Suzhou, used the average vacancy rate for Shanghai's industrial real estate without any adjustment for regional differences.

The second area of controversy involves financial instruments, particularly preferred stock financing for tech companies before their IPOs. When Xiaomi filed its prospectus with the Hong Kong Stock Exchange in 2018, its IFRS net loss for 2017 was a staggering RMB 43.9 billion. But this "huge loss" was almost entirely attributable to fair value changes on convertible redeemable preferred stock — an accounting loss of RMB 54 billion. Stripping out these items, Xiaomi's adjusted profit for 2017 was actually RMB 5.36 billion.

This case is worth unpacking. Pre-IPO tech companies typically undergo multiple rounds of preferred stock financing. These preferred shares carry two key features: a redemption right (if the company fails to complete its IPO by an agreed deadline, holders can demand cash redemption) and a conversion right (holders can convert preferred shares into ordinary shares). Under IAS 32, even if the redemption right is conditional, as long as the condition is not entirely within the issuer's control (an IPO depends on market conditions and regulatory approval), the issuer cannot "unconditionally" avoid delivering cash — creating a financial liability. If the conversion ratio is fixed (i.e., it passes the "fixed-for-fixed" test), the instrument can be bifurcated: the redemption obligation is treated as a liability (typically measured at amortized cost, recognizing only interest expense), and the conversion right is treated as equity (not remeasured). In this case, the income statement impact is limited. But Xiaomi's conversion price varied with its valuation — it was not fixed — so the conversion right could not be classified as equity. The result: the entire instrument was classified as a financial liability, measured at fair value with all changes flowing through the income statement.

This produced a completely counterintuitive phenomenon: the higher the company's valuation, the larger this "liability's" fair value, and the greater the "loss" on the income statement — the better the company performed, the larger its reported loss. Xiaomi's RMB 54 billion fair value loss in 2017 reflected precisely the fact that investors' preferred shares had appreciated by RMB 54 billion. Similar situations arose during the IPOs of Meituan, Meitu, and other companies.

It is worth noting that US GAAP and IFRS differ in their treatment here. IFRS considers that even a conditional redemption obligation (triggered only if the company does not go public) creates a liability as long as the condition is not entirely within the issuer's control. US GAAP (ASC 480), however, holds that a contingent obligation requiring both a specific triggering event and active exercise by the holder does not necessarily constitute a liability. Under US GAAP, the same Xiaomi preferred stock would more likely be classified as equity, and the income statement effect would not arise. This is an area where the two frameworks frequently produce different outcomes in classifying financial instruments.

These two domains illustrate the distinct challenges of fair value in the Chinese market: the investment property problem lies in the subjectivity of valuation assumptions — appraisal-driven appreciation depends on the appraiser's chosen assumptions and can become a tool for managing reported earnings; the preferred stock problem lies in counterintuitive classification criteria — good news turns into bad earnings. What they share is this: investors who do not understand the source and nature of fair value changes will be seriously misled by the income statement.

## How Should Investors Deal with Fair Value?

So as investors, how should we approach fair value?

Buffett's practice at Berkshire offers a useful framework: acknowledge it, disclose it, but do not let it mislead you.

First, distinguish the "purity" of earnings. The A-share income statement has a standalone "Fair Value Changes" line item. Investors should strip it out of core operating profit. When performing valuation analysis, fair value changes do not represent sustainable operating capability, nor do they generate real cash flows — they should not be included in EBIT. The A-share metric "net profit excluding non-recurring items" is a convenient supplementary indicator.

Second, pay attention to the scale of Level 3 assets. The fair value hierarchy disclosure in annual report notes is critical information that should not be skipped. If a company has an unusually high proportion of Level 3 assets, or if significant amounts are being transferred from Level 2 to Level 3, it means more of the valuation relies on management's own assumptions.

Third, test the reasonableness of investment property valuations. The key assumptions in appraisal reports — primarily capitalization rates and expected rental growth rates — can be cross-checked against market data. If a company's investment property appreciates every year in its appraisals, but rental income is declining and vacancy rates are rising, the fairness of the valuation deserves a big question mark.

Fourth — and most often overlooked — pay attention to the "reservoir" in Other Comprehensive Income. Financial assets measured at fair value through OCI carry cumulative unrealized gains or losses in the equity section of the balance sheet. These figures do not affect current-period earnings, but they will flow into the income statement when assets are sold (for debt investments), or they may already be eroding the equity base.

## Epilogue

Looking back across this history — from the U.S. S&L crisis to Enron's collapse, from the 2008 global financial crisis to A-share investment property controversies and the preferred stock puzzles of Chinese tech companies — the story of fair value has always swung between two extremes: without it, the truth gets buried; with it, the standard risks being abused, misinterpreted, or weaponized to amplify panic during a crisis. Every adjustment by standard-setters — from the SFAS 157 three-level hierarchy to the 2009 emergency amendments to IFRS 9's reclassification of financial assets — has been a search for a new equilibrium between reliability and relevance, transparency and stability. The IASB and FASB, in their 2017 post-implementation reviews, concluded that the existing framework was "generally working as intended" — but the debate is far from over: US GAAP and IFRS still diverge on whether equity investments may use the OCI pathway, and China's fair value measurement of investment property is facing its first real test in a declining property market.

Fair value did not "kill" those banks, just as it did not "create" Enron's profits. Excessive leverage and deteriorating asset quality killed the banks; management greed and auditor negligence manufactured Enron's false prosperity. Fair value is merely a mirror. Some use it as a beauty filter, some complain it is unflattering, and some want to smash it when they do not like what it shows.

In that March 2009 CNBC interview, Buffett said something worth pondering again and again: once you give a CEO a pen and tell him it is the honor system, any alternative to mark-to-market will trend toward being abused. "So I'm reluctant to give it up."

For investors, this mirror does sometimes distort. But the answer is not to smash it or turn away. The answer is to understand under what conditions it is accurate and under what conditions it distorts — did this number hit the income statement or OCI? Is the valuation based on market quotes or management models? How large is the fair value change relative to total profit? Answer these questions, and you can see through the accounting fog to the company's true operating picture.
