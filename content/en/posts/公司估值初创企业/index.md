Previous articles introduced the two-stage model for discounting free cash flow to the firm, as well as methods for estimating the terminal value in the second stage. This is the standard approach for calculating a company's intrinsic value.

In the private equity industry, investment targets are often startups -- companies that have not yet established a stable business model. From a financial statement perspective, some startups may not even have revenue, and most are likely operating at a loss on their income statements.

So how do investors determine the valuation of a startup? The overarching principles come down to two methods: relative valuation and absolute valuation. For very early-stage startups, such as those at the angel investment stage, relative valuation may be the only suitable approach. For most startups, however, the more common valuation methodology combines absolute valuation based on discounted cash flow (DCF) with relative valuation based on market multiples.

## Startup Valuation

### Detailed Forecast Period

Given the high uncertainty surrounding the long-term prospects of startups, in practice, the detailed forecast period for cash flows is shorter -- typically projecting financial statements for only the next 3 to 5 years. The specific time horizon can be guided by the startup's IPO timeline or aligned with the private equity fund's exit objectives.

### Value at the End of the Forecast Period

By selecting the average market multiples of industry peers, or multiples derived from acquisitions or publicly listed companies in the same industry, and applying them to the revenue or profit targets calculated during the detailed forecast period, one can estimate the startup's value 3 to 5 years into the future. This approach effectively circumvents the high uncertainty inherent in long-term cash flow projections.

Regarding market multiples: if the calculation targets equity value, the commonly used metric is the price-to-earnings ratio (P/E); if targeting enterprise value, one may consider using revenue multiples (EV/Sales) or EV/EBITDA, depending on whether the company will have reliable profits at the end of the forecast period and on industry conventions for valuation.

### Required Rate of Return

After completing the above two steps, we now have a preliminary conclusion on the startup's future valuation. Using the investor's required rate of return as the discount rate, this future valuation can be discounted back to the present to determine the current valuation of the investment. This required rate of return is also the investment's internal rate of return (IRR).

## Pre-Money Valuation vs. Post-Money Valuation

The equity stake acquired by the investor is calculated based on the post-money valuation of the current funding round:

*Equity Stake for This Investment = Investment Amount / Post-Money Valuation*

*Post-Money Valuation = Pre-Money Valuation + Investment Amount*

This raises the question: is the current valuation calculated through the steps above a pre-money or post-money valuation?

Clearly, for the investor, a post-money interpretation is more favorable, as it yields a higher equity stake compared to treating the same figure as a pre-money valuation. The investor can reasonably argue that the current round of investment is a prerequisite for the startup to achieve the projected future and present valuations described above.

However, the founders and existing investors may see it differently. They may argue that funding could be obtained through other channels, such as debt financing. Under this logic, the calculated current valuation should be treated as a pre-money valuation.

There is no definitive answer to this disagreement. For startups, the valuation calculation serves as an anchor, and the ultimate result depends on the bargaining power of both buyer and seller.
