Tencent Music (TME) just delivered a solid report — full-year 2025 revenue of RMB 32.9 billion, up 16% YoY, with adjusted net income of RMB 9.6 billion, up 25%. Yet on the day of the earnings release, the stock plunged over 20%, hitting near its 52-week low. What is the market afraid of?

Two words: Soda Music.

Under three DCF scenarios, the valuation range is **$10.8–$23.2/ADS**, with the current price of $10.35 sitting almost at the pessimistic floor. The question is: will the bear case actually play out?

## What Does RMB 32.9 Billion in Revenue Look Like?

To figure out where the bottom is, start by breaking down TME's revenue. The RMB 32.9 billion splits into three streams: music subscriptions at RMB 17.66 billion (54%), non-subscription music revenue at RMB 9.07 billion (28%, including advertising, concerts, and artist merchandise), and social entertainment at RMB 6.2 billion (19%, WeSing and Kugou Live).

Subscriptions are the largest piece and the one the market worries about most. The math is straightforward: **paying users × monthly ARPPU × 12 ≈ annual subscription revenue**. At year-end 2025, TME had 127 million paying users with an ARPPU of RMB 11.9/month, which works out to roughly RMB 18.1 billion — close to the actual RMB 17.66 billion (the gap is mainly due to quarterly fluctuations). So forecasting subscription revenue really comes down to two questions: can paying users keep growing? And will ARPPU get dragged down?

Both questions point to the same variable — Soda Music.

## Who Is Soda Music Actually Taking?

Soda Music's MAU surged from 70 million to 140 million over the past year, while TME's MAU dropped from 570 million to 530 million — a loss of roughly 43 million. The timelines match almost perfectly.

But look at the user profiles: Soda Music skews post-90s, lower-tier cities, 63% male. Its core pitch is "watch an 80-second ad, listen free all day." The users it's capturing are mostly casual listeners — no audio quality requirements, no loyalty to licensed catalogs, people who were never going to pay anyway.

This has created a fascinating divergence at TME: MAU has declined for over four years straight, yet paying users grew from 107 million to 127 million. The paying ratio jumped from 18.5% to 24.1% — though there's a statistical trap here, since the denominator is shrinking.

What's really making the market nervous are two Q4 signals: paying users grew by only 170,000 sequentially (near stagnation), and ARPPU was flat QoQ at RMB 11.9 (growth momentum exhausted). Both volume and price stalling simultaneously — the subscription growth engine is sputtering.

## Will an RMB 8 VIP Crush the RMB 15 Pricing Structure?

Soda Music's VIP monthly plan costs RMB 8, SVIP RMB 18; QQ Music's Green Diamond auto-renew is RMB 15, SVIP RMB 30. But the actual ARPPU is only RMB 11.9 because many users take annual plans, promotions, and bundled memberships at deep discounts. The catalog gap remains significant (50 million vs. 260 million tracks), but post-antitrust removal of exclusive licensing, that gap will gradually narrow.

In the bear case, TME is forced to launch cheaper tiers or increase promotions, pushing ARPPU down from RMB 11.9 to around RMB 10.5. Meanwhile, continued MAU decline drags paying users from 127 million to 117 million. Combined, 2027 subscription revenue could fall from RMB 17.66 billion to RMB 14.7 billion — a 17% drop.

The base case is far milder: ARPPU holds roughly flat through SVIP penetration, paying users stabilize around 130 million, and subscription revenue edges up to RMB 19 billion. The key assumption is low overlap between Soda Music's bargain users and TME's paying base — the person paying RMB 8 for VIP and the person paying RMB 30 for SVIP are probably not the same person.

Management was candid on the earnings call: "subscription revenue will experience some short-term pressure due to intense competition." Honest, but no specifics.

## What Cards Can TME Play Beyond Subscriptions?

Easy to overlook: TME's non-subscription music revenue (advertising, concerts, artist merchandise) grew 39.2% in 2025, reaching RMB 9.07 billion. This line has little correlation with MAU decline — ad revenue depends on user profiling precision, while concerts and merchandise ride the fan economy. TME's new ad-supported subscription tier is essentially re-monetizing churned free users.

Even in the bear case, this segment is unlikely to go negative — the assumption is growth decelerating from +39% to flat by 2028. This provides roughly RMB 1–1.5 billion of cushion, keeping the total revenue floor from looking too ugly.

On the other side, social entertainment (WeSing, Kugou Live) is a foregone conclusion: RMB 9.1 billion → 6.7 billion → 6.2 billion, structurally declining as livestream regulation tightens and short video substitution continues. All three scenarios converge around RMB 3.5 billion by 2028.

## Where Is the Earnings Floor?

Adding the three lines together:

The DCF projects five years (2026–2030). In the bear case, profits trough in 2028: total revenue at RMB 27.7 billion (–16% from 2025), with operating profit declining roughly 30% from current levels.

The base case shows much milder pressure: 2030 revenue at RMB 35.9 billion (+9%), operating margins largely intact, with profits dipping near-term but not collapsing.

Discounted at a 10% WACC, the three scenarios yield a valuation range of $10.8–$23.2/ADS — a wide gap. The spread of more than 2x between bear and bull reflects extreme market disagreement, which ultimately boils down to a bet on how severely Soda Music disrupts the paid ecosystem.

Another number worth noting: TME has RMB 38 billion in cash ($3.4/ADS), accounting for a third of its market cap.

## Where Is the Key Disagreement?

The debate around TME ultimately reduces to one question: **are the RMB 8 Soda VIP buyers and the RMB 12 QQ Music Green Diamond subscribers the same people?**

If the answer is "no" — Soda Music is eating free users, TME's paying pool is largely unaffected — then today's pessimistic pricing has overshot. If the answer is "yes" — low-price competition will eventually erode TME's pricing power, dragging ARPPU from RMB 12 to RMB 10 or lower — then the profit floor could be deeper than RMB 6.6 billion.

There's no definitive answer yet, but several leading indicators are worth tracking: whether MAU's sequential decline narrows, whether paying users can resume quarterly net adds of 1 million+, whether ARPPU breaks below RMB 11, and when Soda Music hits its ceiling or starts charging. TME has already announced it will stop disclosing quarterly user metrics — making tracking harder and hinting that management isn't optimistic about the numbers.
