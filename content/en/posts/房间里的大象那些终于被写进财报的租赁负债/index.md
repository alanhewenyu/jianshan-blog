*Stories Behind Accounting Standards (Part 4)*

In 2019, after adopting the new lease accounting standard, McDonald's — the world's largest fast-food chain — suddenly saw over $13 billion in operating lease liabilities appear on its balance sheet. Starbucks added roughly $9 billion. But the most staggering figure belonged to Walgreens, the largest drugstore chain in the United States — nearly $34 billion in lease liabilities surfaced overnight. These companies hadn't signed any new contracts or completed any acquisitions. Tens of thousands of long-term store leases and billions of dollars in annual rent commitments had been there all along — they had simply been tucked away in a small paragraph in the footnotes, never appearing on the balance sheet.

That same year, the balance sheets of China's three major airlines — China Southern Airlines, Air China, and China Eastern Airlines — underwent a similar "transformation." Take Air China as an example: after adopting the new lease standard, total assets increased by 50.5 billion yuan and total liabilities by 49.7 billion yuan, pushing the debt-to-asset ratio from roughly 58.7% to 65.6%. For state-owned airlines, the debt-to-asset ratio is one of the core metrics in SASAC's performance evaluation system. Of course, SASAC would make corresponding adjustments for metric changes caused by standard revisions in its actual assessments — but such adjustments take time to coordinate, and the pressure from financing negotiations and external credit ratings during the transition period was very real. All three airlines had right-of-use assets exceeding 100 billion yuan. Those operating-lease aircraft that had been quietly running off-balance-sheet were brought onto the books overnight.

Yet whether it was McDonald's or Air China, nothing about their underlying business had changed. The same stores, the same aircraft, the same lease contracts, the same cash flows. The only thing that changed was an accounting standard.

That standard is called IFRS 16 (International Financial Reporting Standard 16 — Leases), and its U.S. counterpart is ASC 842. From initial discussions to final implementation, global standard-setters spent over twenty years developing this rule. Throughout those two decades, a conceptually straightforward question — "Should a lessee's lease obligations appear on the balance sheet?" — triggered a prolonged tug-of-war among standard-setters, corporate management, auditors, and investors. This is a story about transparency versus vested interests, and about something "known to be right yet difficult to implement."

## What Problem Were the Lease Standards Trying to Solve?

To understand the significance of lease accounting reform, you first need to understand a simple but crucial distinction: finance leases versus operating leases.

Suppose an airline needs an aircraft. It could acquire one through a finance lease — under this arrangement, the lease is essentially a purchase paid in installments, with the risks and rewards of the aircraft transferring to the airline at the inception of the lease. This type of lease must be recognized as an asset and a corresponding liability on the balance sheet, a point that has never been controversial.

But the airline could equally choose an operating lease — and in fact, operating leases are extremely common in the global aviation industry. Based on data from the three major Chinese airlines' 2018 annual reports (the last full year before the new standard took effect), approximately 30% to 50% of Chinese airlines' aircraft were held through operating leases, with smaller carriers like China Express Airlines exceeding 90%. Airlines lease aircraft from leasing companies (such as GECAS, AerCap, and other global leaders), typically for terms of 6 to 12 years, returning them at expiry. Under the old standard, this arrangement meant airlines did not need to record any asset or liability on the balance sheet — they simply expensed each lease payment as it was incurred. The lease obligation — potentially amounting to tens of billions of yuan in future payment commitments — appeared only in a small paragraph in the financial statement footnotes.

This created a problem: the same aircraft, serving the same economic purpose, was presented in completely different ways on the financial statements, purely because of technical differences in contract terms. A finance lease pushed up the debt-to-asset ratio; an operating lease made the company look "lighter." For management, the incentive was self-evident.

The root of the problem lay in SFAS 13, issued by the U.S. FASB in 1976. This standard established four "bright-line tests" — tests using explicit numerical thresholds as judgment criteria — to distinguish finance leases from operating leases: whether ownership transfers at the end of the lease term, whether there is a bargain purchase option, whether the lease term reaches 75% of the asset's economic life, and whether the present value of minimum lease payments reaches 90% of the asset's fair value. If none of these were met, the lease was classified as an operating lease and stayed off the balance sheet.

The intent behind these four bright lines was to reduce subjective judgment and make the standard more operationally consistent. But reality proved that bright-line tests didn't eliminate room for manipulation — they provided a precise "roadmap." You simply needed to set the lease term at 74% of the asset's life and keep the present value of payments at 89% of fair value to ensure the lease remained off-balance-sheet. Lessees and leasing companies developed an entire industry of sophisticated contract engineering around these numerical boundaries. As former FASB Chairman Robert Herz remarked in 2003: the lease standard had become a set of rules that ensured no lease ever went on the balance sheet, even when you actually controlled the asset and bore inescapable payment obligations.

By the early 2000s, the off-balance-sheet treatment of operating leases had reached staggering proportions. A 2005 SEC report noted that U.S. public companies alone had approximately $1.25 trillion in future cash obligations kept off the balance sheet through operating leases. This figure continued to grow — by the time the new standard took effect in 2019, according to the IASB's impact analysis and CNBC reporting, the global scale of operating leases requiring recognition had reached approximately $3 trillion.

## Why Was the Journey from Consensus to Implementation So Difficult?

#### The Seed: G4+1's Foresight (1996–2000)

Standard-setters were not oblivious to the problem of off-balance-sheet operating leases. As early as 1996, the G4+1 working group — composed of standard-setters from Australia, Canada, New Zealand, the United Kingdom, and the United States — published a report titled *Accounting for Leases: A New Approach*, explicitly proposing that the assets and liabilities of all leases should be recognized on the balance sheet. In 1999, the G4+1 released a more detailed implementation proposal.

This recommendation gained broad support in academic circles and the investor community. The logic was crystal clear: if you sign a ten-year lease, you acquire the right to use an asset and simultaneously assume an obligation to pay rent. The right is an asset; the obligation is a liability — this perfectly fits the definitions of assets and liabilities. Hiding it in the footnotes was a distortion of the balance sheet's function of faithful representation.

But after the G4+1 report was published, standard reform did not immediately commence. The reason was simple: opposition from the corporate world was too loud. A 2006 study by British scholars Beattie et al. uncovered an ironic asymmetry: users of financial statements (investors and analysts) strongly supported bringing all leases onto the balance sheet, while preparers (corporate management) broadly opposed it. But in the lobbying process for standard-setting, preparers' voices were far louder than users' — they were organized, resourced, and motivated, while the investor community was dispersed, lacked focus, and was less willing to spend effort writing comment letters.

This "preparer/user lobbying asymmetry" was the core political dynamic running through the entire lease standard reform process.

#### Enron and the Reckoning of Off-Balance-Sheet Financing (2001–2006)

In 2001, the Enron scandal erupted. This once-mighty energy giant had used special purpose entities (SPEs) to shift massive liabilities off its balance sheet before its spectacular collapse. Enron's problems were multi-layered — involving off-balance-sheet financing manipulation through SPEs to hide debt, abuse of mark-to-market accounting on energy contracts to inflate profits, and systemic corporate governance failures. In the fair value article in this series, we focused on how Enron exploited fair value measurement to fabricate profits; in the context of lease standards, Enron's significance lies in a different dimension: it made "off-balance-sheet financing" as a concept a regulatory bullseye. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 required the SEC to comprehensively review off-balance-sheet financing arrangements, and in the SEC's 2005 report to Congress, the off-balance-sheet treatment of operating leases was listed as a key form of off-balance-sheet financing requiring attention — the report noted that this single item involved approximately $1.25 trillion in off-balance-sheet liabilities for U.S. public companies. The SEC explicitly recommended that FASB revisit the lease standard.

Enron was not the direct cause of lease standard reform, but it was the key that opened the door to a "comprehensive reckoning of off-balance-sheet financing." FASB Chairman Robert Herz became a champion of reform. But even with the lessons of Enron and congressional pressure, FASB did not act immediately. It was not until 2006 that FASB and IASB formally added lease standard reform to their joint agenda. From clear identification of the problem to formal project initiation, another five years had passed.

#### The Long Battle: A Tale of Two Exposure Drafts (2009–2013)

In March 2009, the IASB and FASB jointly released a discussion paper, *Leases: Preliminary Views*, proposing a radical approach: completely eliminate the distinction between operating and finance leases, apply a unified accounting treatment to all leases for lessees, and bring everything onto the balance sheet.

This proposal was elegant in theory but triggered a storm in practice.

In August 2010, the two boards issued the first exposure draft. Feedback poured in like a tidal wave, overwhelmingly from corporate preparers and the leasing industry, and overwhelmingly opposed. The objections were varied: increased complexity, excessive compliance costs, impact on financial ratio covenants in loan agreements, potential credit rating downgrades. A consulting report commissioned by real estate operators, homebuilders, and the U.S. Chamber of Commerce predicted that the new standard would affect $1.5 trillion in U.S. leases, destroy 190,000 to 3.3 million jobs, and reduce GDP and household wealth. The seemingly absurd figures had their own "logical chain": companies forced to reduce leasing due to worsening debt ratios → declining lease demand → contraction of commercial real estate and equipment leasing industries → job losses → GDP impact. Of course, every link in this causal chain was riddled with assumptions and exaggerations, but as a lobbying weapon, it was undeniably effective. Someone even coined a term for the impending disaster: "lease-pocalypse."

Faced with such fierce opposition, FASB and IASB had to compromise. In May 2013, the two boards issued a revised second exposure draft. The most critical change: abandoning the fully unified model and reintroducing a distinction between two types of leases.

Here it's worth explaining why the corporate world was so resistant to the "unified model." Under finance lease accounting, the lessee must recognize depreciation on the right-of-use asset (typically straight-line) and accrue interest on the lease liability using the effective interest method. Since the liability balance is largest at the beginning of the lease, interest expense is also highest early on, causing total expense (depreciation plus interest) to follow a "front-loaded" pattern — similar to a declining-balance mortgage where payments are heaviest in the early years. Under the operating lease model, each period's lease expense is a fixed amount allocated on a straight-line basis — similar to a level-payment mortgage. While total expense over the lease term is identical under both methods, the "front-loaded" pattern means newly signed leases depress profits in the early years, which is the last thing management wants to see.

Ultimately, IFRS 16 and ASC 842 took different paths. IFRS 16 chose the more thorough single model — lessees no longer distinguish between operating and finance leases; all leases are treated similarly to finance leases, with the income statement reflecting depreciation of the right-of-use asset plus interest on the lease liability, producing a "front-loaded" expense pattern. ASC 842 made greater concessions to the corporate world by preserving the distinction between two types of leases: operating leases must still go on the balance sheet, but on the income statement they continue to be presented as a single lease expense, maintaining a straight-line expense pattern. It should be noted that operating leases under ASC 842 are not without financing costs — in the accounting calculations, the lease liability still accrues interest each period using the effective interest method (i.e., the liability balance multiplied by the discount rate, with higher interest early and lower interest later), and the right-of-use asset depreciation is back-calculated as "the current period's straight-line lease cost minus the current period's interest." However, the standard requires the income statement to present only a single straight-line lease cost, typically classified under operating expenses or cost of sales (depending on the use of the leased asset), so investors cannot see the split between depreciation and interest on the financial statements. In other words, interest does exist in the underlying journal entries, but it is "smoothed out" in the financial statement presentation.

Both standards produce the same effect on the balance sheet — all leases must be recognized — but on the income statement, U.S. GAAP gave companies a "more comfortable" transition. There are also differences in the cash flow statement: under IFRS 16, lease payments are split between principal repayment (classified as financing activities) and interest (which can be classified as operating or financing activities, at the entity's policy choice under IAS 7); under ASC 842, all payments for operating leases remain classified as operating cash outflows. This means the same company would report better-looking operating cash flows under IFRS than under U.S. GAAP — because the lease principal has been "moved" to financing activities.

Throughout the comment process, the investor community (represented by CFA Institute) consistently supported reform, viewing the on-balance-sheet treatment as enhancing transparency and comparability. While corporate management and the leasing industry opposed it, the nature of their objections shifted subtly between the first and second exposure drafts: from "it shouldn't be changed at all" to "don't use the front-loaded expense pattern." In other words, they ultimately accepted the general direction of bringing leases onto the balance sheet. Judging from the evolution of comment letters, the standard-setters, through their lengthy deliberation cycle — a full decade from the 2006 project initiation to the 2016 release — gradually shifted the debate from "whether to change" to "how to change," first building consensus on the principle ("bringing leases on-balance-sheet is fine") and then making compromises on the details ("the expense pattern is negotiable"), ultimately completing the reform.

#### Implementation: The Apocalypse That Never Came (2016–2019)

IFRS 16 was issued in January 2016, and ASC 842 in February 2016. Both gave companies ample transition time, with IFRS 16 taking effect on January 1, 2019, and ASC 842 becoming effective for public companies the same year.

What happened after implementation? The prophesied "lease-pocalypse" never materialized. Approximately $3 trillion in operating leases were brought onto balance sheets, and total assets and total liabilities of U.S. public companies expanded by roughly 10% on average. Certain industries were hit harder: a 2018 McKinsey study showed that the broad retail sector saw debt increase by an average of 64%, food and grocery retail by 55%, and transportation by 40%. Walgreens' $34 billion, CVS's $27 billion, AT&T's $26 billion, Amazon's $23 billion — these figures were indeed eye-catching.

But the market remained largely calm. On one hand, sophisticated institutional investors and credit rating agencies (such as Moody's) had long been making capitalization adjustments for operating leases in their own analyses — they had always seen the elephant in the room. On the other hand, 2019 happened to coincide with a strong U.S. bull market and a low-interest-rate environment, which to some extent cushioned the psychological impact of rising leverage ratios. More importantly, many companies and lenders had amended the financial ratio covenants in their loan agreements before the new standard took effect, excluding its impact.

So the doomsday scenario that was predicted to "destroy 3.3 million jobs" ultimately resembled the global Y2K panic of late 1999 — the fear that computer systems would crash because they couldn't process dates in the year 2000. We made it through safely, in part precisely because we took the threat seriously.

## The China Perspective: CAS 21 and the A-Share Market

China's new lease standard, CAS 21, was revised and issued by the Ministry of Finance in December 2018, maintaining substantial convergence with IFRS 16. For companies listed both domestically and overseas, and for domestically listed companies, the new standard took effect on January 1, 2019, and January 1, 2021, respectively.

China's role in this global standard reform can be summarized as "rapid adoption with pragmatic adjustments" — Chinese standard-setters did not participate in the decade-long joint development process of IFRS 16 (which was primarily led by the IASB and FASB), but quickly introduced a highly converged domestic version after the international standard was finalized, while retaining a small number of necessary local adaptations. On the lease standard, the degree of convergence between CAS 21 and IFRS 16 was even higher than that between the revenue standard (CAS 14 and IFRS 15).

The most noteworthy local adaptation involves land use rights. In China, land is owned by the state, and enterprises acquire time-limited land use rights (typically 40 to 70 years) through grant, allocation, or transfer. In terms of economic substance, this does have the nature of a lease — an entity pays consideration for the right to use land for a defined period, which fits the definition of a lease quite well. IFRS 16 treats it exactly this way: under international standards, when Chinese companies list in Hong Kong, land use rights must be recognized as right-of-use assets. However, CAS 21 continued the historical tradition of Chinese accounting standards, stipulating that land use rights acquired through grant, allocation, or transfer continue to be accounted for under the *Intangible Assets* standard rather than being recognized as right-of-use assets. This was not because standard-setters failed to understand the lease nature, but rather a pragmatic choice: Chinese land use rights are typically acquired through a lump-sum payment of the land grant fee (rather than installment payments). In this case, whether classified as an intangible asset or a right-of-use asset, the accounting is the same — recorded at acquisition cost and amortized on a straight-line basis over the useful life, with identical income statement impact. If the grant fee is paid in installments, a financing cost (interest) difference would arise under the lease standard — but such situations are uncommon in A-share practice. Overall, the transition costs of mandating a reclassification would exceed the informational benefit.

The new standard's impact on the A-share market was most pronounced in the aviation and retail industries. The earlier example of Air China's debt-to-asset ratio jumping by 6.8 percentage points has already been mentioned. Here are some additional industry-level figures: operating-lease aircraft accounted for roughly 30% of the fleets of the three major airlines, with smaller carriers having even higher proportions. After the new standard brought hundreds of aircraft lease contracts onto the balance sheet in one go, the right-of-use assets of all three major airlines exceeded 100 billion yuan. China Southern's EBITDA increased by over 50% under the new standard — because operating lease expenses previously recorded in cost of sales were reclassified into depreciation and interest, both of which are added back in EBITDA calculations — but this was entirely an accounting reclassification effect and did not represent improved profitability.

The situation was similar in retail and hospitality. A chain enterprise with thousands of stores saw those thousands of store lease contracts individually capitalized after the new standard was implemented, with right-of-use assets and lease liabilities simultaneously going on the balance sheet, inflating both total assets and total liabilities. For companies that rely on large numbers of stores but have relatively low net assets, the jump in debt-to-asset ratios could affect bank credit assessments and debt financing terms during the transition period.

It is worth noting that the new standard also gave rise to new avoidance strategies during implementation. The standard exempts short-term leases (12 months or less) and low-value asset leases, and variable lease payments (floating rent tied to actual usage or sales rather than fixed amounts or indices) are excluded from the lease liability calculation and recognized as expenses when incurred. An academic study found that after ASC 842 was implemented, companies' variable lease expenses increased significantly while fixed minimum lease commitments decreased, suggesting a systematic shift from fixed to variable leases. The standard closed old loopholes, but new gray areas emerged along with them — a fate that probably no standard reform can escape.

## What Should Investors Watch For Under the New Standard?

The new standard has been in effect for several years, but its impact on financial analysis is ongoing. The following two points deserve investors' long-term attention.

**Understand the "technical shock" of standard changes on financial ratios to avoid misjudging a company's fundamentals.** The most direct effects of the new standard are: higher debt-to-asset ratios, lower interest coverage ratios (due to the addition of interest expense on lease liabilities), and lower total asset turnover ratios (due to asset inflation). Regarding EBITDA, a special note is needed: under IFRS 16, operating lease expenses previously recorded in cost of sales are split into depreciation of right-of-use assets and interest on lease liabilities, and EBITDA by definition adds back both depreciation and interest to profit — therefore EBITDA rises significantly, but this is entirely the result of accounting reclassification, not an improvement in the company's profitability. When making peer comparisons, investors need to ensure that the time windows and standard bases are consistent — if a company's debt-to-asset ratio was 60% in 2018 and became 70% in 2019, the jump is very likely primarily due to the standard transition rather than genuine leverage deterioration.

At the valuation level, lease standard reform directly affects the invested capital base in ROIC analysis. Under the new standard, both right-of-use assets and lease liabilities enter the balance sheet, invested capital increases accordingly, and ROIC gets pulled down. If you're comparing ROIC across companies, make sure all companies are on the same standard basis, or make your own consistency adjustments. Similarly, in DCF models using the FCFF approach, the definitions of EBIT and capital expenditure under the new standard need to be reexamined — right-of-use assets clearly must be depreciated under the standard (this is certain), and lease liabilities should be treated as analogous to interest-bearing debt in valuation (Damodaran's method does exactly this). This means that when calculating enterprise value (EV), lease liabilities should be added on top of equity value just like ordinary interest-bearing debt.

**Pay attention to lease disclosures in annual reports to uncover management's implicit signals.** While the new standard resolved the biggest transparency issue (bringing operating leases on-balance-sheet), management still retains considerable discretion. Discount rate selection is the most important area — the calculation of lease liabilities depends on the incremental borrowing rate, and there is no uniform standard for this rate. If two companies in the same industry choose discount rates of 4% and 6% respectively, their right-of-use assets and lease liabilities will differ significantly. When reading lease disclosures, pay attention to discount rate assumptions and cross-check them against the company's actual borrowing rates.

Additionally, watch the maturity distribution and renewal assumptions of lease contracts. When determining the lease term, companies must judge whether it is "reasonably certain" that renewal options will be exercised. Management can significantly alter the scale of recognized leases through different judgments about renewal options. If a retail company claims that none of its store leases are likely to be renewed, thereby substantially reducing the amounts recognized on the balance sheet, this may be a signal worth questioning.

## Conclusion

The journey of lease standard reform tells the story of a seemingly simple truth being put into practice through a tortuous process. Almost everyone saw the elephant in the room — investors saw it, analysts saw it, credit rating agencies saw it, and standard-setters certainly saw it too. But from the G4+1's first reform proposal in 1996 to the official effective dates of IFRS 16 and ASC 842 in 2019, more than twenty years separated "seeing the elephant" from "formally acknowledging its existence."

During those two decades, the strongest resistance did not come from theoretical disagreements — virtually no one could argue with a straight face that "hiding hundreds of billions of dollars of obligations in footnotes is good disclosure." The resistance came from the realities of vested interests: management didn't want to see leverage ratios suddenly jump, leasing companies worried that clients would shorten contract terms or switch to other financing methods, auditors didn't want to deal with more complex standards, and banks didn't want to renegotiate loan agreements. Each party's concerns had their own legitimacy, but together they created a powerful inertia to maintain the status quo.

The standard-setters ultimately completed the reform. Strategically, they allowed lengthy transition periods; in the details, they made compromises (preserving the operating/finance lease distinction, at least under U.S. GAAP); in timing, they chose an economic upswing — all of which reduced the political resistance to reform.

Warren Buffett once said that accounting is the language of business. But language is not neutral. When the old standards allowed $3 trillion in liabilities to stay off the balance sheet, they didn't just distort the language — they distorted every decision made on the basis of that language: investment decisions, credit decisions, and regulatory decisions. The new standards corrected that distortion.

For investors, perhaps the most fundamental takeaway is this: don't just look at what's on the balance sheet — pay attention to what still isn't there. The elephant of leases has finally been written into the balance sheet, but the off-balance-sheet world hasn't become empty. Contingent liabilities, external guarantees, unconsolidated structured entities, variable interest entities, long-term purchase commitments, and more still lie quietly between the lines of the footnotes. Before lease standard reform, smart investors were already making their own adjustments for operating leases. The reform made that work easier, but investors still need the ability to see through the financial statements to understand economic substance — the next elephant in the room might be hiding in the very financial report you're reading right now.
