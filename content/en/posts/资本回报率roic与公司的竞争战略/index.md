As discussed in a previous article, through DuPont analysis, Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) can be decomposed into two driving variables: operating profit margin and capital turnover. The formula is as follows:

*ROIC = NOPAT/Revenue x Revenue/Invested Capital*

To better understand the drivers behind a company's ROIC, let's explore the competitive landscape and the strategic choices companies make regarding competitive advantage:

## Competitive Landscape

A company's ROIC is first and foremost determined by its industry and competitive environment. For the vast majority of companies within an industry, ROIC tends to revert to the mean. How do we understand an industry's structural characteristics and competitive forces? Michael Porter's Five Forces model provides an excellent analytical framework. First introduced in the early 1980s, it remains one of the most widely adopted tools in the field of strategic management.

![197236830.jpg](197236830.jpg)

The Five Forces model focuses on five major forces that shape industry structure and competitiveness:

- **Supplier Power:** When suppliers have strong bargaining power, they can demand higher prices, putting pressure on company margins.
- **Buyer Power:** When buyers have strong bargaining power, they can demand lower prices and higher quality, squeezing profit margins.
- **Threat of Substitutes:** When substitutes exist, buyers may more easily switch to alternative products or services, posing a threat to incumbents.
- **Threat of New Entrants:** When barriers to entry are low, increased competition can threaten the market share and profitability of existing players.
- **Intensity of Competitive Rivalry:** When industry competition is intense, companies may face greater pricing pressure and other competitive challenges.

## Competitive Advantage

To achieve ROIC above mediocre levels within an industry, a company needs a certain competitive advantage. Porter's competitive strategy framework tells us that there are two primary sources of competitive advantage: differentiation and cost leadership. Differentiation typically manifests as higher operating profit margins, while cost leadership tends to show up as higher asset turnover. A classic example is the retail industry: luxury goods are a low-turnover, high-margin business, while supermarkets earn their money through thin margins and high volume.

### Differentiation

How can a company command a price premium through product or service differentiation? If we observe everyday life closely, several business models stand out:

- **Innovation.** Consumers are clearly willing to pay a premium for uniquely designed, functionally innovative products. New product launches typically carry higher price tags. In the consumer space, markets are becoming increasingly long-tail -- niche and trending products like phone accessories and new beverage offerings may not have massive sales volumes, but they survive because consumers are willing to pay a premium.
- **High Quality.** Survival of the fittest is the law of the market. High quality earns consumer trust and commands higher product premiums. Take China's NEV industry: competition is fierce and price wars are raging, but what ultimately determines the winners is product quality -- low prices alone cannot sustain long-term competitiveness.
- **Branding.** A brand is itself a premium. Brands attract organic traffic, whether they are product brands or the personal IPs that are popular in today's era. Compared to industrial goods markets, consumer-facing industries tend to win on brand strength -- which is why the consumer sector generally delivers higher ROIC.
- **Customer Loyalty.** Once customers become loyal to a product, it creates a word-of-mouth effect that attracts more customers. At the same time, loyalty implies high switching costs, which helps with long-term customer retention. Xiaomi's early smartphones and NIO's recent phone launch both cultivated loyal user bases through fan culture. The membership and loyalty point programs commonly used in retail are also fundamentally designed to enhance customer loyalty.

These factors are rarely isolated -- they are interconnected and mutually reinforcing. A brand may represent high-quality or innovative products and services, and high quality or innovation in turn strengthens customer loyalty. Apple is the quintessential example, combining all of the above. Buffett once made a remark that encapsulates his rationale for heavily investing in Apple stock with elegant simplicity:

> "If you're an iPhone user and someone offers you $10,000 to never use an iPhone again, you'd turn it down. But if someone offered you $10,000 to switch from a Ford, you'd take the money and go buy a Chevrolet."
>

### Cost Leadership

Sustaining cost leadership within an industry is often difficult. Competition always brings lower prices, and price wars drive down industry-wide profitability. Take the NEV industry mentioned earlier: Tesla's price cuts triggered an industry-wide wave of price reductions. Or consider online retail, where Pinduoduo pulled off a stunning comeback through low pricing, forcing JD.com and Taobao to compete on price to attract traffic and increase purchase frequency.

Over the long term, cost leadership tends to be a differentiating characteristic between industries rather than within them. As noted earlier, the retail industry is inherently a thin-margin, high-volume business.

If cost leadership is typically achieved through high capital turnover, we can examine it through two lenses, depending on the type of Invested Capital (IC). Invested capital can be divided into short-term working capital and long-term fixed asset investment. The retail industry exemplifies high turnover of short-term working capital -- specifically inventory and accounts receivable.

How do we understand high turnover of long-term capital? Industries that are long-term capital intensive are commonly referred to as asset-heavy industries, and the challenge with asset-heavy businesses is that achieving high turnover is inherently difficult. In fact, asset-heavy industries typically deliver lower returns on capital. So how do we evaluate cost leadership within an asset-heavy industry? The answer is economies of scale.

Economies of scale are closely related to operating leverage. Operating leverage reflects the principle that, due to the presence of fixed costs, a company's EBIT fluctuates proportionally more than its revenue. This is essentially an amplification effect, similar to how financial leverage amplifies return on equity through the use of debt.

The nature of asset-heavy industries means their operating leverage amplification is significant. As production scale increases, unit costs decline rapidly, leading to swift margin improvement. Therefore, companies in these industries (such as NEV manufacturers) are justified in sacrificing gross margin to drive revenue growth.

However, economies of scale do not mean that bigger is always better. In many cases, scale economies are confined to a specific region. Consider the countless local service businesses -- their service radius is extremely limited, and the cost of geographic expansion is high.

A prerequisite for achieving economies of scale is that the product itself must be mass-producible. Take Tesla's Cybertruck, for example: its unconventional design and extreme innovation have resulted in extremely slow production ramp-up, with the target of producing 250,000 units per year not expected to be reached until 2025. For capital-intensive products like automobiles, the inability to mass-produce means the inability to turn a profit.

It's important to recognize that economies of scale are themselves a form of competitive advantage. If an industry requires massive capital investment, the number of players who can enter is inherently limited, which effectively protects the industry. The automotive and semiconductor industries, for instance, are destined to be games played by a select few with deep pockets.

## A Special Competitive Advantage: Network Effects

Network effects represent a special form of competitive advantage, characterized by near-zero marginal costs that enable rapid expansion. As the user base grows rapidly, the cost of monetizing each individual user drops sharply, the value of the product to each user increases continuously, and return on investment (ROI) improves dramatically. The internet industry's explosive growth in recent years is a prime example -- rapid scaling enables exceptionally high returns on capital. A company like Douyin (TikTok) rose to become a trillion-dollar enterprise in just five to six years, powered by enormous network effects.
