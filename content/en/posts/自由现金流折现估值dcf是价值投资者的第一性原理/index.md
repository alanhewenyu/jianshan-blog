Stock investing is the best way for ordinary people to acquire company equity and share in the growth of corporate revenue and profits.

The barrier to entry for stock investing is low, but the risks are enormous. Without going too far back, just from the beginning of this year to now, A-shares have experienced dramatic roller-coaster swings.

Buffett once said that if you are not prepared for a 50% decline in stock prices, you should not enter the stock market. Looking at the past few years, stocks that have lost half their value are everywhere.

For non-professional investors, Buffett's recommendation is to buy a low-cost S&P 500 index fund. The S&P 500 is a broad-based index that comprises some of America's finest listed companies. This passive approach to investing requires no stock selection, and its returns typically match or exceed those of actively managed funds.

In fact, most actively managed equity funds struggle to outperform broad-based indices, and fund managers with consistently strong long-term track records are virtually nonexistent. Is it luck or skill that creates star fund managers? This is something that deserves careful scrutiny. Buffett himself once conducted a famous experiment on this very question.

## Buffett's Ten-Year Bet

On December 19, 2007, Buffett wagered $500,000 and issued a challenge to all of Wall Street: he bet that over the ten-year period from January 1, 2008 to December 31, 2017, the returns of an S&P 500 index fund would exceed the net-of-fees returns of any hedge fund portfolio.

After Buffett issued the challenge, out of thousands of professional investment managers, only Ted Seides stepped forward to accept. He selected five funds of funds that invested in over 100 hedge funds. In the end, the S&P 500 index fund achieved an actual compound annual growth rate of 7.1%, while Ted's active strategy returned only 2.2% annually.

Despite the advantages of index fund investing, many investors are not content with passive investing. They seek to hedge risks through stock and asset portfolios, or even aim to enhance returns by allocating to individual stocks.

In the current A-share market, while index levels have already risen and short-term upside may be limited, opportunities in individual stocks remain plentiful.

## Types of Stock Investors

Making money from stocks is not easy, which is why the market is flooded with all sorts of methods claiming to teach people how to profit from the stock market. Generally speaking, stock investing can be divided into technical analysis, value investing, and speculation. Technical analysis focuses on market momentum, using various stock indicators to identify buying and selling opportunities. Value investors focus more on company fundamentals, identifying quality stocks through fundamental analysis and holding them for the long term. Speculators chase market hot topics and rumors — they are the noise in market trading. Technical analysis and value investing are not entirely opposed; many people use a combination of methods when selecting stocks, balancing short-term and long-term positions.

As ordinary investors, we lack information advantages and institutional-grade research capabilities. Trying to capture market momentum is probably too difficult for us. Therefore, long-term investing and value investing are better choices.

But is value investing an easier path? Can you just buy and hold? After enduring the stock market declines of recent years, value investors have found that even quality stocks are not immune, and holding for the long term can be excruciating. Of course, the holding period may not yet be long enough — Buffett's definition of long-term starts at ten years, but how many of us can truly commit to that?

Many people equate value investing with long-termism. While long-termism certainly creates value, holding for the long term does not guarantee profits. The timing and price of your purchase matter enormously — in other words, you need to buy cheaply enough. This is the concept of margin of safety, as taught by Buffett's mentor Benjamin Graham.

In his early years, Buffett's investment strategy was to buy fair companies at wonderful prices. Later, influenced by Munger, he gradually shifted to buying wonderful companies at fair prices. The shift in investment targets from "fair" to "wonderful" reflects the core conviction of value investing: only excellent companies can create value over the long run. And the shift in price from "wonderful" to "fair" means that while you are no longer hunting for bargains, the price still needs to be reasonable.

Price is not the same as value — price is what we pay, value is what we get. No matter how great a company is, if you overpay, there is no investment value.

## How to Determine Whether a Stock Is Expensive or Cheap?

Determining whether a stock is expensive or cheap is fundamentally a question of valuation. In the market, investors typically prefer relative valuation methods such as the P/E ratio and P/B ratio, often combined with their historical percentile rankings. For ordinary investors, the advantage of this approach is its simplicity — the learning curve is minimal, and it facilitates easy comparison across markets and companies.

However, if we only scratch the surface and judge whether a stock is cheap or expensive based solely on the P/E ratio, the analysis becomes too vague and subjective. A 15x P/E may be cheap for one company but expensive for another. Many factors determine whether a P/E ratio is high or low. For instance, if a company carries massive net debt, a low P/E stock may not actually be cheap. Conversely, if a company is asset-light, holds substantial net cash on its balance sheet, and has tremendous free cash flow growth prospects, a high P/E stock may not actually be expensive. Yet many analysts forget that the P/E ratio is merely an imperfect proxy for a discounted cash flow model and should not be used in isolation.

For value investing, there is only one true measure of value: a company's intrinsic value, which is the discounted value of its free cash flow to the firm (FCFF) — that is, DCF valuation. Therefore, buying cheaply means purchasing at a price below a company's intrinsic value.

## Stock Prices Reflect Market Expectations

Why do stock prices sometimes fall after a listed company reports strong earnings? Conversely, why does a company's stock price keep rising despite poor results? In reality, "good" and "bad" are relative concepts — they are measured against expectations. Stock price movements reflect the gap between a company's actual performance and investor expectations. A company may report strong results that still fall short of the expectations implied by its current stock price; a company may report weak results that are nonetheless better than what the current stock price implied.

So whose expectations do stock prices reflect? We often see consensus forecasts from institutions like Wind and Bloomberg, which aggregate the views of mainstream investment firms. In this sense, market expectations represent the collective view of most institutions. Taken as a whole, this is essentially the opinion of Mr. Market.

Value investors are familiar with the concept of "Mr. Market." In *The Intelligent Investor*, Benjamin Graham personified the stock market and introduced this concept. Mr. Market offers a price quote every day, and judging by price movements, he is extremely emotional. If you are heavily influenced by his moods, you will inevitably make poor decisions. For value investors, the key is to learn to ignore Mr. Market's emotions and to take advantage of them by transacting when prices are cheap.

Is Mr. Market truly that irrational? Such episodes do occur from time to time — the A-share market in February this year being a prime example. But most of the time, Mr. Market's mood swings actually reflect the inherent uncertainty in a company's future performance and the fluctuating expectations of investors.

## DCF Valuation Helps Us Understand Market Expectations and Decide Whether to Accept Them

While a DCF valuation model produces a specific number, the number itself — given the forecasts and assumptions involved — should be treated only as an approximate indicator of a company's intrinsic value. "Approximate" does not mean inaccurate or unreliable. As noted in previous articles, it is better to be roughly right than precisely wrong.

In practice, the significance of DCF valuation extends well beyond the calculated result. It helps us understand the uncertainty behind a company's intrinsic value and, through the model, helps us decipher the expectations of other investors that are embedded in the stock price. As ordinary investors, we are negotiating with Mr. Market. In these negotiations, what matters more than producing a forecast or a valuation is understanding the expectations implied by Mr. Market's quoted price, so that we can decide whether to accept or reject his offer.

As discussed repeatedly in previous articles, two core variables drive DCF valuation: revenue growth and operating profit margin. In earlier valuation examples, I conducted sensitivity analysis based on these two metrics. Sensitivity analysis is also scenario analysis — we can trace along the sensitivity table to find the revenue growth rate or operating margin that supports the current stock price. The gap between these implied figures and our own expectations may mean that we do not yet understand the company well enough and need deeper analysis, or it may signal that the market price has significantly overestimated or underestimated the stock. In either case, DCF valuation provides us with a tool to understand market expectations and decide whether to accept them. Most importantly, DCF valuation is the best safeguard against overpaying for a stock.
