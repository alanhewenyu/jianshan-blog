According to Wind data, during the first half of this year, 1,645 A-share listed companies repurchased a combined total of approximately RMB 99.3 billion in stock, nearly triple the amount over the same period last year. Since July, the enthusiasm for buybacks among listed companies has continued to surge. As of July 16, 157 listed companies had announced new buyback plans.

Turning to Hong Kong stocks, many listed internet companies have been conducting substantial and sustained buybacks of their own shares. For instance, Tencent has been repurchasing up to HKD 1 billion per day, with a commitment to buy back more than HKD 100 billion in 2024. Alibaba repurchased USD 5.8 billion worth of shares in Q2 this year, bringing the first-half total to USD 10.6 billion, with an additional USD 26.1 billion in buyback capacity remaining. Meituan has also been repurchasing between HKD 300 million and HKD 500 million per day.

## The Positive Signals Conveyed by Share Buybacks

As ordinary investors, we lack the informational advantages of major shareholders and find it difficult to obtain real-time insights into a company's actual operating conditions. As a result, the various signals the stock market sends every day become crucial inputs for investment decisions, influencing market sentiment. Among these, a company's financial performance and corporate decisions serve as important windows into its prospects and management's outlook.

Generally speaking, a share buyback signals to the market that a company's stock is undervalued. If a company is actively repurchasing its shares, it suggests that major shareholders and management are confident in the company's future, which helps bolster investor confidence.

So does simply following a company's buyback and purchasing its shares guarantee profits? If only it were that simple. In practice, it is common for a company to be actively buying back shares while its stock price remains lackluster or even continues to decline. Consider the stock price performance of Tencent, Alibaba, and Meituan mentioned above: previous articles have analyzed the valuations of Tencent and Meituan, and their current stock prices are not expensive compared to conservatively calculated DCF valuations. Yet the ongoing buybacks have not driven further price appreciation. Alibaba's stock price has been even more sluggish, with virtually no gains year-to-date.

If management understands the company better than ordinary investors, why do share buybacks so often fail to inspire investor confidence and drive stock prices higher? This question has long puzzled me. Put differently, can share buybacks actually create value for shareholders?

## Can Share Buybacks Create Value for Shareholders?

By repurchasing its own shares, a company reduces the number of outstanding shares without affecting its earnings. Since the same profits are distributed across fewer shares, this increases the company's earnings per share (EPS). In theory, the stock price should rise accordingly to reflect the improved EPS. For shareholders who continue to hold their shares, the value of their holdings increases.

However, a contrarian view holds that share buybacks do not create value by increasing EPS. After all, the company spent cash to repurchase those shares, and investors will adjust their valuations to reflect the reduction in cash, thereby offsetting any EPS impact.

### Buffett on Share Buybacks

Regarding the relationship between share buybacks and company value, Buffett provided a simple and clear answer in his 2016 letter to shareholders:

> From the standpoint of exiting shareholders, repurchases are always a plus. Though the day-to-day impact of these purchases is usually minuscule, it's always better for a seller to have an additional buyer in the market.
>

> For continuing shareholders, however, repurchases only make sense if the shares are bought at a price below intrinsic value. When that rule is followed, the remaining shares experience an immediate gain in intrinsic value. Consider a simple analogy: If there are three equal partners in a business worth $3,000, and one is bought out by the partnership for $900, each of the remaining partners realizes an immediate gain of $50. If, however, the exiting partner is paid $1,100, the continuing partners each suffer a loss of $50. The same math applies to corporations and their shareholders. Ergo, repurchase actions that enhance value for continuing shareholders and those that diminish it are entirely dependent on the purchase price.
>

In other words, share buybacks can only create value for continuing shareholders when the company's stock price is below its intrinsic value. As for what constitutes intrinsic value, previous articles on valuation have discussed this extensively. Simply put, it is the present value of the free cash flows the company is expected to generate in the future.

So does a low stock price automatically make buybacks the best option for enhancing shareholder value? Buffett identified two situations where a company should not repurchase shares even if the stock is undervalued:

> One is when a business needs all available funds to protect or expand its own operations and is also uncomfortable adding further debt. In this case, the internal need for funds takes priority. This exception, of course, assumes the business has a decent future awaiting it after the necessary expenditures are made.
>

> A second, less common exception involves a business acquisition (or other investment opportunity) that offers far more value than the undervalued stock of the potential repurchaser.
>

In summary, share buybacks are essentially an investment decision for the company, one that requires consideration of whether superior investment opportunities or alternatives exist. If better investment opportunities are available, the positive signal from a buyback may actually backfire.

This is particularly true for companies in high-growth industries, where buybacks may be interpreted as a sign that growth is decelerating and the company lacks other meaningful opportunities to deploy its capital. Alternatively, investors may conclude that management is overly focused on the short-term stock price at the expense of long-term growth opportunities.

## How to Make Decisions Based on Buyback Signals

Based on the discussion above, share buybacks clearly can create value for shareholders. However, when a company announces a buyback, investors should adopt a systematic mindset and consider the issue from multiple angles:

First, DCF valuation should always serve as the first-principles approach for determining whether a stock is cheap. The cheapest investment as determined by DCF analysis is the one investors should buy, regardless of whether the business is growing or stagnating, whether its earnings are volatile or stable, or whether its price relative to current earnings and book value (i.e., P/E and P/B) appears high or low.

A share buyback does not necessarily mean the current stock price is below intrinsic value. This is especially true for some small-cap companies, where management's motivation to boost the stock price may stem from margin call risks on pledged shares held by major shareholders, or from facilitating share sales by major shareholders or management. When a company is buying back shares while major shareholders or management are simultaneously selling, it severely undermines market confidence.

That said, the vast majority of corporate buybacks are genuinely motivated by undervalued stock prices. This is particularly true in the current A-share and Hong Kong markets, where there is no shortage of cheap stocks. In such cases, buybacks help enhance company value and shareholder returns.

Second, when we have reason to believe the buyback is being conducted below intrinsic value, we should also watch for whether the buyback signals that the company's future growth may be decelerating and that it may struggle to maintain or improve its current return on assets through increased investment. In this scenario, from the company's perspective, the buyback essentially functions as a form of dividend distribution due to the lack of better investment opportunities, which is favorable for shareholders. However, from the shareholder's perspective, if the company's long-term growth rate slows, the valuation may decline.

Finally, investors should continuously monitor other signals the company is sending to see whether they are consistent with the buyback signal. For example, does the company's subsequent financial performance corroborate management's stated confidence in its prospects? And what are major shareholders and management doing with their own holdings: are they adding to their positions or taking the opportunity to sell?

If a buyback sends a convincing signal that the stock is undervalued, it represents a buying opportunity. Be patient: share buybacks will ultimately create value for long-term shareholders. Here is how Buffett evaluated the buybacks at Apple, one of his largest holdings:

As early as 2018 he stated, "When I bought Apple, I knew Apple was going to repurchase a lot of shares." In 2021, Buffett said, "The return on capital from buybacks may be slow, but over time, that return can become powerful."
