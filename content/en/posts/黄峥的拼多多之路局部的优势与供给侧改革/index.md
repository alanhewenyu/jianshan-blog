Pinduoduo's market capitalization recently surpassed that of Alibaba, sparking widespread public discussion — even Jack Ma came out to congratulate Pinduoduo. I personally rarely use Pinduoduo for shopping and had been stuck on the impression that the platform was rife with counterfeit and low-quality goods. After doing some research online, I realized that Pinduoduo operates on a fundamentally different business model from Taobao. The reason Pinduoduo can offer low prices while still achieving strong sales is that its core competitive advantage lies in supply chain integration and product selection and operations capabilities. Its overseas e-commerce platform, Temu, takes this model to the extreme.

I then discovered that Colin Huang actually had a WeChat public account. There were not many articles, published in 2016 and 2017 — right around the time Pinduoduo was just getting started. Through these publicly available writings, one can discern the cognitive framework behind Huang's creation of Pinduoduo.

In April 2015, Colin Huang founded Pinhaohuo (a fresh fruit platform), and in September he launched Pinduoduo, expanding into general merchandise. At that time, JD.com and Taobao had already dominated the e-commerce market and occupied consumers' mindshare. The prevailing view was that there was no room for new entrants in e-commerce, and very few believed Pinduoduo could succeed. So how did Colin Huang think about it?

## Local Advantage

"Local advantage" — this is the life lesson he summarized in a biographical essay titled *My Middle School and University Years*. Huang has said that the elementary school he attended was quite ordinary. Thanks to winning a math olympiad prize, he had the incredible luck of being admitted to Hangzhou Foreign Language School, which set him on a path of upward mobility and opened the door to elite circles. Reflecting on this formative experience, he likened it to the ancient Chinese parable of Tian Ji's horse race — achieving a local advantage despite an overall disadvantage in resources, and thereby winning the entire battle.

Pinduoduo's ability to carve out a path between JD.com and Taobao is precisely an embodiment of this "local advantage" philosophy. Clearly, Huang's strategy for building Pinduoduo was rooted in wisdom distilled from his own life experience.

## Supply-Side Reform

"Supply-side reform" — this was the subject of his thinking in another essay. Inspired by how World War I drove the rapid, large-scale production of clothing, he reflected on several models of supply-side reform: first, creating entirely new demand, such as the invention of the airplane; second, the emergence of new use cases, such as wartime needs; and third, aggregation-driven changes in frontend demand, such as ride-hailing. Huang's deep thinking about the third model of supply-side reform is the very operating logic behind Pinduoduo today: driving planned aggregation on the demand side to enable large-scale planned production on the supply side.

## Bad Money Drives Out Good

In yet another essay, Colin Huang discussed the phenomenon of "bad money driving out good" (Gresham's Law), and he clearly disapproved of such destructive dynamics. He also offered suggestions on how to mobilize grassroots power and channels to maintain healthy social and commercial ecosystems. Interestingly, as Pinduoduo's user base has grown, many commentators have characterized the trend as "consumption downgrade" — which is itself a form of bad money driving out good.

In reality, Pinduoduo has been continuously evolving, and low prices do not necessarily mean inferior quality. What matters is value for money. The so-called "consumption downgrade" is actually "consumption substitution" — finding comparable alternatives at lower prices — and this trend is likely driven more by the current economic environment than by any fundamental shift in consumer preferences.
