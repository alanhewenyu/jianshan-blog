## Equity Investment Returns and Intrinsic Value

The return on a stock investment can be understood simply as the change in stock price over the holding period, plus any dividends paid by the company. For example, if a stock appreciated 8% over the past year and the company paid a 2% dividend yield, the annual return on the stock would be 10%.

Stock prices fluctuate daily, sometimes dramatically. On the surface, stock prices appear irrational, seemingly bearing little relationship to a company's fundamentals or intrinsic value. In reality, however, intrinsic value acts as the gravitational force on stock prices, with prices always oscillating around value.

As Buffett wrote in his 2005 letter to shareholders:

> "In most cases, the investor's returns from now until Judgment Day will equal the aggregate earnings of the companies they own. Of course, by buying and selling wisely, Investor A can gain more than Investor B. But in the aggregate, what A gains is exactly what B loses, and the total returns remain the same. When the market rises, all investors feel wealthier, but for one shareholder to exit, a new one must step in to replace them. If one investor sells at a high price, another must buy at a high price. For all shareholders as a whole, without some miraculous shower of money from the heavens, they cannot possibly extract more wealth from a company than what that company earns."
>

A company's intrinsic value is the discounted present value of its future cash flows. Therefore, the stock price reflects expectations about the future, not current financial performance. Take the P/E ratio, for instance: the numerator (price) reflects cash flow expectations over many future years, while the denominator (earnings) is merely a snapshot of the past or present. When the P/E multiple expands, it may indicate an upward revision in the market's expectations for the company's future earnings or cash flows.

Different investors hold different views about a company's future, and their estimates of intrinsic value may diverge considerably. But that is not what matters. What matters is understanding the core financial metrics behind stock prices, the ones that drive equity investment returns and intrinsic value. Without a clear understanding of where investment returns come from, it is difficult to know which key financial metrics are influencing stock prices.

## The Drivers of Equity Investment Returns

### Stock Price Movements

Saying that stock price movements affect investment returns may sound like a tautology. But if we decompose the sources of stock price movement further, we find that:
Stock Price (P) = Earnings Per Share (EPS) x P/E Ratio. Consequently, changes in stock price can be attributed to two factors: earnings (expected changes in EPS) and valuation (expected changes in the P/E ratio).

Stock price fluctuations over a given period result from the combined effect of changes in EPS and the P/E multiple. When the valuation center shifts upward, meaning the P/E ratio expands, stock price appreciation tends to exceed EPS growth. When the valuation center shifts downward, meaning the P/E ratio contracts, stock price appreciation tends to lag behind EPS growth.

The market continuously adjusts its expectations for a company's future performance, and P/E movements often reflect these adjustments. Of course, in many cases, it may simply be human fear and greed driving the market. Therefore, distinguishing between changes in fundamental expectations and mere shifts in investor sentiment is essential for effective market timing.

### Dividend Yield

Dividend yield can be expressed as: Payout Ratio x (Net Income / Stock Price) = Payout Ratio x the inverse of the P/E Ratio. Distributable dividends represent the portion of after-tax profits remaining after the investments needed to maintain or grow the business.

Excluding external financing, a company's organic earnings growth rate has a natural ceiling, which is its Return on Invested Capital (ROIC). Companies typically need to retain a portion of their earnings for capital expenditures, i.e., the reinvestment rate. Therefore, Operating Earnings Growth Rate = ROIC x Reinvestment Rate = ROIC x (1 - Payout Ratio).

### Revenue Growth and Operating Margins Are the Core Drivers

Further attribution analysis reveals that operating profit growth (or improvement in EPS) depends on revenue growth and improvements in operating margins. ROIC can be further decomposed through DuPont analysis into operating margin and asset turnover. Clearly, revenue growth and operating margin are the core metrics driving equity investment returns. Since long-term stock returns are aligned with intrinsic value, these are also the core factors driving company valuations.

It is important to note that high readings on core metrics do not necessarily translate into high investment returns. What truly matters is whether the future trajectory of these core drivers turns out better or worse than current market expectations.

Strong financial performance at a company and the returns investors earn from holding the stock are two different things. The reason is that stock prices reflect the market's expectations for future financial performance, and excess stock returns result from further upward revisions to those expectations. If a high-growth company fails to consistently exceed expectations over time, the stock will struggle to deliver attractive returns. The market, after all, always lives in the realm of expectations.

## How to Analyze a Company's Intrinsic Value

Excess stock returns come from upward revisions to expectations. Changes or revisions in expectations involve forecasting a company's future cash flows and analyzing its strategic advantages.

Long-term cash flow forecasting forms the foundation for estimating intrinsic value, with revenue growth and operating margins serving as the core variables in those forecasts.

Given the inherent uncertainty in cash flow projections, analyzing a company's competitive moat is equally important. One should carefully evaluate the company's market position relative to its peers, considering factors such as market growth and stability, competitive dynamics, and sustainable competitive advantages. Strategic advantage analysis, in turn, serves as a cross-check on the reasonableness of the core variables in the cash flow forecast, ensuring in particular that projections are neither blindly optimistic nor unjustifiably inflated.
